- **Answer:** CHOKE KNOBS
- **Puzzle type:** Crossword with transposed-letter errors
- **Mechanic:** Five entries in the crossword grid contain transposed letter pairs. Solvers must identify these errors and correct them:
  - 17-Across: MARHC KING → MARCH KING
  - 23-Across: BRKOEN DOWN → BROKEN DOWN
  - 33-Across: THE WEKEND → THE WEEKND
  - 48-Across: PHOONGRAPH → PHONOGRAPH

  "Un-transposing these letters into their correct positions yields the phrase CHOKE KNOBS."

## Music Puzzle 4: Key Changes
- **Author:** Jonah Ostroff
- **Solves:** 102
- **Answer:** MAC TAYLOR
- **Puzzle type:** Encoding-chain / invariant-letter extraction
- **Mechanic:** The puzzle involves 15 encoding systems arranged in a loop: Maritime flags, Gold Bug, Pigpen, Ternary, Binary, NATO, Tap Code, Phone spelling, ASCII, Semaphore, A1Z26, Braille, Roman numerals, Elements, and Morse code — each encoding decodes to the next. "Each instruction can be interpreted to send a letter in one encoding to the next encoding in loop order." The key insight is identifying which single letter maps to itself under each transformation. "For each instruction, there is exactly one letter which is mapped to the same letter in the new encoding." The self-mapping letters spell "GARY SINISE ON CSI," which leads to the final answer.
- **Meta role:** Answer rhymes with "quack" (duck) in Music Meta.

## Music Puzzle 6: Counterpoint
- **Author:** Amos Eshel
- **Solves:** 30
- **Answer:** URBAN HEAT
- **Puzzle type:** Multi-round antonym extraction / tournament bracket
- **Mechanic:** Three iterative rounds of antonym pairing:
  - Round 1: Identify antonym pairs from a word list where each word has an extra letter added. Alternate the parenthetical letters (starting with the alphabetically first word) to form new words.
  - Round 2: Find antonym pairs that are "once removed" (e.g., DARK and HEAVY both oppose LIGHT). Alternate remaining letters to create another word list.
  - Round 3: Locate antonym pairs "twice removed" — their antonyms are themselves antonyms of each other (e.g., WRONG and STAYED, whose antonyms RIGHT and LEFT oppose each other).
  Final stage: a pseudo-tournament bracket with 8 level-1 words, 4 level-2 words, and 2 level-3 words. The 14 extra letters from this bracket form an almost-consecutive alphabetic sequence with one missing letter. That missing letter sequence spells "RURAL/COLD," whose antonyms yield the answer. (18 Level 1 pairs, 18 Level 2 pairs, and 9 Level 3 pairs provided.)
- **Meta role:** Answer rhymes with "tweet" (bird) in Music Meta.

## English Puzzle 1: Themes and Counterthemes
- **Author:** Justin Melvin
- **Solves:** 160
- **Answer:** PARIS, SHELF
- **Puzzle type:** Alphabetical word-list extraction (dual-answer)
- **Mechanic:** Each of five columns contains alphabetically-ordered words. Solvers extract one letter from each column to form items in a specific category. The removed letters spell the primary answer (theme). The leftover words are each one letter short of a category member. A second unique letter can be inserted into each incomplete word to form non-thematic words, spelling the secondary answer (countertheme).

  - Theme Letters: P, A, R, I, S = PARIS
  - Countertheme Letters: S, H, E, L, F = SHELF
- **Author's note:** References a missed opportunity to respond with "Marked as resolved" when teams submit second answers.

## English Puzzle 2: Alternative Orthographonology
- **Author:** Mike Sylvia
- **Solves:** 111
- **Answer:** DEN, TOOTH
- **Puzzle type:** Phonetic letter-pairing cipher (dual-answer)
- **Mechanic:** Each of twenty-five answers phonetically contains the spoken name of an English letter. Answers can be paired such that replacing one answer's spoken letter with another produces the other answer, with one exception. The pairs form a bidirectional cipher key that translates a given string to reveal the final answers. The puzzle contains 24 paired answers covering most of the alphabet. By process of elimination, the unpaired answer DECKS (containing X) must pair with N, implying one answer is DEN. Using the cipher key derived from the letter pairs, the ciphertext decrypts to "A WORD OF LENGTH FIVE AFTER EM OR BEFORE CUE ZEE." Translating EM and CUE ZEE through the cipher yields EYE and DECAY, revealing TOOTH as the second answer.
- **Author's note:** The word "FISHY" appearing in the ciphertext was coincidental but retained as an intentional red herring.

## English Puzzle 4: How the Other Half Lives
- **Authors:** Justin Melvin, Jonah Ostroff
- **Solves:** 104
- **Answer:** SCHEMA, STOOL
- **Puzzle type:** Duck Konundrum variant (dual-player, dual-culture)
- **Mechanic:** Mimics a Duck Konundrum format where both players receive identical instructions, but one player is American and one is British. Cultural and linguistic differences — vocabulary (sweater vs. jumper), date formats (MM/DD vs. DD/MM), measurement systems, spelling (aluminum vs. aluminium), and trivia answers — cause them to interpret instructions differently and navigate distinct paths. Players progress through 56 steps involving: eating challenges, driving around a pond, hand/foot contortions, Monopoly board movement based on bingo numbers, grid navigation with trivia answers, floor climbing with number system differences, and board position swaps. Points accumulate based on claimed positions, which are indexed at the end to produce the final answers. Authors note they "tried to cram as many types of cultural differences" into the puzzle as possible.

## English Puzzle 6: Articles of Incorporation
- **Author:** Philip Z Loh
- **Solves:** 73
- **Answer:** HYPOTHESIS, RAM
- **Puzzle type:** Article-insertion compound-word puzzle (dual-answer)
- **Mechanic:** The puzzle presents nonsensical questions containing the word "which" as a divider. Solvers split each question into left and right halves, then discover the right halves belong to subsequent rows. By inserting alternating articles ("a" and "the") in place of "which," solvers create compound words. After reordering by the left half alphabetically, they extract letters at specified indices to form "SCIENTIFIC GUESS," which becomes HYPOTHESIS when prefixed with "the" and RAM using remaining letters with "a." Uses two letter banks provided in the puzzle. Requires recognition that clues are sorted alphabetically by left half, necessitating reordering. Final answers incorporate articles thematically.
- **Author's notes:** The original concept aimed for "HYPO THE SIS" as separate chunks but was modified. An earlier draft required loop formation instead of treating the first clue separately.

## English Meta: English Meta

- **Solves:** 33
- **Answer:** LET EM HALVE IT
- **Puzzle type:** Irregular-plural cycle meta
- **Mechanic:** Six puzzles each contain two answers. One word in each pair has an irregular plural form. The other word can be transformed using an irregular plural from a different puzzle to create an unrelated word. For example, "SHELF becomes SHELVES, and GIF can be similarly 'pluralized' to GIVES." ## History Puzzle 1: Learning from Our Old Mistakes
- **Authors:** Jonah Ostroff, Justin Melvin
- **Solves:** 49
- **Answer:** MISSISSIPPI ADMITTED TO UNION
- **Puzzle type:** Personalized errata puzzle
- **Mechanic:** Teams encounter a list of errata for other puzzles in the hunt, formatted as corrections to clues. These fictional clues always feature word pairs differing by a single letter. Reading the "corrected" letters yields: "SEE WRONG ANSWERS; FIND SHIFT FOR EACH LETTER; THEN SHIFT TITLE OF THIS." The errata correspond to teams' actual wrong answers submitted during the hunt. The number of corrections per puzzle matches the length of each wrong answer. Each letter A-Z receives a specific shift value (ranging from 1 to 24) determined by comparing wrong answers to clue letter changes. The 26-letter puzzle title is then shifted accordingly using these values, yielding the final answer.
  - Clues provided: 478 crossword-style clues covering words like ADDER, BALLOON, CANYON, and ZIPPER
  - Puzzle content varies per team based on their unique wrong answer history
  - Authors encountered server errors during testing due to rare bigram combinations in Q words

## History Puzzle 3: The Telescope
- **Author:** Kenny Young
- **Solves:** 22
- **Answer:** SEATTLE COUNTY NAMESAKE DIES
- **Puzzle type:** Hybrid logic (Galaxies + Masyu + Kropki Sudoku)
- **Mechanic:** Three integrated puzzle types that must be solved simultaneously:
  - Galaxies: identifying 2×2 or larger symmetric regions with dark/light cells around galactic centers
  - Masyu: a path-following puzzle with white (straight through) and black (turn) circle clues
  - Kropki Sudoku: standard 9×9 Sudoku with consecutive number and 2:1 ratio constraints between adjacent cells
  Solvers must determine which dots represent which puzzle type, then solve all three simultaneously using logical deduction and constraint propagation. "A good place to start is in the lower right, where the open circle in the corner cannot be a Masyu clue and therefore must be a 1×1 galaxy." Leverages that "there is only 1 3×3 magic square with digits 1-9 (modulo rotation/reflection)" to heavily constrain the Sudoku portion.

## History Puzzle 6: The Hundred Years' War
- **Authors:** Jonah Ostroff, Mike Sylvia
- **Solves:** 32
- **Answer:** JOHNSON ASSUMES PRESIDENCY
- **Puzzle type:** Card game simulation (War) with historical battle setup

## History Meta: History Meta
- **Authors:** Amos Eshel, Jason Deakins
- **Solves:** 32
- **Answer:** A CLERICAL ERA
- **Puzzle type:** Flexible calendar extraction
- **Mechanic:** The puzzle presents historical events that each correspond to two distinct dates referencing the same occurrence. Solvers extract letters from a flexible calendar grid (accommodating any starting day and up to 31 days) using these dates. The calendar cells are color-coded by day of week. By identifying the two dates associated with each answer and locating them on the calendar, solvers extract specific letters. Ordering these letter pairs chronologically (by year of the historical events) reveals the final answer. Uses eleven historical events spanning 1817-2007, including state admissions, presidential successions, space missions, and sports championships. Some events have multiple possible dates (e.g., Mississippi readmission), though both options yield identical letters.
