## Museum Rules
**Source:** MIT Mystery Hunt 2023
**URL:** https://puzzles.mit.edu/2023/interestingthings.museum/puzzles/museum-rules
**Answer:** TOTALLYUNACCEPTABLE
**Type:** Location-based rule-violation deduction + state authority extraction. A tourist commits various activities, each of which is prohibited in some specific location (U.S. state or jurisdiction). Solvers identify where each activity is forbidden, then extract a letter from the relevant state's "highest power" (state supreme court or governor) to spell the answer. The puzzle is presented as an interactive flippable book.

### Puzzle Text

**Flavor text / framing:** The puzzle is presented as a tourist's visit to various locations. Everything the tourist is doing is not allowed somewhere. Solvers must track where the tourist was during each activity and identify which rules they broke.

**Core mechanic:** For each activity the tourist performs, identify the location where that activity is prohibited. Then identify the "highest power" you would appeal to if caught breaking the law in that state — there is one in each U.S. state.

**Presentation:** An interactive page-flipping book interface ("Drag corners or use arrow keys to flip pages"), featuring an Atrium image as the cover. Puzzle content is delivered as illustrated pages.

**Canned hints:**
1. Everything the tourist is doing isn't allowed somewhere.
2. Where was the tourist during each activity?
3. What "highest power" would you appeal to if you were caught breaking the law? There is one in each state.

## Much Ado About Nothing
**Source:** MIT Mystery Hunt 2023
**URL:** https://puzzles.mit.edu/2023/interestingthings.museum/puzzles/much-ado-about-nothing
**Answer:** RANDOMDECISION
**Type:** Statistical analysis puzzle. Solvers compute Pearson correlation coefficients between pairs of variables (X_i, Y_j) across datasets derived from scenes of Shakespeare's *Much Ado About Nothing*, then filter for statistically significant correlations (p < 0.05) to extract letters via A1Z26 cipher, spelling the final answer.

### Puzzle Text

**Framing:** The puzzle title references Shakespeare's play *Much Ado About Nothing*, and the datasets are derived from lines of the play's scenes.

**Procedure (from flavor text / hints):**
1. For each triple (X_i, Y_j, scene k): run X_i and Y_j on each line of the k-th scene of *Much Ado About Nothing*.
2. Compute the Pearson correlation coefficient (rho) for the resulting data.
3. Convert to a t-score, then to a p-value.
4. Identify the exactly seven correlations that fall below the p < 0.05 significance threshold.
5. Sort the seven significant correlations by p-value (ascending).
6. Read out the letters given by A1Z26 encoding of the X and Y variable indices to extract the answer.

**Canned hints:**
1. Use the procedure outlined in the flavortext (Pearson rho → t-score → p-value), try to find statistically significant correlations on triples (X_i, Y_j, text k), when one runs X_i and Y_j on each line of the k-th scene.
2. For each pair of variables and each dataset: compute the Pearson correlation coefficient associated to the data, the t-score, and the p-value. There should be exactly seven correlations which are below the p < 0.05 threshold.
3. Sort by p-value and read out the letters given from A1Z26 by the X and Y variables.

## Brain Freeze
**Source:** MIT Mystery Hunt 2023
**URL:** https://puzzles.mit.edu/2023/interestingthings.museum/puzzles/brain-freeze
**Answer:** GEOMETRICSNOW
**Type:** Anagram + song identification + acrostic extraction. Solvers unscramble a word list to find that every word is a name for a mathematical number type (triangular, square, lucky, happy, etc.). Each mathematical number type corresponds to a song by a specific artist. The songs' next lyrics, kept in the same order, yield first-letter acrostic extraction.

### Puzzle Text

**Round:** Science (Act 1)

**Puzzle content** (reconstructed from hints — the puzzle itself is image-based):

The puzzle presents a word list where the words appear to be scrambled or out of order. Meaningless-seeming letters appear at the top in alphabetical order. Solvers must:

1. Identify that the scrambled words are all names for types of mathematical numbers (triangular numbers, square numbers, lucky numbers, happy numbers, etc.).
2. Recognize that each mathematical number type corresponds to a song by a particular artist (artists share a common theme).
3. Each song's length corresponds to the number of blanks given.
4. Bolded words in the puzzle relate to two things that could fill the blanks — find the "next lyrics" for each song.
5. Restore the words to their original (non-alphabetical) order.
6. Read the first letters of specific words ("ground," "ever," "reflected," "coats," "sky" or similar) to extract the final answer.

**Canned hints:**
1. A word list where some words may seem out of place — look at what all the words have in common after unscrambling them.
2. The meaningless letters at the top appear in alphabetical order; deciphering their original order may help.
3. After unscrambling, all words are names of something mathematical.
4. These are all names of types of mathematical number sequences.
5. Words are derived from songs by specific artists who all share a common theme; each song's length matches the blank counts.
6. Bolded words relate to the blanks — two things could fill each blank.
7. Find the "next lyrics" for each song within the puzzle's context.
8. Keep the same order and read the first letters of the relevant words (e.g., ground, ever, reflected, coats, sky).

## World's Smallest Logic Puzzles
**Source:** MIT Mystery Hunt 2023
**URL:** https://puzzles.mit.edu/2023/interestingthings.museum/puzzles/worlds-smallest-logic-puzzles
**Answer:** CHAOSDOMAIN
**Type:** Miniature Nikoli-style logic puzzle identification + indexing + grid overlay. Eight sets of tiny grids each contain multiple miniature logic puzzles; solvers identify which puzzle type each grid belongs to, solve them, index numbers from solutions into puzzle-type names, then overlay one blank grid per set onto a letter grid, using neighbor-cell positions to extract the final answer.

### Puzzle Text

**Framing:** A collection of the world's smallest Nikoli-style logic puzzles, organized into 8 sets.

**Instructions:** Match the puzzle names listed on the side to each grid. Fill them in. Connectors between grids denote "equality" or "inequality" of certain cells, providing a unique constraint that makes each set solvable. Each set contains exactly one completely blank grid.

**Puzzle types included** (approximately 40–46 total across 8 sets):

Set 1: Clouds, Hamle, Linesweeper, Masyu, Tatamibari

Set 2: Castle Wall, Dosun-Fuwari, Dotchi-Loop, Douieru, Fobidoshi, Tren

Set 3: Canal View, Kurotto, Simple Loop, Suraromu, Tents

Set 4: Doppelblock, LITS, Nonogram, Rectslider, Santoitchi

Set 5: Calcudoku, Hidato, Snake Pit, Sudoku, Sukoro

Set 6: Norinori, Rukkuea, Shikaku, Tetroid, Tilepaint

Set 7: Arofuro, Gaidoaro, Sashikabe, Star Battle, Yokibunkatsu

Set 8: Kakurasu, Kurodoko, Nanbaboru, Ring-ring, Shakashaka, Sto-stone

**Extraction procedure:**
1. Solve all miniature logic puzzles in each set.
2. Index numbers from puzzle solutions into the names of the logic puzzle types; read left-to-right.
3. Each set yields exactly one completely blank grid.
4. Overlay the blank grids into the "blanks" in a master letter grid.
5. Look at the positions of the 1–2 "neighbor" grids — highlight cells 2 spaces away where the neighbors lie.
6. Read the highlighted letters to get the final answer.

**Canned hints:**
1. Each set of grids is a collection of tiny Nikoli-style logic puzzles. Match the puzzle names on the side to each grid and fill them in. Using the connectors to denote "equality" or "inequality" of cells, there is a unique way to fill each set of grids.
2. Try indexing the numbers into the names of the logic puzzles and read them left-to-right.
3. Each set of grids should have exactly one completely blank grid. What happens if you overlay these in the "blanks" in the grid of letters, and look at the positions of the 1 or 2 "neighbor" grids? Try highlighting the cells 2 spaces away where the neighbors lie.

**Answer:** NORMALBUNDLE
**Type:** Cryptic wordplay via fake film reviews + "morbing" anagram operation. Each of 16 fake *Morbius* reviews (8 critic, 8 audience) contains a hidden clue resolving to a word. Solvers identify matching critic/audience word pairs that share many letters, count the "morb" instances, use that count as an index into the anagrammed ("morb'd") word, and extract the final two-word answer.
**Enumeration:** (6 6)

### Puzzle Text

**Round:** Science

**Framing:** A spoof review aggregator for the 2022 Marvel film *Morbius*, playing on the "It's morbin' time" internet meme. The puzzle title riffs on the meme phrase "Morbius is one of the movies of all time."

**Critic Reviews** (8 reviews):
1. Contains a reference to Alan Turing inventing the Turing machine.
2. References a blue puppet YouTuber.
3. References a surrealist artist.
4. References Jiangdu (the largest district by area in Zhenjiang, China).
5. References a debate about whether zero is even or odd (parity of zero).
6. References the planetary scale of "morbing" — as if an entire planet is doing it.
7. References the helium-neon laser.
8. References the least-populated province of the Netherlands.

**Audience Reviews** (8 reviews):
1. References executive committee membership.
2. References conventions about superhero films.
3. References a reviewer's favorite month.
4. References a Bay Area bridge.
5. References cigarette purchase economics.
6. References a trophy award.
7. References a former New York City mayor.
8. References competitive rankings ("morbiest plot").

**Mechanism:**
- Each review contains a clue that solves to one word.
- Match critic review words to audience review words that have many letters in common.
- Count the total number of "morb" instances (from the meme) and use this as an index into the "morb'd" (anagrammed) word.
- Morb the result one more time to get the final two-word answer.

**Canned hints:**

3. Add the number of "morb"s together, and use this number as an index into the morb'd word.
4. Try morbing the word once more! Your answer should be a two-word phrase.

## H2No
**Source:** MIT Mystery Hunt 2023
**URL:** https://puzzles.mit.edu/2023/interestingthings.museum/puzzles/h2no
**Answer:** ENCAMPMENT
**Type:** Date-arithmetic location identification + differential extraction. Six clues each describe a historical event at a location using an offset from a different historical event at the same location (e.g., "X years after Y happened here"). Solvers identify all six locations, determine what they have in common, then extract letters based on the "differences" (as flagged by the flavor text) to spell the answer.

### Puzzle Text

**Flavor text:** "These locations have something in common, but watch out for any differences."

**The Six Clues** (with enumerations):

1. 124 years after a glassware company was founded by Neumann (5 11)

2. 633 years after a sung-about wooden bridge began to be replaced with a stone one (5 4 7)

3. 184 years after being the state capital for a day (8 6 5)

4. 54 years before a rebellion protesting Britain's rule started on a holiday (8 6's storehouse)

5. 30 years after a man began a project to dig his own subway system (7 warehouse)

6. 20 years before a landmark roller coaster named after a weather event was opened (8 3 7 factory)

**Extraction procedure:**
1. For each clue, identify the anchor historical event and work out the target year.
2. Identify the location where both events (anchor and target) occurred.
3. Determine what all six locations have in common (the shared property named in the flavor text).
4. Watch for "differences" — variations between locations in how they express the common property — and extract letters from those differences to spell ENCAMPMENT.

**Canned hints:**
1. Try to look for other notable events that have happened in the same place. It may be easier to find things about the smaller cities.
2. Look at the flavor text, particularly the part about "watching out for differences."
