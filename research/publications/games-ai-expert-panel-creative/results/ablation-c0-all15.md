## Scoring Dimensions (1–5 each, total /30, pass ≥ 22)

- **Clarity** — solver understands what to do
- **Solvability** — appropriate knowledge reaches correct answer
- **Elegance** — mechanism feels clean, not arbitrary
- **Reading Reward (RR)** — domain/puzzle knowledge is load-bearing
- **Fun** — enjoyable, creates aha moment
- **Confirmation** — solver knows when they're right

## C0 Observations

**Tier separation is real but partial.** The MIT/Elite tier averages 22.75, Standard averages 23.2 (slightly higher, pulled up by Information Relay and Front and Center's near-perfect scores), and Games Magazine averages 19.4. Without a rubric, the expert reviewer detects the quality gap but the boundary between tiers is not crisp — two Standard Hunt puzzles (H2No, Dropypasta) score below the MIT/Elite minimum, and one Games Magazine puzzle (Logic Grid) passes.

**Elegance and Reading Reward are the primary differentiators.** Games Magazine puzzles score maximum Clarity (5.0) and near-maximum Solvability (4.8) and Confirmation (4.6), but flatline on Elegance (2.0) and Reading Reward (1.0). These two dimensions account for the entire tier gap. The absence of a rubric does not prevent the reviewer from detecting them — they are visible from design alone.

**Confirmation is the inverse dimension.** Games Magazine crosswords score highest on Confirmation (crossing letters guarantee a correct fill); hunt puzzles score lower because multi-step extraction can compound errors invisibly. This creates an interesting inversion: the "easier" puzzles are actually harder to get wrong, while the sophisticated puzzles carry more confirmability risk.

**Best in corpus:** Information Relay (26/30) and Front and Center (26/30) — both Standard Hunt puzzles. Both achieve complete thematic unification (mechanism, answer, and confirmation participate in the same idea). Front and Center's self-confirming palindromic answer is the most elegant single design decision in the corpus.

**Lowest in corpus:** GM-05 Word Search (17/30) — the only puzzle with a construction defect (unverified extraction), and the only one where the answer is stated rather than discovered, eliminating the confirmation satisfaction that word searches normally provide.

**Solvability penalty pattern:** MIT/Elite puzzles take a recurring hit on Solvability (avg 3.5) due to double-specialist requirements (Scicabulary's portmanteau knowledge, People Watching's dual ornithology requirement, Bridge Building's Hashi + organic chemistry). The Standard Hunt puzzles largely avoid this by using accessible cultural domains (palindromes, ASCII, Billy Joel, hex arithmetic) rather than requiring two simultaneous specialist domains.
