# Ablation Study — Condition C6 (Full Profiles, Current System)
**Evaluator condition:** C6 — all three reviewers operating with complete profile identities (full bio, design philosophy, review lens, credentials).
**Evaluation date:** 2026-02-28
**Puzzles evaluated:** 15 (Batches 1–3 + Games Magazine GM-01–GM-05)
**Scoring rubric:** Clarity (1–5), Solvability (1–5), Elegance (1–5), Reading Reward (1–5), Fun (1–5), Confirmation (1–5) = /30. Pass threshold: ≥ 22/30.
**Reviewers:** Dan Katz (DK), Thomas Snyder (TS), Dana Young (DY)

---

## Overview: Reviewer Voice Commitments

**Dan Katz** reads structurally. He checks whether the mechanism contract is honest, whether the extraction chain is robust, whether the puzzle justifies its slot, and whether a solver who stalls has been abandoned or served. He names failure modes. He has no patience for puzzles that are technically impressive but experientially empty.

**Thomas Snyder** reads as a constructor. He traces the solve path from forced entry point to forced conclusion. He strips each element to test whether it is load-bearing. He asks whether the puzzle teaches the skill it claims to require. He cannot ignore noise masquerading as difficulty, and he will not credit a puzzle that could have been generated without intentional craft.

**Dana Young** reads visually and experientially. She asks whether the mechanic is the theme or merely represents it. She checks whether the extraction earns its step. She wants the answer to be inevitable — the word that names what you were doing the whole time. She goes straight to the parts the designer finished last, because that is where puzzles fail.

## Puzzle 1: Scicabulary — Answer: SHAMROCK
**Source:** MIT Mystery Hunt 2023
**Type:** Portmanteau deconstruction + two-word phrase matching + letter indexing

### Reviewer Panel Notes

**Dan Katz:** The mechanism contract here is genuinely novel and honestly presented. You are told exactly what to do — "each jargon term is a combination of two opposite halves of a real portmanteau" — and the operational demand is real. The 24-term scale is calibrated correctly; enough entries to prevent lucky guessing, few enough that a team working in parallel can distribute the load. My concern is the steepness of the entry-point curve. The gap between understanding the instruction and executing the first successful match is substantial. A solver who does not immediately grasp what "opposite halves" means will stall at the threshold. The canned hints address this, but that means the puzzle is partially relying on its hint structure to compensate for a presentation that could be clearer. Once you are in, though — once you've cracked two or three pairings — the mechanism is deeply satisfying. The extraction step is clean and proportionate. The answer SHAMROCK does not feel inevitable from the mechanism, which is a minor penalty; it is arbitrary rather than thematically resonant. But the mechanism itself earns the score.

**Thomas Snyder:** The construction elegance here is high. The decision to use "opposite halves" of portmanteaus as the building blocks is a genuine design invention, not an assembly of existing parts. Each jargon term is load-bearing: remove any term and you lose a letter from the answer. The definitions are inventive — the two-word phrase clues are doing real work, and the funnier ones (the Nutella-loving women, the alligator superhero) help distinguish entries that might otherwise blur together. What I notice as a constructor is that the forced solve path is less tightly constrained than I would prefer: several jargon-to-phrase mappings could plausibly be attempted in multiple orders, and wrong early pairings could cascade. A puzzle that can be run forward from any entry point is strong; this one rewards working from the easier pairings first rather than having a clean forced start. The index step is clean. My only other note is that the difficulty is noise-adjacent in a few cases — some jargon terms are harder to parse not because the deduction is subtle but because the word construction is particularly unusual.

**Dana Young:** This puzzle delights me at the concept level and frustrates me slightly at the execution level. The idea — fake jargon built from fragments of real portmanteaus, reassembled backwards — is genuinely original and deserves to work. The two-word phrase clues are the puzzle's best element: they're funny, they're specific, and the best ones (the bear and the Nutella society) have a warmth that makes the solve feel like play rather than work. Where I feel the theme thinning is the extraction: indexing into the two-word phrase is a clean but emotionally neutral step. After the richness of the portmanteau deconstruction, the extraction feels like administration. The answer SHAMROCK doesn't name what you were doing. A puzzle about inventing fake scientific portmanteaus could deliver an answer that resonates with that experience — and it doesn't quite, here. Still, this is real puzzle design. The concept is finished.

## Puzzle 2: People Watching — Answer: HINDWING
**Source:** MIT Mystery Hunt 2023
**Type:** Bird identification via anthropomorphized vignettes + 4-letter IBP alpha code extraction

### Reviewer Panel Notes

**Dan Katz:** This is a structurally sound puzzle with one significant clarity problem and one elegant structural insight. The clarity problem: the path from "each tweet describes a person" to "the person is a bird in human form" requires a leap that not every solver will make at the same moment. The mechanism is not explained; it must be discovered. That is a legitimate puzzle design choice for Mystery Hunt, but it means the Clarity score must reflect the discovery cost. Once you're in — once you realize that "screams 'The pot! The pot! The pot!'" is a bird call rendered as human speech — the puzzle is wonderful. The IBP alpha code extraction is clever and fair: a specific, verifiable external reference that anyone can look up. The answer HINDWING feels like it arrived from the code system rather than from the theme, which is a mild disconnect. But this is a well-constructed puzzle. The mechanism is honest, the extraction is defensible, and the solve experience rewards ornithological curiosity.

**Thomas Snyder:** From a construction standpoint this puzzle demonstrates genuine craft in the vignette writing. Each clue is doing three simultaneous jobs — it must read as a coherent social scene, it must encode the bird's vocalization as human speech, and it must include a visual field mark for confirmation. That triple-duty writing is technically demanding, and most of the 14 entries execute it cleanly. The confirm-ability is strong: if you've identified the bird correctly, the visual details (the white eyebrow, the yellow hat, the orange waistcoat) should corroborate without being mechanically necessary. What I find less satisfying is the lack of a forced solve path. You can attack the 14 entries in any order, which means the experience is more search than deduction. A puzzle that requires you to solve all entries to get the answer is robust only if every entry is independently solvable — and a few of these are genuinely hard without the cross-referencing that comes from having multiple entries solved.

**Dana Young:** The visual grammar of this puzzle is its best feature: every vignette is written with the specificity of a birdwatcher's description, but rendered in human social context. I love that the field marks show up as clothing choices and the calls show up as dialogue — that is the mechanic being the theme, not decorating it. You are doing what a birdwatcher does; you are just watching a different kind of flock. My concern is in the extraction. The IBP 4-letter alpha code system is real and verifiable, but it requires knowing that such a system exists or being willing to find it. That is a reading-reward dimension where specialist knowledge is a gate rather than a bonus. For a general solver, the jump from "I've identified the birds" to "IBP alpha codes" requires an explicit hint. The answer HINDWING is anatomically specific (the hindwing of a butterfly) — I don't feel it reframes the birdwatching experience in a satisfying way.

## Puzzle 3: H2No — Answer: ENCAMPMENT
**Source:** MIT Mystery Hunt 2023
**Type:** Date-arithmetic location identification + differential extraction

### Reviewer Panel Notes

**Dan Katz:** I find this puzzle's structure admirably disciplined. Six clues, six locations, one shared property, with extraction via the "differences" that the flavor text forewarns. The flavor text is doing real work here — "watch out for any differences" is a fair and honest signal rather than misdirection — and the mechanism is proportionate. My main concern is the research burden. Each clue requires identifying two historical events at the same location — the anchor event and the date-calculated target event. For at least two of the six (the smaller cities), this is a genuine research challenge that could stall teams without local knowledge or database access. The puzzle is structurally fair but operationally demanding in a way that is more about search engine fluency than puzzle-solving skill. The answer ENCAMPMENT I find satisfying: it names a kind of temporary location (camp), and the puzzle is about identifying what six locations have in common — there is a thematic logic in the answer, though it is not strongly inevitable.

**Thomas Snyder:** The entry point here requires identifying the anchor event — and these anchor events vary substantially in obscurity. The glassware company founded by Neumann or the subway-digging man are not common knowledge, and without knowing those anchors, you cannot calculate the target year, and without the target year, you cannot identify the location. This is difficulty through research load, not difficulty through subtle deduction, which I find less satisfying. The mechanism, once understood, is elegantly simple: calculate, locate, extract. The differential extraction step is the puzzle's most interesting structural choice — using the differences between instances of a shared property to extract letters is genuinely clever. I would have preferred fewer entries with more constructible solving paths and less reliance on historical database lookup.

**Dana Young:** The concept is clean and the flavor text is honest. I like that the puzzle tells you to look for differences — that transparency is an act of respect for the solver. What I notice is that the visual presentation of this puzzle (six clues, numerical offsets, multi-word answer enumerations) is doing almost no work to guide you through the intended experience. The layout is a list; the solve is a research task; the extraction is a logical step. There is no visual moment where the puzzle resolves. The answer ENCAMPMENT — a temporary settlement — is modestly resonant with the idea of historical events at a location, but it does not feel earned in the way that an inevitable answer does. This is a well-designed research puzzle, but it is more of a procedure than an experience.

## Puzzle 4: Bridge Building — Answer: PROTEINPOWDER
**Source:** MIT Mystery Hunt 2023
**Type:** Hashiwokakero logic grid → molecular polymer backbone identification

### Reviewer Panel Notes

**Dan Katz:** This is an ambitious cross-domain puzzle — logic genre plus chemistry — and the ambition is mostly earned. The Hashi puzzle is self-contained and solvable on its own terms; if you know the genre, you can solve it without the chemical layer. The chemical layer then adds a second, independent aha: the solved grid is a molecular diagram. This two-stage structure is one of my favorite puzzle architectures because each stage rewards different solvers. A chemistry-blind team can get the Hashi; a chemistry team might backsolver from the polymer structure. The concern I have is that the second aha — reading a solved Hashi grid as a polymer backbone — requires a very specific piece of chemical knowledge (amino acid polymer structures, with the + and − charge superscripts as disambiguators). A team without a biochemist may solve the logic puzzle completely and then stall in the extraction. The mechanism is brilliant when it works; it fails gracefully for teams with chemistry knowledge, which is exactly what a Mystery Hunt puzzle should do.

**Thomas Snyder:** From a logic puzzle construction standpoint, the Hashi grid is doing real work. The constraint that nodes have maximum 4 connections (which maps to carbon's valence) is not just clever — it shapes the grid in ways that make the chemical reading possible. This is theme and structure being the same thing, which is what I value most. The (+) and (−) superscripts are load-bearing: they are not decorative charges but functional disambiguators for the polymer identification. Every element in this puzzle is earning its place. My question is about uniqueness of solve path — in Hashi puzzles, there can be multiple valid solutions, and the puzzle's constraints must be tight enough to force the specific molecular reading. If the Hashi has any ambiguity that the chemical reading has to resolve, the puzzle's architecture inverts: the extraction is doing work that the logic grid should have done.

**Dana Young:** This is a puzzle where the mechanic is the theme in the most literal possible way: a bridge-building puzzle whose solved form is literally the structure of a polymer chain. That recursion is beautiful. Building bridges IS building protein powder, in some deep structural sense. I am fully here for this. What I find slightly uncomfortable is the presentation requirement: the (+) and (−) charges are doing critical work, but they must be precisely placed and readable to function. In a print context, superscript legibility is a physical constraint. The answer PROTEINPOWDER is compound-word flat, which is the convention, and it names the product of what you built — there is something satisfying about that, even if "powder" is doing commercial work that "protein chain" or "polypeptide" would do more precisely.

## Puzzle 6: Dropypasta — Answer: VINDICATIONISLAND
**Source:** MIT Mystery Hunt 2023
**Type:** Dropquote (missing letters from Super Smash Bros. quotes) + stage-specific character-win extraction

### Reviewer Panel Notes

**Dan Katz:** The architecture of this puzzle is sophisticated in a way I appreciate: stage win conditions as the extraction mechanism is genuinely novel. Each named stage has a different rule for determining which character "wins," and those rules are varied enough (alphabetical by letter, release date, tier ranking, weight, proximity on character select screen, damage resistance) that no single strategy dominates. This is mechanical variety embedded directly in the extraction layer, which I find elegant. The concern is depth of domain knowledge required: Super Smash Bros. tier lists, inhale damage values, and character release dates are genuinely specialized knowledge. A team without a Smash expert may have correctly filled the dropquotes and still stall at the extraction. The win condition for Fountain of Dreams (inhale damage from Kirby) is particularly opaque — it requires knowing a specific gameplay mechanic, not just fandom familiarity. That said, this is a Mystery Hunt puzzle, and specialist knowledge as a tier gate is genre-appropriate.

**Thomas Snyder:** The dropquote mechanic is the most mechanical part of this puzzle — fill in missing letters from a grid, a technique that requires no insight. The interest is entirely in the extraction stage, which is where the genuine design lives. Each stage win condition represents a different logical relationship between two named characters, and the variety of those relationships is the puzzle's intellectual substance. From a construction standpoint, my question is: is each dropquote entry uniquely forced? In a standard dropquote, the missing letters are constrained by the grid structure. Here, each row has exactly one missing letter — which means the extraction depends entirely on correctly identifying the quote and its source, not on the dropquote mechanics themselves. The "one missing letter per row" variant is almost degenerate as a dropquote; it functions more as a quote-identification task.

**Dana Young:** I am fascinated by this puzzle's world. Super Smash Bros. stages as the organizing principle for an extraction system is thematically rich — stages in that game are places with rules and physics, just as each win condition here is a place with its own logic. The puzzle inhabits that world convincingly. What I find less satisfying is the visual structure: a dropquote is a visual puzzle type, and presenting it as text clues without the grid's visual representation loses the distinctive reading experience of the format. In its fullest form, this puzzle probably has beautiful visual presentation. The answer VINDICATIONISLAND is long and compound — it names a place (island) and a concept (vindication), which has a faint thematic resonance with the stage-as-place structure, but it reads more as two words forced together than as something inevitable.

## Puzzle 7: Information Relay — Answer: FINISH LINE
**Source:** Huntinality III, 2023
**Type:** Telephone game / five bears with different clue failure modes + self-referential extraction

### Reviewer Panel Notes

**Dan Katz:** This is one of the most structurally inventive puzzles in this batch. The "Exquisite Froot" framing is doing real architectural work: each bear's failure mode is different, which means each bear's clue set is solving a different kind of puzzle, which means the final instruction ("apply Grandma's method to Grandpa's clues") is using a structural insight about the puzzle itself as its extraction mechanism. That is meta-architecture of a high order. The puzzle is not just a set of clues — it is a taxonomy of clue failure modes, and understanding that taxonomy is the solution. From a structural standpoint, I want to verify that the six target words are uniquely forced by the union of all bears' clue sets — if you need all three clues for each word to pin it, the puzzle is perfectly constrained; if two suffice, some bears are decorative. Either way, the mechanism earns my admiration.

**Thomas Snyder:** The Just One Cell principle applies here in an interesting way: each bear is testing one specific skill. Papa Bear tests your tolerance for technically-correct uselessness; Mama Bear tests vocabulary collocation; Child Bear tests video game knowledge; Grandma Bear tests homophone deduction; Grandpa Bear turns out to be the extraction key. Identifying which bear contributed which clue pair is itself a deduction — you have to understand each bear's failure mode to assign the clues correctly. This is the puzzle teaching its own rules through the solve path, which is what I value most. The solve path is: identify target words → assign bears → extract via Grandpa's pairs → apply Grandma's method → FINISH LINE. That path is elegant and clean. The entry point (recognizing that "dark, time" is cluing NIGHT, not KNIGHT, because Grandma mishears) is genuinely brilliant.

**Dana Young:** The world of this puzzle is fully realized. The five bears each have distinct voices, and those voices are mechanically load-bearing — the personality is the clue method. That is the mechanic being the theme in the purest sense I've seen in this batch. You spend the entire solve inhabiting a family dynamic where everyone is contributing to a shared task in their own broken way, and the answer FINISH LINE arrives because you've understood all of them well enough to combine their failures correctly. The answer itself is perfect: you finished the relay; you crossed the finish line. That is an inevitable answer in Dana's sense — it names what you were doing. This is the best puzzle in Batch 3.

**Type:** Palindrome headline reconstruction → center-letter extraction → palindromic answer

### Reviewer Panel Notes

**Dan Katz:** The structural elegance of this puzzle is exceptional. The mechanism has three layers of self-reference: the newspaper is called SEMI TIMES (a palindrome), the headlines are palindromes, the extracted answer is a palindrome. The self-reference is not decorative — it is the argument. The puzzle is making a claim about what palindromes are and then demonstrating it at three scales. From a construction standpoint, the five headlines are beautifully chosen: A SANTA DEVIL NEVER EVEN LIVED AT NASA and CIGAR TOSS IN EDEN IS SO TRAGIC are genuinely witty as palindromes, and the subtitles that clue them do real disambiguating work without giving away the palindrome structure. The subtitle for Clipping 2 — "rodents feature in analytics of 2022 Mystery Hunt prologue" — is self-aware in a way that Mystery Hunt audiences will love. The word-length enumerations are the constraint system that forces unique reconstruction, and that is exactly the right way to design a palindrome puzzle.

**Thomas Snyder:** From a construction standpoint, I want to know whether the word-count constraints uniquely force each palindrome reconstruction, or whether multiple palindromes fit the given word lengths. If the subtitles are necessary for uniqueness — if the word lengths alone admit multiple palindromes — then the subtitles are load-bearing as constraints. If the word lengths plus the knowledge that it must be a palindrome are sufficient, the subtitles are confirmation. Either way is valid design, but it changes what the puzzle is testing. What I find genuinely impressive is that five palindrome headlines were constructed that are each independently funny and that each yield a different letter at their center. That is extremely demanding constrained writing. The palindromes feel natural, not forced, which is the hardest thing to achieve.

**Dana Young:** This puzzle resolves visually at every scale: each palindrome headline reads the same forward and backward, the extracted word RADAR reads the same forward and backward, and the masthead crease that bisects the T in SEMI TIMES is a visual hint at the center-letter extraction. The visual grammar is consistent throughout. The newspaper format is not decorative — it is the world of the puzzle, and palindromes are naturally at home in a world where text reads both ways. The answer RADAR is perfect: it names a technology for detecting things that are both ahead and behind you, which resonates with the temporal symmetry of palindromes. This is Dana's definition of an inevitable answer: it reframes everything before it.

That double resonance is exactly the kind of answer I design toward. This puzzle knows what it is about.

## Puzzle 10: What's Next? — Answer: AFGHANISTAN
**Source:** Huntinality III, 2023
**Type:** "We Didn't Start the Fire" sequence recognition + next-item indexing + lyric continuation

### Reviewer Panel Notes

**Dan Katz:** The architecture is clean and the aha structure is double-layered in a satisfying way. First aha: these images are from "We Didn't Start the Fire." Second aha: the puzzle asks for what comes NEXT in the song, not what you see in the image. Third aha: the extracted letters spell "RUSSIANS IN," which is itself a lyric, and the answer is what comes next in the song after that phrase. That three-stage structure is appropriately complex for a hunt puzzle without being exhausting. ## Evaluator Notes (Post-Panel)

**On the GM-05 failure:** The word search puzzle is the only puzzle in this evaluation where the stated answer cannot be produced from the given grid. The construction was left unfinished — the source document itself flags this with a parenthetical. All three reviewers penalized this heavily. Snyder's score of 1 for Solvability, Elegance, and Confirmation reflects the position that an unfinished puzzle is not a puzzle. The 13/30 score is the lowest in this evaluation and lower than the theoretical floor for a puzzle of this type (a correctly constructed word-search-with-hidden-message would score approximately 18–20, given the mechanism's inherent low Elegance and Reading Reward ceiling).

**On the H2No and Dropypasta failures:** Both puzzles fail at the margin (20 and 21 respectively). H2No's failure is research-load-as-noise; the mechanism is correct but the entry difficulty depends on historical database access rather than puzzle-solving skill. Dropypasta's failure is Confirmation and Clarity; the answer VINDICATIONISLAND is long and compound in a way that reads as two arbitrary words joined, and the extraction step (stage-specific win conditions) requires specialist Smash Bros. knowledge that is less fairly accessible than the domain knowledge in, e.g., Bridge Building or Characters.

**On the GM-04 pass:** The logic grid is the only Games Magazine puzzle that passes. This is structurally significant: it demonstrates that passing threshold at ≥22 requires a real forced solve path, and that correct constraint satisfaction construction — even at the simplest possible scale — produces a passing puzzle. Elegance and Reading Reward are still low (3 and 2) because the mechanism is trivially simple, but Clarity, Solvability, and Confirmation are maximal, and Fun is 3 (mild genuine engagement from deduction). This confirms that the pass threshold is not a proxy for complexity — it is a proxy for whether the puzzle is finished and whether the mechanism is honest.
