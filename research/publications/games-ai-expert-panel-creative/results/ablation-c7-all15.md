### Puzzle 1: Scicabulary
**Answer:** SHAMROCK
**Reviewer:** Dan Katz (structural complexity, 24-clue matching mechanism)
**First encounter**

#### Profile Lens Checks

**"Does every puzzle justify its slot?"** FIRES STRONGLY. This puzzle performs a double portmanteau inversion — each fake jargon term is built from the *opposite* halves of two real portmanteaus. It is not a lookup puzzle. The solver must identify real portmanteaus (motel → motor + hotel; brunch → breakfast + lunch), determine their component halves, swap opposite halves between two portmanteaus to construct the fake term, match that construction to a two-word definition, and then index. This is a legitimately complex multi-step identification problem that earns its place in a Mystery Hunt.

**"Is the narrative encountered while solving or reported afterward?"** Fires mildly negative. The flavor text "Each jargon term is a combination of two opposite halves of a real portmanteau" is instructional rather than narrative. There is no story — the puzzle is self-consciously mechanical. This is not a defect at this tier; MIT puzzles in the Science section are permitted to be mechanism-forward.

**"Are the mechanisms varied enough?"** Does not fire at the single-puzzle level. The internal mechanism has genuine variety: each definition-matching step is locally independent, and the portmanteau pairs vary in difficulty (MOTEL vs. more obscure targets).

**"Is the thematic coherence structural, not decorative?"** FIRES — positive. The fake scientific vocabulary is the puzzle. The solver inhabits a false science that operates by the puzzle's logic. Theme and mechanism are integrated.

**"Would he want to solve this?"** FIRES — with enthusiasm. This is the kind of puzzle that wins awards: a clean central aha (portmanteau inversion) with high solve satisfaction on each matching pair. Katz's criterion "does it justify the effort?" answers yes. The 24-item scope is appropriate for a Mystery Hunt feeder.

#### Principles Applied

**Riven Standard — "Puzzle IS what field does."** TEST: Would a portmanteau linguist or wordsmith recognize this as their own work? Partially yes. The puzzle demands knowledge of how portmanteaus are constructed (which half belongs to which word), which is genuine linguistic awareness. However, the "fake jargon" framing is puzzle-construction-specific rather than linguistic-field-specific. FIRES ambiguously — adds modest signal beyond profile.

**Solving = Understanding — "Solving shows you understood."** TEST: Does a solver who finishes know more? Yes. Completing the puzzle requires accurately reconstructing which halves compose each portmanteau, which is a genuine transfer of understanding. FIRES in the puzzle's favor — redundant with Katz profile coverage.

**Blame the Player — "Blame yourself."** TEST: At reveal, respect or resentment? FIRES. A solver who cannot identify "MOTEL" from the fake term "Hotor" (hotel-half + motor-half reversed) will feel they did not know enough about portmanteaus, not that the puzzle failed them. The mechanic is self-consistently fair. Resentment score: low. FIRES positively — partially redundant with profile.

**No Over-Scaffolding — "Remove instructions — still a puzzle?"** TEST: The instructions state the inversion rule explicitly. Remove them: can the solver work out the mechanic from the data? With 24 terms and definitions presented side by side, a careful solver could discover the portmanteau inversion pattern. The instructions are confirmatory, not load-bearing. Does not fire as a defect — puzzle survives removal of explicit instructions. Adds NEW signal: confirms the puzzle is not worksheet-dependent.

**Surprise Answer — "Pause, not nod."** TEST: Is SHAMROCK guessable from "portmanteau deconstruction + Science Hunt"? Implausible. The answer is not predictable from the topic. FIRES positively — adds NEW signal, not duplicated by profile.

**One Aha — "Name the single aha."** TEST: The aha is: "these fake terms are made from the wrong halves of two real portmanteaus." Is there one aha? Yes. But there is also a second aha when the solver recognizes how to match fake-term constructions to definitions. This is a two-aha puzzle, which the principle flags as a potential overload — but at 24 items, having a two-stage aha is appropriate. FIRES as mild concern — adds NEW signal.

**Reading Reward — "Genuine domain engagement."** TEST: Is keyword search sufficient? No. Knowing what MOTEL stands for (motor + hotel) requires constructive knowledge, not lookup. The puzzle rewards genuine portmanteau literacy. FIRES positively — partially redundant with profile.

**No Computation Without Deduction — "Formula isn't solving."** TEST: Remove the indexing instructions — still a puzzle? The matching step alone constitutes a real puzzle (identify and pair 24 items). The indexing is the extraction step, not the puzzle core. Does not fire as a defect. Adds modest NEW signal.

**Snyder's Computer Test — "Can a 10-line script solve this?"** TEST: A script would need: (1) a full portmanteau dictionary, (2) logic to identify opposite-half combinations, (3) matching to definitions. Step 2 requires semantic understanding of "opposite" in the portmanteau context — this is not trivially scriptable. The puzzle likely passes the computer test (resists trivial automation). FIRES positively — adds NEW signal.

**Interlock Not Independence — "Independent lookups = quiz."** TEST: Can clues be solved in any order without affecting each other? Yes — each fake-term/definition pair is independent. But the dependency is in the *portmanteau knowledge base*, not in the puzzle's sequential structure. This is a quiz-adjacent concern. FIRES mildly negative — adds modest NEW signal.

**Verify Last Mile — "Trace letter by letter."** TEST: Tracing SHAMROCK: 24 pairs, each yielding one letter. This requires verifying each index into each two-word phrase. At 24 items, Last Mile verification is non-trivial. The extraction is long but mechanically sound. Does not fire as a defect.

**"Is the thematic coherence structural, not decorative?"** FIRES — positive. The six locations all share a property (presumably related to "H2No" — no water, or water-adjacent activity), and the extraction comes from the "differences" between how each location expresses that property. #### Principles Applied

**Reading Reward — "Genuine domain engagement."** TEST: Is the historical knowledge required for genuine research or keyword lookup? **No Over-Scaffolding — "Remove the flavor — still a puzzle?"** TEST: Without "watch out for differences," the solver still has six clues to resolve but loses the extraction direction.
