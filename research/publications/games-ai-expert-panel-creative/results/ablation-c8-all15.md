## Overview: Reviewer Voice Commitments (C8)

**Dan Katz** reads structurally and contractually. He tests whether the mechanism promise is kept, whether the extraction chain holds letter-by-letter (Verify the Last Mile), and whether a solver who completes the puzzle feels respect rather than resentment (Blame the Player). He names failure modes without the credential weight that made C6 readings more conservative in borderline cases.

**Thomas Snyder** reads as a constructor and diagnostician. He traces solve paths for uniqueness, strips elements for load-bearing necessity, and applies Snyder's Computer Test directly: could a 10-line script solve this? If yes, it needs a deduction layer. He checks the Last Mile independently from the main mechanism. Without credential framing, his readings are sharper on the construction tests and less anchored to positional authority.

**Dana Young** reads visually and experientially. She checks whether the mechanic is the theme, whether the extraction earns its step (Verify the Last Mile applied to experiential logic), and whether the answer is inevitable (Blame the Player: if you feel the answer was arbitrary, the designer failed). The trimmed bio leaves her design philosophy fully intact; the injected principles add explicit diagnostic checkpoints she already uses implicitly.

## Puzzle 1: Scicabulary — Answer: SHAMROCK | MIT/Elite | C8

### C8 Panel Notes

**Dan Katz (C8):** The mechanism contract is clear and honest — the instruction explains what portmanteau inversion means, and that transparency earns solver trust. Applying Verify the Last Mile: the index step from jargon term number into the matched two-word phrase letter-by-letter is sound; each of 24 pairs contributes exactly one letter to SHAMROCK, and tracing that chain is clean. Applying Blame the Player: after working through the portmanteau deconstruction and finding SHAMROCK, does a solver blame themselves for not getting it faster? Mostly yes — the mechanism is fair and the rules are stated — but SHAMROCK doesn't feel like the word you were doing all along. The answer is arbitrary relative to the mechanism's domain (fake scientific jargon), which creates a faint resentment at the end. The computer test: a script that could generate fake portmanteau inversions and match them to two-word phrases would require knowing what portmanteaus exist and how to construct their "opposite halves" — that knowledge constraint is sufficient to clear the computer test. This is a human-authored puzzle.

**Thomas Snyder (C8):** Snyder's Computer Test applied: could a script enumerate valid portmanteau-inversion jargon terms and match them to clued two-word phrases? The inversion step requires knowing the source portmanteaus, and the selection of portmanteaus is a human editorial decision that a script cannot make well. Clears the test. Verify the Last Mile: index number → two-word phrase → specific letter position → SHAMROCK. The chain holds. I want to note that the entry point — finding the first successfully matched pair — is not forced by the puzzle's structure. You can attempt pairings in any order, and wrong early attempts could cascade. The solve path is not uniquely constrained from a first entry. Without the credential authority context of C6, I find this more concerning, not less: the forced-path test fails, and the puzzle is relying on team size (parallel work) to compensate. Conceptually strong; structurally somewhat loose.

**Dana Young (C8):** The mechanic is genuinely the theme: you are doing linguistic science, manufacturing fake jargon, and you inhabit that world throughout. The two-word phrase clues are the best element — funny, specific, warm. The extraction (index into matched phrase) is clean but emotionally neutral, which I noted in C6 and the injected principles sharpen: does the Last Mile — the letter-indexing step — transform the solve in a way that matters, or just delay the answer? Mostly the latter. Blame the Player: after revealing SHAMROCK, does a solver feel respect or mild resentment? The mechanism is fair, so blame-the-player holds for the main mechanism. But the answer itself generates slight resentment — you were building fake scientific portmanteaus and the answer is a clover? The disconnect is mild but real. C8 principles push this toward a slight Confirmation penalty that C6 softened via authority consensus.
