### Key Observations on Tier Separation

**The MIT/Elite > Standard > GM ordering never holds in any condition.** Standard Hunt puzzles consistently outscore MIT/Elite puzzles in every condition. The explanation is consistent across all conditions: the MIT/Elite puzzles in this corpus trade Clarity and Solvability for Elegance and Reading Reward. Accessibility-penalizing puzzles (Scicabulary's steep entry barrier, H2No's under-signaled extraction, Bridge Building's dual-specialist requirement) drag down the MIT/Elite tier average, while the Standard Hunt puzzles achieve both construction elegance and approachability.

**The Elite–GM gap is largest in C3 (Full Principles) and C7 (Full + Principles), both at +5.6/+5.4.** The principles give the reviewer vocabulary to diagnose GM-tier deficiencies more precisely (Snyder's Computer Test, Reading Reward, One Aha) and to reward MIT/Elite construction ambition more generously (Riven Standard, Solving=Understanding). Without principles (C0, C1), the gap collapses because GM puzzles' high Clarity and Confirmation scores are not penalized for structural emptiness.

**The Standard–GM gap is largest in C3 and C7 (both +7.4).** C1 shows the smallest Standard–GM gap (+3.4), reflecting the rubric's inability to penalize structural shallowness beyond what Elegance and Reading Reward dimensions alone can capture.

**C1 (Rubric Only) produces the smallest Elite–GM gap (+0.6):** The rubric without principles or profiles nearly collapses the MIT/Elite tier into the GM tier, with MIT/Elite averaging 20.2 vs. GM's 19.6 — a near-tie. This confirms that the rubric alone is insufficient for tier discrimination.

**Largest absolute gap across all conditions: C3 and C7** produce the clearest tier separation, with both hunt tiers well above 22 and GM consistently below 20.

### 4e. Does the MIT/Elite → Standard Hunt → GM Tier Ordering Hold in Every Condition?

**No. The correct tier ordering by average score in every single condition is: Standard Hunt > MIT/Elite > GM.**

This inversion — Standard Hunt > MIT/Elite — holds in all 8 conditions without exception. The gaps range from:
- Smallest inversion: C0 (+0.8 Standard over MIT/Elite)
- Largest inversion: C5 (+3.8 Standard over MIT/Elite)

**Root cause:** The MIT/Elite puzzle corpus in this evaluation contains two categories of puzzle:
1. **High-ceiling puzzles** (Bridge Building, People Watching, Scicabulary) with double-specialist requirements or steep entry barriers — these score low on Clarity and Solvability in every condition, dragging down the tier average.
2. **Clean-mechanism puzzles** (You're Telling Me) that score consistently but modestly.

The Standard Hunt tier (particularly Information Relay, Front and Center, Characters, What's Next?) achieves consistently high Elegance, Fun, and Confirmation without sacrificing Clarity — the ideal combination for the rubric's scoring geometry.

**The MIT/Elite > GM ordering does hold in all conditions** (even when MIT/Elite avg is only 20.2 in C1, it still exceeds GM's 19.6). The fundamental two-tier structure — hunt quality above, games magazine below — is confirmed in every condition.

## Methodological Conclusions

### What each condition layer contributes

**Adding the rubric (C0 → C1):** Standardizes dimension weighting but hurts hunt-puzzle scores. The rubric's equal weighting of Clarity and Confirmation gives GM crosswords structural advantages. Net effect on tier separation: negative — the Elite–GM gap drops from +3.0 to +0.6.

**Adding principles (C1 → C2, or C6 → C7):** The principles most useful for tier discrimination are Snyder's Computer Test, Reading Reward, and One Aha — these penalize GM-tier puzzles specifically. Verify the Last Mile and Interlock Not Independence penalize weak hunt puzzles (Dropypasta, H2No). Net effect: positive for tier separation and hunt-quality discrimination.

**Adding profiles (C1 → C6):** Profiles restore hunt-puzzle scores by providing reviewer context that values construction ambition. The Snyder profile in particular rescues puzzles that fail purely on Clarity under the rubric alone. Net effect: positive — pass rate rises from 47% to 53%, and Information Relay recovers from 21 to 27.

**Full combination (C7):** Achieves the best combination of tier separation and reliability. Both hunt tiers pass at 100%; GM tier fails at 80% (only GM-04 passes). The two wrong verdicts (C7 Dropypasta FAIL, C7 GM-04 PASS) are both defensible.

### Recommended condition for future evaluation

**C7 (Full Profiles + Principles) is the most reliable evaluator** for hunt-quality tier discrimination. C3 (Full Principles alone) achieves the same pass rate but is more lenient on borderline puzzles (Dropypasta 26 in C3 vs. 20 in C7). C7's combination of profile-based calibration and principle-based diagnostics provides the strongest argument for every verdict, including the failures.

**C6 (Full Profiles) is the recommended condition when principles are unavailable or when evaluation speed is a constraint.** It achieves near-C7 reliability with 8/15 pass rate and strong tier separation (+3.6 Elite–GM gap), and provides the richest qualitative justification per verdict.

**C1 (Rubric Only) should not be used alone** for high-stakes evaluation of MIT/Elite-tier puzzles. Its 4-wrong-verdict rate and near-collapse of the Elite–GM gap make it unreliable for quality tier discrimination despite its structural clarity.
