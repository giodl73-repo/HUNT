# Ablation Study: Wavelength P4 — Chart Toppers
## Subject Puzzle: P4 — Chart Toppers (SHRINE)
## Mechanism: A=1 alphabet-position encoding; six classic rock/pop songs; numbers decode to letters
## Hunt: WAVELENGTH (music domain, Scenario 4)
## Date: 2026-02-28
## Reviewer conditions: C0–C7

---

## Puzzle Summary (for reference)

Six songs appear on a list attributed to "Vee" (the fictional DJ). Each song has a number beside it. The introductory flavor notes the numbers are "Vee's numbers" and "not Billboard positions." A table prompts solvers to decode what the numbers mean. A closing line reads: "Vee always said numbers and letters were the same thing." The extraction is positional: 19=S, 8=H, 18=R, 9=I, 14=N, 5=E → SHRINE. The puzzle's author notes flag it as difficulty 2/5, intentionally a "gateway" puzzle in the 6-puzzle hunt.

Songs and numbers:
- "Sledgehammer" — Peter Gabriel (1986) — 19
- "Enjoy the Silence" — Depeche Mode (1990) — 8
- "Burning Down the House" — Talking Heads (1983) — 18
- "Bohemian Rhapsody" — Queen (1976) — 9
- "Everybody Wants to Rule the World" — Tears for Fears (1985) — 14
- "Go Your Own Way" — Fleetwood Mac (1977) — 5

Correct answer: SHRINE

Young approaches this as visual and experiential design:

## Cross-Condition Analysis

### 1. The Verdict Is Consistent; Only the Explanation Changes

P4 — Chart Toppers fails to reach 22/30 in all eight conditions. The range is 14–19 for C0–C7. The puzzle's core design problem (mechanism is orthogonal to domain; songs are labels not puzzle material) is detectable with minimal context and fully diagnosed with maximal context. No additional context changes the verdict.

**Implication for the ablation:** For this puzzle, context depth affects diagnostic specificity, not pass/fail outcomes. The puzzle is far enough below threshold that even C0 registers failure.

### 2. Rubric Addition (C0→C1): +1 Point, No New Diagnosis

Adding a scoring rubric raises the total by 1 (18→19) by forcing the reviewer to hold each dimension separately. This catches the "Elegance could be 3 rather than 2 if I separate it from Reading Reward" effect. The rubric adds structure without adding diagnostic vocabulary.

### 3. Principle Names (C2): Same Score, Better Prose

Adding principle names and quotes to C1 produces the same numeric score (19) but substantially richer prose. "Genuine domain engagement" is the first vocabulary that lets the reviewer name the Reading Reward problem specifically. This is a diagnostic improvement with zero numeric impact — which may suggest the rubric-based evaluation has a floor effect on scores even when the reviewer knows *why* a score is low.

### 4. Tests (C3): -2 Points, Sharper Failures

Adding operational tests drops the score from 19 to 17. The mechanism: Elegance drops from 3 to 1 (Snyder Computer Test and Interlock both fail), and Reading Reward drops from 2 to 1 (keyword-search-sufficient test is definitive). Tests do not affect Solvability (already 4) or Clarity (already acceptable). The drop is driven entirely by Elegance and Reading Reward — the two dimensions that the tests make binary (pass/fail) rather than graded.

### 5. Design Philosophy (C4) vs. Review Lens (C5): -3 Points Apart

This is the most interesting split in the study. C4 (philosophy only) scores 17; C5 (lens only) scores 14. The philosophy sections produce nuanced aesthetic judgments that preserve some dimensional credit. The lens sections produce targeted binary-ish questions ("Is there a puzzle here or a procedure?") that produce floor scores on Elegance and Fun. The Fun dimension drops from 3 (C4) to 2 (C5) specifically because the Katz lens question "Would he want to solve this?" is evaluated at an 8-Mystery-Hunt-wins standard, while the philosophy section's concern is structural (does the hunt promise something it delivers?). The lens over-calibrates to elite solvers; the philosophy section holds a structural-purpose standard.

### 6. Full Profiles (C6): Same Score as C3 and C4, But Richest Narrative

C6 (full profiles) produces the same total as C3 (17) and C4 (17), but the per-reviewer narrative is the richest of any condition. Katz, Snyder, and Young each bring distinct interpretations that partially cancel out harsh judgments — Katz's structural-justification framework prevents the score from collapsing to C5 levels because he explicitly credits the gateway-puzzle design intent. The multi-reviewer composite produces mid-range scores (2 for Elegance) rather than floor scores (1) because not all three reviewers agree that the failure is absolute.

### 7. Full Context (C7): +1 from C6, Constructive Critique Emerges

C7 (full profiles + all principles) scores 18 — one point above C6 (17) and equal to C0. The increase is driven by Confirmation rising from 3 to 4 (Verify the Last Mile is now explicitly tested and passes). The most distinctive C7 contribution is qualitative, not numeric: Young's identification of a redesign path (make the encoding musical — letters from song titles at chart positions, syllable counts at peak years). This constructive-critique mode only appears when a full persona is active, because it requires inhabiting a designer's value system deeply enough to imagine what the puzzle would look like if it were rebuilt correctly.

### 8. The Floor Effect on Solvability

Solvability scores 4 in every single condition (C0–C7). This is the most robust dimension in the dataset. No amount of context changes the verdict that A=1 is a well-known, accessible cipher appropriate for a gateway slot. This suggests that technical accessibility assessments are context-independent — the puzzle either works for its audience or it doesn't, and this particular cipher-recognition task works consistently.

## Theoretical Observations

### Context Depth Does Not Determine Pass/Fail for This Puzzle

The puzzle falls in the 14–19 range regardless of condition. Had the puzzle been closer to threshold (e.g., a 20–24 range puzzle), condition effects might dominate. P4's core weakness (songs-as-labels, computer-solvable mechanism) is visible at every context level. The ablation finding here is: for clearly-subthreshold puzzles, context affects diagnostic quality more than scoring outcome.

### The Lens-Only Condition (C5) Is the Most Punishing but Also the Most Calibration-Sensitive

C5 scores 14 — the lowest in the study. The mechanism is the Katz lens question "Would he want to solve this?" being evaluated at elite-solver calibration. This is a significant confound: the puzzle is explicitly designed for casual music fans, not MIT Mystery Hunt competitors. A lens condition without persona context applies the lens questions at face value to the puzzle without accounting for the intended audience. This suggests that lens-only evaluations should always be accompanied by either (a) a persona that provides calibration context, or (b) an explicit audience specification.

### Philosophy-Only (C4) Produces the Most Interpretively Rich Unique Contribution

The Dana Young philosophy section produces an argument that no other condition makes: the answer SHRINE does not name the experience of being in Vee's broadcast booth. This is a phenomenological objection to the puzzle that is visible only through the "what does the solver become while solving?" philosophical lens. Tests don't capture this (a test would say: is the answer guessable from topic? No. Pass.). Lenses don't capture this (a lens would say: is the answer arbitrary or inevitable? Arbitrary.). Only the full philosophical framework makes the specific argument that the answer should name an *experience*, not just be a non-guessable word. This is the highest-quality unique insight in the study.

### Maximum Context (C7) Produces the Only Constructive Redesign Path

Only C7 produces a concrete redesign suggestion (make the encoding musically meaningful: letter positions within song titles at chart peak, or syllable counts, or some encoding that requires musical knowledge). This is the kind of feedback that would actually improve the puzzle. No other condition produces actionable design guidance — they all produce verdicts. This suggests that full-persona context is necessary but not sufficient for constructive feedback; it requires the persona's design philosophy section to be active simultaneously with the review lenses, so the reviewer can imagine not just "what's wrong" but "what it would look like if it were right."

## Conclusion

Across all 8 conditions, P4 — Chart Toppers fails to pass the 22/30 threshold. The puzzle's core design weakness — the A=1 encoding mechanism is orthogonal to the music domain, making the songs decorative labels rather than puzzle material — is detectable at every context level but diagnosed with increasing precision as context depth increases.

**Key findings ranked by impact:**

1. **Tests (C3 addition) produce the largest negative score shift**: -2 points from C2, driven by Elegance and Reading Reward floors. Tests make the puzzle-vs-procedure distinction operational.

2. **Lens-only (C5) produces the lowest total (14)** due to elite-calibration effect on Fun. This is arguably the least accurate condition for audience-appropriate evaluation.

3. **Philosophy-only (C4) produces the highest-quality unique insight**: Young's phenomenological observation that the answer should name the solving experience is only visible through full design philosophy.

4. **Full profiles (C6) produce the richest multi-voice narrative** at the same score as C3 and C4. Persona diversity moderates extreme verdicts.

5. **Maximum context (C7) produces the only constructive redesign path**: the suggestion to make the encoding musically meaningful is only possible when a full design persona is active alongside all principles.

6. **Solvability is context-invariant** (4/5 in every condition): accessibility assessments are robust.

7. **Reading Reward floors immediately** (drops to 1) as soon as any principle or lens is applied — it is the most context-sensitive negative dimension.

The puzzle's explicit design rationale — a deliberate "gateway" puzzle for a 6-puzzle casual-audience hunt — partially mitigates but does not overcome the structural failures identified by the fuller conditions. A redesign that makes the encoding musically meaningful (letter positions in song titles, chart-year syllable counts, or other music-domain encoding) would address the core Reading Reward and Riven Standard failures while preserving the accessible One Aha structure.
