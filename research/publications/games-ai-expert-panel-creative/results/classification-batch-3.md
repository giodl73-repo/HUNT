## Characters
**Source:** Huntinality III, 2023 | **Answer:** UNHEXING

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | Standard Hunt | Intrinsic quality is MIT-adjacent (multi-layer encoding system, elegant triple-mechanism aha), but context is Huntinality III (accessible hunt); classifying by intrinsic puzzle quality |
| Mechanism | Hybrid (Knowledge-extraction + Transformation) | Layer 1: clues yield hex-valid words. Layer 2: arithmetic in base 16. Layer 3: ASCII decode. Three distinct transformations chained |
| Domain Load | 2 | Hex notation helpful but not essential; ASCII lookup table typically provided or derivable; the harder demand is knowing clue answers (BABA, AC, etc.) |
| Aha Clarity | 3 | Single clean aha: "all clue answers are A–F only — they ARE hex." Title and flavor ("base," "code") are the nudge. Once seen, everything snaps |
| Paper Suitability | Yes | Three-layer transformation chain is textbook-reproducible and illustrates layered mechanism design cleanly |
| Quality Signal | 3 | The answer UNHEXING is an invented portmanteau that names the mechanism; elegant self-reference. Clue selection enforcing hex-only letters is the hardest construction constraint and it holds throughout |

**Katz note:** The structural elegance here is the answer naming the thing you did — UNHEXING. That's the kind of closed loop that justifies a puzzle's existence. Eight equations is the right count: enough to recover 8 ASCII characters without over-cluing. The flavor text does the minimum scaffolding needed without spoiling the aha.

**Snyder note:** Construction difficulty here is formidable: every clue answer must consist only of letters A–F. That's not a filter applied at the end — it constrains every clue choice from the start. And it holds without a single exception. The solve path is one-directional: recognize hex → compute → decode. No branching, no ambiguity. The intended entry point (recognizing the hex constraint) is designed in through the flavor text. This is finished construction.

**Young note:** The title "Characters" is doing triple duty: characters in the forest (flavor), ASCII characters (mechanism), and character of the puzzle itself. The flavor text's "find their base" is precise without being obvious. The resolution — UNHEXING — lands with a smile because it names exactly what you spent the puzzle doing. Inevitable rather than arbitrary.

**Use for ablation:** Yes — strongest mechanism-chain example in the batch. Ideal contrast with single-aha puzzles.

## Tales from a Dating Show
**Source:** Huntinality III, 2023 | **Answer:** GEORGE III

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | Standard Hunt | Multi-stage interactive structure with embedded wordplay (acrostic, Guess Who elimination, enumeration clue) — above accessible, below MIT/Elite in insight requirement |
| Mechanism | Hybrid (Knowledge-extraction + Interactive-JS + Transformation) | Stage 1: Disney lyric ID + acrostic. Stage 2: Guess Who elimination grid. Stage 3: extracted phrase → enumeration → external reference (Hamilton) |
| Domain Load | 3 | Disney canon required for lyric identification; Hamilton required for final step. The Guess Who elimination criteria are genre-specific (Disney villains, Disney forest singers). Domain load is genuinely high |
| Aha Clarity | 2 | Two ahas present: (1) PLAY GUESS WHO acrostic reveals the game mechanic; (2) "KING FROM HAMILTON" as the extracted phrase. Neither is the single-aha type — the puzzle is a three-stage procedure |
| Paper Suitability | Partial | Interactive Guess Who grid is not text-reproducible; static description conveys mechanism but not solving experience |
| Quality Signal | 2 | Construction is solid and the acrostic PLAY GUESS WHO is elegant. But the Hamilton pivot at the end is a knowledge-gate — if you don't know Hamilton, the final step stalls completely. Domain stacking (Disney + Hamilton + Guess Who rules) creates accessibility cliffs |

**Katz note:** The PLAY GUESS WHO acrostic is the best moment here — it's instruction embedded in content, which is structurally correct. The puzzle would be stronger if the Hamilton extraction were clued internally rather than relying on recognizing an external cultural product. As constructed, the difficulty curve spikes at a point the solver cannot predict, which is a fairness problem in competitive contexts.

**Snyder note:** The elimination grid is doing real combinatorial work, which I respect. But the elimination criteria require genre knowledge (what counts as a Disney villain, what counts as a "forest singer") that introduces ambiguity at the classification stage. The solve path has a branch — you can partially identify characters before applying criteria — and that partial-solve flexibility is somewhat uncontrolled. Tight where it matters (the acrostic), looser where it doesn't.

**Young note:** The dating show frame is committing to its world — the flavor text, the contestant declarations, the heart/devil icons on the board. But the Hamilton reveal at the end imports an external world rather than remaining inside the puzzle's own universe. The answer GEORGE III is fine but not inevitable — it's a trivia lookup disguised as an extraction. The frame does not earn the final step.

**Use for ablation:** Conditional — useful for studying knowledge-gate penalties in domain load, but JS dependency limits clean static comparison.

## Information Relay
**Source:** Huntinality III, 2023 | **Answer:** FINISH LINE

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | MIT/Elite | The meta-mechanism (apply Grandma's hearing method to Grandpa's clues) requires a non-obvious second-order reasoning step — understanding not just what each bear does, but how the rules combine. The intermediate extraction phrase "HOW NANA HEARS GRAMPS" is itself the instruction |
| Mechanism | Hybrid (Transformation + Wordplay + Meta) | Each bear's clue style is a distinct transformation type; the extraction requires composing two transformation rules; the final answer is a homophone chain |
| Domain Load | 2 | Video game references (Child Bear) are helpful; the puzzle provides enough structural redundancy that most clues can be gotten without all domain knowledge |
| Aha Clarity | 3 | The single clean aha: "HOW NANA HEARS GRAMPS" tells you to apply Grandma's mishearing method to Grandpa's clues. Once you see this, the solve path is forced. The aha IS the mechanism |
| Paper Suitability | Yes | Fully text-reproducible; the mechanism is precise and documentable; the intermediate phrase is a beautiful self-descriptive clue |
| Quality Signal | 3 | This is exceptional construction. Five distinct transformation types, each internally consistent, composing into a meta-transformation. The intermediate result "HOW NANA HEARS GRAMPS" is a puzzle within a puzzle — it describes exactly what to do next while being a product of what you just did |

**Katz note:** The structural architecture here is rare. Five different rule systems that each hold internally, then a synthesis step where two of those rules must be composed in the right order. The puzzle doesn't tell you which bear's method is used for extraction (Grandpa) and which governs how to read his clues (Grandma) — you have to figure out the composition from the intermediate phrase. That's a load-bearing design choice, not an accident.

**Snyder note:** Every clue pair is load-bearing — remove any one bear's style and the composition breaks. The entry points are distributed: you can crack Child Bear's video game clues first, or Grandma's homophones, or Mama's sandwiches, in any order. But convergence is forced. The solving path doesn't branch; it narrows. The final step — homophone of FINISH LINE — is elegant without being arbitrary. This is what finished construction looks like.

**Young note:** The Exquisite 'Froot' frame is doing real work here: it establishes a world of imperfect communication, and the puzzle's answer names the condition of being done with that broken relay. FINISH LINE is both an end-state and a commentary on what the telephone game produces when you apply the right lens. The thematic coherence between frame and mechanism is as close to exact as I see in most hunts.

**Use for ablation:** Yes — the strongest single puzzle in the batch. A clear candidate for ablation alongside MIT/Elite puzzles; intrinsic mechanism quality exceeds its accessible-hunt context.

## The Bakery
**Source:** Huntinality III, 2023 | **Answer:** LOAF-BEARING

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | Accessible Hunt | The "baker's dozen" concept is accessible and well-telegraphed; identifying the extra set member is the primary skill; domain knowledge (knowing all members of named sets) is the main challenge |
| Mechanism | Knowledge-extraction | Set completion: identify sets from clued members, find the uncued extra, index. Pure knowledge retrieval plus indexing |
| Domain Load | 3 | Requires knowing complete membership of named groups (all 9 reindeer, all flags over Texas, etc.); gaps in any set block the corresponding extraction letter |
| Aha Clarity | 2 | Aha is present: "baker's dozen means one extra per set." The flavor text delivers it directly. Mechanism is clear from early contact — no hidden second layer |
| Paper Suitability | Yes | Fully text-reproducible; mechanism is canonical; good representative of knowledge-extraction puzzle type |
| Quality Signal | 2 | Competently constructed. The answer LOAF-BEARING is a pun on LOAD-BEARING (structural term) + LOAF (bread/bakery), which is thematically tight. The clue set is varied and well-calibrated. But this is execution of a well-established type without novel mechanism insight |

**Katz note:** The baker's dozen concept is earned — it's a real quantity (13 = 12 + 1) that names the structural gimmick. The puzzle is solving a set-completion problem, which is a legitimate puzzle type. The weakness is that domain gaps cause complete blockages: if you don't know that "Sixpence" is the fifth element of "Something Old, New, Borrowed, Blue," you get nothing from that entry. The puzzle has no cross-checking — each letter is independent. That's a construction choice that makes it brittle for partially-knowledgeable solvers.

**Snyder note:** The clues are precise and the sets are well-chosen (varied domains, clear canonical membership). The extraction index (7, 3, 5, 4, 11, 8, 1, 9, 6, 2, 10) requires solvers to have all extra members before they can extract — this creates a completion gate rather than a progressive reveal. I would prefer a construction where partial sets yield partial confirmation, but the current design is internally consistent.

**Young note:** LOAF-BEARING landing as a pun on LOAD-BEARING is the moment where the bakery frame pays off — the answer names the puzzle's structural role (it's a load-bearing puzzle in the hunt) while being thematically grounded. That's the kind of answer that feels inevitable. The path to get there is conventional, but the destination is right.

**Use for ablation:** Conditional — good representative of knowledge-extraction type, but low novelty limits its comparative value unless studying domain-gap penalty effects.

## What's Next?
**Source:** Huntinality III, 2023 | **Answer:** AFGHANISTAN

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | Standard Hunt | The double-aha structure (image → song → "next item" → index → phrase → "next word after phrase") is above accessible; requires both song knowledge and the insight that the extracted phrase is itself a lyric |
| Mechanism | Hybrid (Knowledge-extraction + Transformation) | Layer 1: identify "We Didn't Start the Fire" reference from image. Layer 2: find the *next* item (not the item itself). Layer 3: recognize extracted phrase is itself a lyric. Layer 4: the answer is what comes next |
| Domain Load | 3 | "We Didn't Start the Fire" lyric sequence knowledge is essential; without it, neither the image-to-event mapping nor the "next item" step is possible. Deep domain specificity |
| Aha Clarity | 2 | Two ahas: (1) "what's next in the song?" (not the depicted item but its successor); (2) "RUSSIANS IN" is itself a lyric — the title was telling you to ask what comes next twice. Second aha lands harder than first |
| Paper Suitability | Partial | Images not reproducible as text; the puzzle's mechanism is text-describable but the actual clues (images) require external reproduction. The description captures the mechanism cleanly |
| Quality Signal | 3 | The recursive structure — "what's next?" applied twice, at different scales — is genuinely elegant. The title "What's Next?" is load-bearing (it describes the mechanism AND is itself answered twice). This is a puzzle that knows what it's doing |

**Katz note:** The recursion is the key: the puzzle's title is also its final instruction. "What's next?" applied to an image gives you the next lyric; "what's next?" applied to the extracted phrase gives you the answer. This is the kind of structural elegance that distinguishes mechanism from execution. The domain wall is high (requiring sequence knowledge of a specific song) but the mechanism is clean enough that the domain is the only barrier.

**Snyder note:** The "next item, not this item" constraint is a deliberate misdirection during the extraction step — you get the depicted thing first, then must shift to its successor. That's a designed forced-error moment, which I find somewhat artificial. The stronger construction would make the "next" constraint feel emergent from the logic rather than imposed. That said, the confirmation check (B = letter count of next item) is excellent: it's a built-in verification step that tells you when you have the right next item, which is considerate construction for a potentially tricky step.

**Young note:** The puzzle lives and dies on whether the solver feels the recursive click — "what's next?" working at two different scales. When it lands, the title retroactively means something it didn't seem to mean before. That's the kind of reframing I look for. The image set (maps, movie posters, historical photographs) is doing real work as primary material, not illustration. The visual design is in service of the mechanism.

**Use for ablation:** Yes — recursive meta-mechanism embedded in a feeder puzzle is unusual and makes it a strong comparison case.

## Front and Center
**Source:** Huntinality III, 2023 | **Answer:** RADAR

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | Standard Hunt | Palindrome reconstruction from enumerated word-lengths and subtitle clues is above accessible (requires creative word-filling across multi-word palindromes) without requiring the deep insight of elite puzzle design |
| Mechanism | Wordplay | Palindrome reconstruction + center-letter extraction. The mechanism is fully self-contained within the English wordplay tradition |
| Domain Load | 1 | No external domain required; the subtitle clues are self-sufficient; the palindrome constraint plus word-length enumeration narrows the solution space deterministically |
| Aha Clarity | 3 | Single clean aha: "SEMI TIMES is a palindrome → all headlines are palindromes → take center letters." Each layer confirms the prior. The aha IS the mechanism and it's visible in the puzzle's own masthead |
| Paper Suitability | Yes | Fully text-reproducible; all clues are text-based; answer is a palindrome — cleanly demonstrable in static format |
| Quality Signal | 3 | The answer being RADAR (a palindrome) while every headline is a palindrome while the publication name is a palindrome is a closed thematic loop. The palindromes themselves range from simple (STAR RATS STATS STAR RATS) to genuinely witty (MR. OWL ATE MR. METAL WORM; A SANTA DEVIL NEVER EVEN LIVED AT NASA). The construction has voice |

**Katz note:** SEMI TIMES is doing everything — it's a palindrome, it's the source of the "center" idea (a newspaper has a crease, the crease marks the middle), and it's the aha delivery vehicle. The puzzle earns every one of its structural choices. Five is the right palindrome count: enough to spell a word without over-straining the construction. MR. OWL ATE MR. METAL WORM is the highlight — it reads as a mini-narrative while being a perfect palindrome.

**Snyder note:** Each palindrome's word-length enumeration plus subtitle uniquely constrains the answer — I verified this mentally on two of the five and found no ambiguity. The entry point (noticing SEMI TIMES is a palindrome) is constructed into the puzzle's visual header, which is correct design. A solver who misses the header aha can still derive it from the headline pattern. Two entry routes to the same discovery is robust construction.

**Young note:** The newspaper frame is thematically exact — a newspaper has a fold, a front page has a center, "front and center" is the extraction instruction disguised as an idiom. The subtitles are written with genuine wit (the Tootsie Pop / iron wiggler one is absurd in the best way). RADAR as the answer resolves the theme completely: the thing you extracted is also a palindrome. Visual grammar consistent throughout.

**Use for ablation:** Yes — exemplary single-mechanism design with closed thematic loop. Clean, text-reproducible, demonstrates wordplay construction quality clearly.

## Itinerary
**Source:** Teammate Hunt 2021 | **Answer:** WILLIAMSBURG

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | Standard Hunt | Chronological ordering plus historical entity identification is the core skill; above trivia (requires knowing obscure October 1st dates) but the mechanism is indexing rather than insight |
| Mechanism | Knowledge-extraction | Historical event identification + chronological ordering + indexed letter extraction. Mechanism is sequential rather than transformational |
| Domain Load | 3 | Requires identifying specific historical entities from brief descriptions; some are very obscure (Eadwig the All-Fair, Third Treaty of San Ildefonso, Mrs Beeton's Book). Domain gaps block letters |
| Aha Clarity | 2 | Aha is present: "all events happened on October 1st, the carnival date — do them in order." The flavor text ("make sure we do things in order") telegraphs the ordering step. No hidden second layer |
| Paper Suitability | Yes | Fully text-reproducible; the clues are text descriptions; the mechanism is documentable |
| Quality Signal | 2 | Competently constructed with a clean thematic hook (October 1st = carnival day = "do things in order"). The erratum (first two rows swapped) suggests a production error that affected the solve on launch day — a construction quality signal worth noting. WILLIAMSBURG is an appropriate answer for a hunt with a carnival theme |

**Katz note:** The October 1st constraint is load-bearing thematically and mechanically — it's why the events exist as a set and why chronological ordering is the right step. The puzzle is honest about what it's testing. My concern is the obscurity gradient: Eadwig the All-Fair (959 AD English king) and the Third Treaty of San Ildefonso (1800) require either specialist knowledge or research. In a hunt with research access this is fine; in a timed setting it creates a hard knowledge wall. The erratum on launch day is a calibration failure.

**Snyder note:** The solve path is well-defined: identify entities, sort by year, extract indexed letters. Each step confirms the prior (wrong year order = garbled answer). The letter indexing method (ticket stubs marking specific positions) is a visual design choice that works in the puzzle's fair presentation context. My construction note: the erratum suggests the puzzle shipped with a row-swap error that presumably came from a late ordering change. This is a testing failure — a final read-through with the solve path explicit would have caught it.

**Young note:** The carnival itinerary frame is doing legitimate thematic work — an itinerary has dates, dates go in order, "make sure we do things in order" is the mechanic stated as diary logic. WILLIAMSBURG (colonial Williamsburg, a living-history experience) connects to the carnival/historical theme. The frame holds. The obscurity of some historical events creates moments where the solver is just searching rather than deducing, which breaks the experiential flow.

**Use for ablation:** Conditional — good representative of knowledge-extraction with ordering step, but the launch erratum and knowledge-wall issues make it a weaker choice than cleaner puzzles in the batch.

## Single Elimination
**Source:** Teammate Hunt 2021 | **Answer:** PLATINUM

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | MIT/Elite | A constraint-satisfaction puzzle over a 26×26 binary space, requiring strategic combinatorial probing, plus entanglement with a companion puzzle (Quick Response), plus a QR code mask decoding step. Multi-session, technically complex, genuine systems design |
| Mechanism | Interactive-JS | Cannot be meaningfully classified otherwise — the core mechanism requires iterative submission to probe a hidden matrix; the static puzzle document is just a rule-set; the puzzle is the interface interaction |
| Domain Load | 1 | No external knowledge required; the puzzle is self-contained within its rule system; the challenge is logical and combinatorial, not cultural |
| Aha Clarity | 1 | No single clean aha — the puzzle is a research project: design a probing strategy, submit words, recover the matrix, apply the mask. The "aha" is distributed across many submissions |
| Paper Suitability | No | Fundamentally JavaScript-dependent; cannot be described in static text in a way that captures the solving experience; the mechanism requires interaction to exist |
| Quality Signal | 3 | As a systems design achievement this is exceptional — two entangled puzzles (Single Elimination + Quick Response) that must be solved in tandem, with the final answer requiring combining outputs from both. The 26×26 binary matrix recovered through strategic word submission is a novel constraint-satisfaction mechanism. Construction quality is elite |

**Katz note:** This is the kind of puzzle that works only in a specific hunt context: a team hunt with a long time window and a companion puzzle to coordinate. The mechanism is technically impressive — a constraint-satisfaction space probed through strategic combinatorial input, with a companion puzzle that completes the decoding — but it's not a puzzle you "solve" in the traditional sense. You run a systematic investigation. That's a legitimate puzzle design choice; it just limits generalizability.

**Snyder note:** From a construction standpoint, the 26×26 binary matrix is a designed artifact — letter-vs-letter matchup outcomes are not random; they encode the answer when read through the correct lens. The construction challenge is ensuring the matrix can be recovered through the submission interface without ambiguity. If the probing strategy is designed correctly, every submission narrows the space deterministically. That's load-bearing construction at scale. I would want to audit whether the matrix has any cells that cannot be uniquely determined through the legal probing moves — that's the failure mode.

**Young note:** This puzzle does not have a visual resolution moment. There is no click, no reframe, no thematic landing — the output is a binary grid that you then run through a QR mask. The mechanism is brilliant as engineering; as an experience it is remote. I would never have designed this and I understand why someone did. It belongs to a different theory of what a puzzle is for.

**Use for ablation:** No — JS-dependent and experience-incompatible with static text corpus; useful as a type-classifier reference but not for direct ablation comparison.

## Par Re-sing
**Source:** Teammate Hunt 2021 | **Answer:** BLACKADDER'S CHRISTMAS CAROL

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | MIT/Elite | Double syllable rearrangement (rearrange syllables → real word → index → extract phrase → rearrange that phrase's syllables → answer) with 27 clue pairs plus category-indexed extraction is multi-step at MIT scale |
| Mechanism | Hybrid (Wordplay + Transformation) | Two-stage syllable rearrangement with intermediate knowledge-category indexing; the mechanism is applied to itself at the second stage |
| Domain Load | 2 | The nonsense phrases and category clues are self-cluing; knowing BLACKADDER'S CHRISTMAS CAROL is helpful for confirmation but the syllable rearrangement is the primary mechanism |
| Aha Clarity | 2 | Two ahas: (1) "these nonsense phrases are syllable rearrangements" (the title models it: PAR RE-SING = REPARSING); (2) the extracted phrase is itself a syllable rearrangement of the answer. The second aha is stronger |
| Paper Suitability | Yes | Fully text-reproducible; the mechanism is documentable; 27 clue pairs is manageable in static format |
| Quality Signal | 3 | The self-referential title (PAR RE-SING is itself a syllable rearrangement) is the tell: this puzzle knows exactly what it is. Applying the syllable rearrangement mechanism twice — once to get intermediate words, once to get the final answer — with the intermediate extraction phrase being the raw material for the second application is structurally elegant. The answer BLACKADDER'S CHRISTMAS CAROL is unexpected and specific, which means the syllable-rearrangement space was genuinely constrained |

**Katz note:** The second application of the mechanism is what elevates this. A puzzle that makes you do a thing twice — with the output of the first pass being the input to the second — has structural depth that most puzzles don't attempt. The risk is that solvers miss the second layer; the mitigation is that 27 clue pairs is enough that the intermediate extraction phrase should be legible enough to recognize as a syllable rearrangement itself. The mechanism is clean; the scale is right.

**Snyder note:** The title is a designed entry point — PAR RE-SING models the mechanism before the solver opens the puzzle. That's the right construction choice. With 27 clue pairs, the risk is that some syllable rearrangements are ambiguous (multiple valid rearrangements mapping to multiple plausible categories). I would want to verify that each left-side clue produces exactly one valid rearrangement that matches exactly one right-side category. The extraction confidence depends on that uniqueness holding throughout.

**Young note:** BLACKADDER'S CHRISTMAS CAROL is an odd, specific, delightful answer. It's not a famous title, it's not a generic answer, it's a particular thing that results from a particular syllable rearrangement of a particular extracted phrase. That specificity is the signature of good construction — the mechanism constrained the designer toward an answer that they could not have planned from the start. The puzzle found its own answer.

**Use for ablation:** Yes — self-referential mechanism design (mechanism applied to its own output) is a sophisticated construction technique worth including.

## Ablation Study Recommendation

**Strongest additions for ablation alongside MIT Mystery Hunt puzzles:**

**Tier 1 — Include without reservation:**

1. **Information Relay** — The highest intrinsic quality puzzle in the batch. The five-transformation-system composing into a meta-transformation, with the intermediate phrase "HOW NANA HEARS GRAMPS" as a self-descriptive instruction, is MIT/Elite construction that happens to appear in an accessible hunt. Strong contrast case: what does AI evaluation do with a high-quality puzzle in an accessible-hunt context?

2. **Front and Center** — Exemplary single-mechanism design with a closed thematic loop (palindromes throughout, answer is a palindrome). Text-reproducible, no domain required, domain load of 1. Clean test case for wordplay quality evaluation.

3. **Characters** — The three-layer chained transformation (clue → hex-valid word → hex arithmetic → ASCII) with a self-naming answer (UNHEXING) is a model of mechanism elegance. Ideal for studying how AI panels assess multi-layer mechanisms.

4. **Par Re-sing** — Self-referential mechanism (applied twice; second application operates on output of first) with a self-modeling title. Tests whether AI reviewers recognize second-order structural sophistication.

**Tier 2 — Include with study-design note:**

5. **What's Next?** — Recursive "what comes next?" mechanism at two scales is strong, but image-dependency limits text reproducibility. Include if image descriptions can be standardized; note the domain barrier (song sequence knowledge).

**Tier 3 — Conditional:**

6. **Tales from a Dating Show** — Useful for studying domain-stacking effects and JS-dependency penalties, but knowledge walls at both Disney and Hamilton stages introduce noise.

7. **The Bakery** — Good canonical knowledge-extraction representative; low novelty limits comparative value.

8. **Itinerary** — Useful for ordering-step mechanism; launch erratum and knowledge obscurity create quality confounds.

**Do not include:**

9. **Single Elimination** — Fundamentally JS-dependent and experience-incompatible with static text methodology. Valuable as a type-classifier data point (highest construction quality score, lowest paper suitability) but cannot participate in ablation on equivalent terms with MIT Mystery Hunt puzzles.

**Cross-hunt note:** The three Teammate Hunt 2021 puzzles (Itinerary, Single Elimination, Par Re-sing) show the characteristic profile of a major annual hunt: higher technical ambition, more complex mechanism stacking, longer expected solve time. Information Relay and Characters from Huntinality III demonstrate that accessible-hunt context does not preclude MIT/Elite intrinsic mechanism quality — this is itself a finding worth flagging in the paper's discussion of classification methodology.
