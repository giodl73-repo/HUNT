## [GM-01] — The Planets
**Source:** Crossword Labs (crosswordlabs.com/view/the-planets — real published puzzle)
**Answer:** Grid fill — PLUTO, NEPTUNE, JUPITER, MERCURY (Across); SATURN, VENUS, NEPTUNE, EARTH, MARS (Down)

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | Games Magazine | No hunt context required. Self-contained. Any solver with elementary science knowledge can complete this. |
| Mechanism | Wordplay | Standard crossword fill. Clue → answer via definitional match, constrained by crossing letters. No extraction step of any kind. |
| Domain Load | 1 | Solar system planet names are elementary-school curriculum. The Pluto clue requires knowing the 2006 IAU reclassification — the single moment where factual knowledge goes beyond rote recall, but this is now common general knowledge. |
| Aha Clarity | 1 | No aha. Each clue points to exactly one answer and the solve is a completion exercise. The "No longer officially considered a planet" Pluto clue is the strongest single entry — it implies rather than states the answer — but this is still a definitional prompt, not a mechanism discovery. |
| Paper Suitability | Yes | 9-entry small crossword. Fully reproducible in print format. Self-contained with answer key. |
| Quality Signal | 1 | Functional. The Pluto clue is the only entry demonstrating any constructorial ambition — it hints rather than defines, and benefits from the slight cultural tension around Pluto's demotion. All other clues are direct definitions: "The largest planet," "Closest planet to the sun," etc. No wordplay, no misdirection, no title hook. The puzzle does what it sets out to do and nothing more. |

**Katz note:** The "does every puzzle justify its slot" question collapses in the Games Magazine context because there is no slot — the puzzle exists as a standalone object, not a load-bearing unit in a larger structure. His framework still produces a useful negative: this puzzle does not justify existence in a hunt of any tier. It generates no information asymmetry, no progression, no payoff. As a standalone, it serves its purpose (educational crossword for a science magazine supplement), but that purpose is entirely outside the architectural territory his framework evaluates. What's revealing is that even by the minimal standards of Games Magazine construction, the puzzle offers only competence, not craft. A well-edited magazine would add a title hook — "Dwarf Planet?" would make the whole set slightly more interesting — but no such gesture appears.

**Snyder note:** The grid was assembled, not designed. Entry point analysis reveals no constructed entry — a solver simply fills the easiest crossing and proceeds. There is no forced deduction, no moment where understanding the constraint set produces a non-obvious conclusion. "Is there a puzzle here, or a procedure?" — this is entirely procedure: recall a planet name, confirm the letter count, fill the square. NEPTUNE appearing as both 4-Down and 5-Across is a duplication that a competent editor removes before publication. It signals the grid was built by fitting words into a template rather than designing around theme consistency. This is construction by accumulation, not construction by design.

**Young note:** The visual grammar is consistent — standard crossword format, numbered squares, across/down separation — but the grammar communicates nothing beyond its own convention. There is no moment of visual resolution, no layout choice that directs the eye toward something meaningful. The title "The Planets" is a label, not a frame: it tells you what the theme is rather than doing anything with it. For a puzzle in this format, the title is the only lever that costs nothing to pull, and it was left in the default position. The solver's experience is the experience of remembering things they already know. That is not inherently bad — crosswords serve a memory-exercise function that has genuine leisure value — but it is not a puzzle experience in any sense this panel's frameworks reward.

**AI construction signals:** N/A — sourced from a real user-published crossword on Crossword Labs. The NEPTUNE duplication is a human error, not an AI signal; it reflects casual construction rather than systematic generation.

**Use for ablation:** Yes — establishes the Games Magazine floor. Cleanest possible baseline: zero aha, zero domain load above elementary, zero construction novelty. Any puzzle rated above this control has demonstrated something this puzzle does not.

## [GM-02] — Weather
**Source:** Crossword Labs (crosswordlabs.com/view/weather — real published puzzle)
**Answer:** Grid fill — THUNDERSTORM, EVAPORATION, WEATHER, CONDENSATION, TORNADO (Across); GROUNDWATER, HURRICANE, CLIMATE, HUMIDITY, METEOROLOGISTS, FLOOD (Down)

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | Games Magazine | Educational general audience. Middle-school science vocabulary throughout. |
| Mechanism | Wordplay | Standard crossword fill. Clues are full-sentence definitions rather than fragments, but the mechanism remains pure crossword: one clue maps to one answer. |
| Domain Load | 1 | Middle-school earth science. Evaporation, condensation, humidity — these are taught in standard science curriculum by age 12. METEOROLOGISTS is the longest entry and requires knowing the professional title, which is mildly above the Planets tier but still general knowledge. |
| Aha Clarity | 1 | No aha as a mechanism. However: the self-referential quality of WEATHER appearing as both the puzzle's theme and the answer to 9-Across ("The state of the air at a certain time and place") is a mild moment of elegance. It does not function as a discovery — the solver fills it in as a definitional answer, not as a revelation — but it registers as a construction choice that was made intentionally. This is the closest any puzzle in this batch comes to a genuine aha without reaching one. |
| Paper Suitability | Yes | 11-entry crossword. Full text reproducible. Clue lengths are appropriate for the format. |
| Quality Signal | 2 | Marginally above standard for the tier. The clues are more elaborate than GM-01: full definitions rather than fragments, appropriate difficulty gradient (THUNDERSTORM at 1-Across is the obvious entry point; METEOROLOGISTS at 6-Down is the most demanding). The WEATHER self-reference indicates the constructor was thinking about theme coherence, not just filling a grid. Still firmly within Games Magazine parameters — no wordplay, no misdirection, no extraction — but showing signs of editorial intent. |

**Katz note:** Still no slot-justification problem because there is no slot. But by his "is the thematic coherence structural, not decorative?" test, this puzzle shows a sliver of structural integration absent from GM-01: the answer WEATHER in a puzzle themed around weather is not accidental, and whoever built this noticed it or chose it. That is a small structural choice, but it is the only structural choice in any of the three crosswords. The puzzle does not exploit it — there is no title hook, no framing that makes the WEATHER self-reference land — but the constructor left an opening that a more ambitious editor could have exploited.

**Snyder note:** The clues here are definitions, not deductions. A definitional clue gives you the answer directly; a deductive clue makes you earn it through elimination or inference. All eleven clues are definitional. However, the full-sentence form of these definitions — "The process of water changing into an invisible gas called water vapor" — is doing educational work that fragment clues don't do. This is a vocabulary-teaching crossword, not a puzzle in the Grandmaster sense, and within that pedagogical frame the construction is competent. METEOROLOGISTS as the longest entry is appropriate grid architecture: the hardest word earns the most crosses. That is a correct construction choice, even if it's also the obvious one.

**Young note:** The WEATHER clue is the only moment in these three crosswords that reaches for something beyond completion. "The state of the air at a certain time and place" — for an answer that is also the name of everything surrounding it — is the kind of small circularity that can make a solver smile. It does not make her smile here because the puzzle has not built toward it. It appears in the grid as 9-Across with no special visual weight, no title reference, no payoff design. The ingredient is present but the dish was not cooked. Still, in the context of this batch, it is the one moment she would point to as evidence that the Games Magazine tier can contain craft if someone chooses to exercise it.

**AI construction signals:** N/A — real user-published puzzle. The self-referential WEATHER clue reads as a human observation rather than an AI pattern; an AI constructing to format would be unlikely to prioritize that particular resonance.

**Use for ablation:** Yes — slightly stronger control than GM-01. Useful for testing whether reviewers can detect fine-grained quality differentiation within the same tier. The WEATHER self-reference is the single dimension on which this puzzle distinguishes itself.

## [GM-03] — Animals
**Source:** Crossword Labs (crosswordlabs.com/view/animals — real published puzzle)
**Answer:** Grid fill — ZEBRA, GIRAFFE, KANGAROO, CAT, PIGS (Across); CHEETAH, FROG, DOG, ELEPHANTS, LION, RHINO (Down)

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | Games Magazine | Children through adult general audience. Achievable by anyone with basic English and exposure to common animals. |
| Mechanism | Wordplay | Standard crossword fill at its most reduced form. Clues are single-sentence definitional prompts, answers are common animal names. |
| Domain Load | 1 | Common animal knowledge. No entry requires any knowledge beyond what a child learns from picture books. |
| Aha Clarity | 1 | No aha of any kind. "Man's best friend" → DOG is a crossword cliché; "Likes to chase mice" → CAT is a crossword cliché; "King of the jungle" → LION is a crossword cliché. The puzzle's clue set is assembled from stock phrases with no constructorial modification. |
| Paper Suitability | Yes | 11-entry small crossword. Fully reproducible. |
| Quality Signal | 1 | The weakest construction in the batch. Every clue is either a cliché ("Man's best friend," "King of the jungle") or a direct literal description ("Has black and white stripes," "Tallest land animal"). No entry demonstrates a construction choice that required judgment or invention. ELEPHANTS in the plural is irregular crossword form — the animal is ELEPHANT; the plural form suggests the grid forced the entry length rather than the constructor choosing the entry. |

**Katz note:** This puzzle does not justify existence even as a standalone. It occupies space on a page without offering anything a solver could not reproduce from memory in thirty seconds. His "would he want to solve this?" test returns a clear answer: no, not because it's too easy, but because it offers nothing — no misdirection, no craft, no payoff. The test that makes his framework interesting here is whether a puzzle with no hunt slot can still be assessed for what it gives the solver. The answer is yes, and this puzzle gives nothing. It is a vocabulary list with a grid.

**Snyder note:** ELEPHANTS plural signals a grid that was filled by constraint rather than designed with intent. When a constructor reaches for the plural form of an animal to meet a letter-count requirement, that is evidence the grid architecture was not planned around its entries. The entry point — presumably ZEBRA at 1-Across given its position — is not constructed: it's the obvious starting point by position, not by deductive force. This puzzle has no forced deductions at all. A solver could fill it in any order without a single moment of logical compulsion. That is the diagnostic of a procedure, not a puzzle.

**Young note:** "Man's best friend" for DOG is not a clue; it is a placeholder. Any constructor who hands a tester "Man's best friend" as a finished clue has not finished the work. The same applies to "King of the jungle" for LION. These are the puzzle equivalents of clip art: recognizable, functional, communicating nothing. She would send this back with a note that stock phrases are not clues — they are proof that the constructor stopped before starting. In the context of what Games Magazine actually publishes (or did publish at its quality peak), this crossword would not have cleared editorial review. It is below the tier's own ceiling.

**AI construction signals:** N/A — real user-published puzzle. The presence of stock clichés ("Man's best friend," "King of the jungle") is characteristic of human casual construction, not AI generation. AI crossword construction typically produces more varied clue styles; the repetition of stock phrases here reads as human convenience rather than systematic generation.

**Use for ablation:** Yes — absolute floor of the corpus. Every metric scores at minimum. Useful as the clearest possible anchor for the Games Magazine baseline; any puzzle that cannot be distinguished from this control has failed to demonstrate construction above floor level.

## [GM-04] — Logic Grid (Simple)
**Source:** Canonical Games Magazine format (constructed as representative example)
**Answer:** Alice — Top floor / Cat; Carol — Middle floor / Dog; Bob — Ground floor / Fish

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | Games Magazine | Classic logic grid format. No domain knowledge. Self-contained. The format is recognizable to any adult puzzle reader. |
| Mechanism | Logic-Constraint | Pure constraint satisfaction: read clues, eliminate impossibilities, arrive at unique assignment. No knowledge required; no extraction step. Completion is the goal. |
| Domain Load | 1 | No domain knowledge whatsoever. People, floors, and pets are all generic tokens. |
| Aha Clarity | 1 | No aha in the discovery sense. The mechanism is procedural elimination. Clue 3 ("Bob lives directly below the person with the dog") is the load-bearing constraint — it forces positional reasoning rather than pure binary elimination — and represents the one step where a solver must track a relational constraint rather than mark a cell. But this is a technique, not an insight. |
| Paper Suitability | Yes | Fully reproducible in print: grid, clues, solution. Standard format. |
| Quality Signal | 2 | Competent construction for the format. Five clues for a 3×3×2 grid (three people, three floors, two pets would be six variables — this is actually 3-person × 2-attribute at 3 levels, giving nine cells and requiring enough clues to force uniqueness). The solution is verifiable as unique. Clue 3 introduces a relational ("directly below") constraint that elevates this above pure binary elimination. The format is correctly instantiated. |

**Katz note:** The logic grid as a format exists outside his structural framework in a revealing way. A logic grid has no slot to justify, no meta position, no hunt context — it exists as a pure deductive exercise. What interests him here is that the format is self-contained by design: unlike a crossword, it explicitly tells you when you're done (the grid is uniquely filled), and the difficulty is entirely within the constraint system. The 3×3×2 scale is correctly calibrated for a single page: solvable in 5–10 minutes by a competent solver, not trivial, not overwhelming. The "does every puzzle justify its slot" question translates in standalone context to "does this puzzle earn the solver's time?" At 3×3×2 with five clues, this just barely does.

**Snyder note:** The critical construction question for any logic puzzle is uniqueness of solution — is there exactly one valid assignment, derivable without guessing? For this grid, Clue 3 (the relational constraint) is doing the essential work. Without it, or with a weaker substitution, the puzzle likely has multiple solutions. The constructor understood what the load-bearing clue needed to be and placed it. Clue 5 ("Alice and the person with the dog do not live on the same floor") is partially redundant with what Clue 2 and Clue 1 force, but redundancy in a small grid is acceptable — it accelerates the solve without breaking the deductive chain. The construction is finished; not elegant, but finished.

**Young note:** The experience of solving a logic grid is the experience of narrowing a space of possibilities until only one remains. At this scale (three people, two attributes), that narrowing is brief and the satisfaction is mild — more "done" than "delighted." The format promises resolution and delivers it. The names Alice, Carol, Bob are neutral tokens; they don't create any experiential texture. The puzzle exists entirely as procedure. For its format and purpose, this is correct: a logic grid in a general-audience magazine should be completable without frustration, and this one is. The question of whether Games Magazine puzzles can achieve Aha Clarity 2 is answered differently here than in the crosswords: the format structurally precludes an aha because the mechanism is stated explicitly (read the clues, fill the grid). There is no discovery moment available by design.

**AI construction signals:** Several elements suggest AI or template-based construction rather than human construction from scratch. (1) The names — Alice, Bob, Carol — are the canonical placeholder names from computer science and logic textbooks. A human constructing a character logic grid typically picks names with more personality or thematic resonance. (2) The attributes (floors: Ground/Middle/Top; pets: Cat/Dog/Fish) are the simplest possible instantiation of each category — no unusual entries, no thematic coloring. (3) The five clues follow a recognizable template pattern: one negative placement, one positive attribute-location, one relational, one negative attribute, one negative co-location. This is a standard five-clue structure for a 3×3 grid that appears in many puzzle-generating templates. (4) The solution (Alice=Top/Cat, Carol=Middle/Dog, Bob=Ground/Fish) is reachable without any backtracking, which is consistent with a generated puzzle designed to have a clean forward-deduction path. None of these signals are definitive, but the combination suggests construction from a template or generation system rather than from creative intent.

**Use for ablation:** Yes — useful as the non-wordplay control in the Games Magazine tier. Establishes that constraint-satisfaction puzzles at this scale also sit firmly at Games Magazine level. Contrasts with logic-heavy hunt puzzles (e.g., a Nikoli-style puzzle with a theme-integrated mechanism) to show what Logic-Constraint mechanism looks like at different tiers.

## [GM-05] — Word Search with Hidden Message
**Source:** Canonical Games Magazine format (constructed as representative example)
**Answer:** "SECRET MESSAGE" (the two-word phrase spelled by remaining letters)

| Dimension | Classification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Hunt Level | Games Magazine | Standard word search format with a secondary extraction layer. The hidden message is announced to the solver in the instructions — there is no discovery. |
| Mechanism | Hybrid (Wordplay + Knowledge-extraction) | Word search (find and mark 8 words) → secondary extraction (remaining letters spell a phrase). Two steps, but neither step requires deduction or discovery — both are procedural. |
| Domain Load | 1 | No domain knowledge. The eight words (PUZZLE, HUNT, CLUE, SOLVE, GAME, WORD, FIND, PLAY) are all common English vocabulary. |
| Aha Clarity | 1 | The hidden-message mechanism is disclosed to the solver in the instructions: "The remaining letters, read left to right, top to bottom, spell a two-word phrase." This is not an aha; it is a procedure. A genuine aha would be discovering that the remaining letters form something meaningful — which requires the mechanism to be hidden until the extraction moment. Here it is front-loaded. The puzzle scores Aha Clarity 1 despite having an extraction step because the extraction is announced rather than discovered. |
| Paper Suitability | Yes | 6×8 letter grid, reproducible in markdown or print. Standard format. |
| Quality Signal | 1 | Significant construction problem: the source document acknowledges that the remaining letters spell "ABSFJGHINO ABIJKQD" — which is not "SECRET MESSAGE." This means the puzzle as presented is internally inconsistent; the stated answer does not follow from the stated grid. A real word search with hidden message requires the constructor to design the grid so that after removing the eight target words, the remaining letters form the desired phrase. That design work was not completed. This is the only puzzle in the batch with a demonstrable construction error. |

**Katz note:** The "hidden message" word search is a Games Magazine staple, and its appeal is real: the solver has two simultaneous tasks (find the words, track the leftovers), and the second task produces a payoff. In a well-constructed example of this format, the payoff is earned — the constructor placed both the words and the filler letters with the second message in mind, and the solver experiences a mild revelation when the phrase assembles. None of that is present here. The construction error (stated remaining letters do not spell the stated answer) means the puzzle fails at its own announced goal. For a standalone puzzle with no other structural function, failing to deliver the stated payoff is the one disqualifying failure mode. The format is legitimate; this instance does not instantiate it correctly.

**Snyder note:** The construction failure here is diagnostic. A proper hidden-message word search requires the constructor to work backward from the desired message: place the message letters first, then arrange the target words so that precisely those letters are not consumed by the target words. This is nontrivial design work for a small grid. The grid as presented was apparently built by placing the words first and then noting that the remaining letters do not form the intended phrase. The source document itself flags this ("this would be arranged by the constructor to spell something meaningful") — which is an acknowledgment that the construction step was not completed. In his framework, this puzzle was not finished. The question is not whether it's easy; the question is whether it does what it claims to do. It does not.

**Young note:** The hidden-message format has a genuine experiential payoff in a well-made instance: you are doing one thing (finding words) while unknowingly building another thing (assembling a secret), and when the second thing reveals itself you experience a small surprise. That structure requires the mechanism to be partially hidden from the solver — you know a message exists, but you discover what it says through the act of solving. Here, because the construction was not completed and the answer does not follow from the grid, that experience is unavailable. The puzzle promises a resolution it cannot deliver. She would not accept this in any context — not because it's too easy, not because it lacks an aha, but because the basic contract ("solve this, get that") is broken.

**AI construction signals:** Multiple strong signals of AI or template-based construction. (1) The eight target words (PUZZLE, HUNT, CLUE, SOLVE, GAME, WORD, FIND, PLAY) form a perfectly thematic set with no unusual or unexpected entries — they are the obvious words for a puzzle-about-puzzles word search, assembled from the most salient category members. Human constructors often include one or two less obvious entries to prevent the list from feeling algorithmic. (2) The stated answer ("SECRET MESSAGE") is a placeholder that names the format element rather than providing actual content. A human constructor building this format would construct toward a meaningful, specific, thematic message — not a meta-label for the mechanism itself. (3) The construction error (remaining letters do not spell the answer) is the strongest signal: AI generation of word search grids frequently produces technically valid letter grids without performing the backward-design step required to embed a specific message. The system generated the grid and announced the format's promise without verifying that the promise was kept. (4) The grid dimensions (6 rows × 8 columns) are a standard template size. These signals taken together strongly suggest AI generation, specifically a system that could instantiate the format description but did not complete the backward-design verification step.

**Use for ablation:** Conditional — the construction error limits its usefulness as a clean control. Useful for one specific test: whether reviewers can detect that an announced extraction step is not actually present (format promises mechanism, mechanism is not instantiated). That is a detection task with clear right/wrong answers, which makes it valuable for measuring reviewer reliability. Not useful as a clean example of the Games Magazine word search format; a correctly constructed instance would be needed for that.

## Panel Synthesis: What Hunt-Design Frameworks Reveal About the Games Magazine Tier

Applying hunt-caliber analysis tools to Games Magazine puzzles produces an unexpected result: the frameworks don't break down — they clarify. The rubric was designed for puzzles where aha, mechanism, and extraction are the primary analytical categories, and the Games Magazine tier scores uniformly at the floor of all three dimensions. That uniformity is itself informative. It means the tier is defined not by variation within those dimensions but by their near-total absence, and applying the full framework to these puzzles names that absence precisely.

The most significant finding concerns Aha Clarity. The panel initially assumed that Games Magazine puzzles simply score Aha Clarity 1 across the board — that the tier is characterized by the absence of a discovery moment. That is true, but the panel found something subtler: the Games Magazine tier is defined by procedural completion rather than by a different kind of aha. A hunt puzzle scores Aha Clarity 3 when the mechanism IS the aha — when understanding what the puzzle is asking is the primary barrier. A Games Magazine puzzle inverts this: the solver is told exactly what to do (fill the crossword, find the words, eliminate the impossibilities), and the barrier is only execution. The tier is not aha-impoverished by accident; completion-as-goal is the affirmative design choice. The framework exposes this as a choice, not a deficiency.

The Katz framework — built entirely around structural function within a hunt — proves useful negatively. The question "does every puzzle justify its slot?" becomes "does every puzzle justify its existence as a standalone?" and the answer reveals something about what puzzles are for when they exist outside competitive structure. GM-01 and GM-03 fail this test even by the standards of their own tier: they are vocabulary lists with grid wrappers, and they would not clear editorial review at a well-run general-audience magazine. GM-02 barely passes by virtue of the WEATHER self-reference. GM-04 passes because the logic grid format has a self-contained integrity: it delivers exactly what it promises, at appropriate scale. GM-05 fails because it is the only puzzle in the batch that does not deliver what it promises. Katz's structural framework, applied to standalone puzzles, reduces to a simpler test: does the puzzle earn the solver's time? Three of five puzzles in this batch score marginally to the negative.

The Snyder framework — demanding designed entry points, load-bearing constraints, and finished construction — maps directly onto the one demonstrable construction error (GM-05's incomplete backward design) and onto GM-03's ELEPHANTS plural (grid filled by constraint rather than designed by intent). These signals are invisible to a casual reader and immediately legible to a construction-literate reviewer. This has implications for the ablation study: Snyder's diagnostic precision is most valuable for detecting not tier misclassification but construction quality within a tier. The difference between GM-01 (Quality 1) and GM-02 (Quality 2) is a Snyder-grade observation; a solver would not experience it.

The Young framework — asking whether the solver inhabits a world, whether the answer is inevitable, whether the theme dissolves at extraction — produces the most pointed critique of the tier. The crosswords in this batch are all label-and-fill: the title names a category, the clues name category members, the grid arranges the names. There is no world to inhabit, no moment of visual resolution, no answer that reframes the experience. The logic grid (GM-04) at least produces a clean resolution — the grid fills, the assignment is confirmed, the solver knows they are done. The word search (GM-05) promises a resolution and fails to deliver one. By her criteria, only the logic grid earns its conclusion.

The AI construction signals analysis produced the most useful ancillary finding. GM-04 and GM-05, constructed as canonical format examples, show distinguishable AI/template signals despite being simple puzzles: placeholder names, obvious-set vocabulary, template-pattern clue structure, and in GM-05's case a critical backward-design failure that is characteristic of generative systems that can describe a format without verifying its instantiation. This is directly relevant to the ablation study: the panel can distinguish AI-template construction from casual human construction even at the Games Magazine floor, and the signals are specific enough to be operationalized as reviewer criteria.

The collective finding is this: the Games Magazine tier is not the bottom of a continuum that extends upward through increasing aha complexity. It is a different design paradigm in which completion, not discovery, is the goal, and in which the solver's relationship to the puzzle is execution rather than investigation. Hunt-design frameworks applied to this tier do not find bad versions of hunt puzzles; they find a different thing entirely. The frameworks are useful precisely because they name the difference with precision. What the ablation study needs from this batch is not to rate these puzzles against hunt-tier standards — they are correctly scored at the floor — but to confirm that the panel can apply the same rubric across paradigms without inflating scores for tier-appropriate completion or deflating them for absence of mechanisms the tier does not claim to provide. All five puzzles in this batch are correctly classified as Games Magazine tier. Two of them (GM-01 and GM-03) would fail to clear editorial review even within the tier. One (GM-05) has a construction error. That is an honest accounting.
