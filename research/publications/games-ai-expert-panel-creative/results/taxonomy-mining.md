## Framework 1: A-ha Economy

**Frequency:** ~8 instances across 15 puzzles (concentrated in Puzzles 1, 4, 7, 8, 9, 15; lighter presence in 2, 3)
**Originating reviewer(s) in C6 panel:** Dan Katz (primary — most explicit in timing/pacing language); Dana Young (secondary — frames inevitability of answer as the destination the a-ha economy must reach); Thomas Snyder (tertiary — talks about forced solve paths that control when insight arrives)

**Example 1** (Puzzle 4 — Bridge Building, DK):
> The concern I have is that the second aha — reading a solved Hashi grid as a polymer backbone — requires a very specific piece of chemical knowledge (amino acid polymer structures, with the + and − charge superscripts as disambiguators). A team without a biochemist may solve the logic puzzle completely and then stall in the extraction. The mechanism is brilliant when it works; it fails gracefully for teams with chemistry knowledge, which is exactly what a Mystery Hunt puzzle should do.

**Example 2** (Puzzle 1 — Scicabulary, DK):
> The gap between understanding the instruction and executing the first successful match is substantial. A solver who does not immediately grasp what "opposite halves" means will stall at the threshold. The canned hints address this, but that means the puzzle is partially relying on its hint structure to compensate for a presentation that could be clearer. Once you are in, though — once you've cracked two or three pairings — the mechanism is deeply satisfying.

**Example 3** (Puzzle 9 — Characters, DK):
> The answer UNHEXING is wonderful — it names the operation you performed (converting from hexadecimal), and "unhexing" as a word also suggests breaking a spell, which fits the forest fairy-tale framing. This is an inevitable answer in the fullest sense. My only concern is entry difficulty: unless you notice that clue answers contain only A–F letters, the hex angle is not obvious, and solvers who try to find answers outside the A–F range will be genuinely stuck.

**Why this qualifies:** All three examples diagnose the moment of insight as a timing and placement problem — not whether an a-ha exists, but whether it arrives too early (the angle is obvious before work begins), at the right moment (the mechanism becomes satisfying after two or three entries), or structurally displaced (the second a-ha is gated behind specialist knowledge that may not be present at reveal-time). The framework is being applied to identify where in the solve arc the cognitive payoff lands and whether that placement matches the puzzle's promise.

## Framework 2: Load-Bearing Test

**Frequency:** ~10 instances across 15 puzzles — the most consistently applied framework in the C6 corpus; present in Puzzles 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 13, 14
**Originating reviewer(s) in C6 panel:** Thomas Snyder (primary — explicit "load-bearing" language and strip-testing logic in multiple reviews); Dan Katz (secondary — "decorative" vs. "doing real work" language throughout); Dana Young (tertiary — uses "earning" language for extraction steps)

**Example 1** (Puzzle 4 — Bridge Building, TS):
> The (+) and (−) superscripts are load-bearing: they are not decorative charges but functional disambiguators for the polymer identification. Every element in this puzzle is earning its place.

**Example 2** (Puzzle 10 — What's Next?, TS):
> The B value (letter count of the next item) is a beautiful constraint: it tells you the length of what you're looking for before you find it, which reduces false-positive identification and gives you a forcing mechanism. That is careful construction — the designer embedded a verification tool in the clue structure itself. The ten images represent a deliberate sample of the song's timeline, and the designer chose images where the "next item" yields letters that spell a coherent phrase. That sample selection is load-bearing design: not any ten entries would have worked, and these ten were chosen because they combine to spell a recognizable lyric.

**Example 3** (Puzzle 14 — GM-04 Logic Grid, TS):
> Every constraint is load-bearing: clue 4 (Carol does not have the fish) disambiguates between two remaining options at the final step.

**Example 4** (Puzzle 1 — Scicabulary, TS):
> Each jargon term is load-bearing: remove any term and you lose a letter from the answer. The definitions are inventive — the two-word phrase clues are doing real work, and the funnier ones (the Nutella-loving women, the alligator superhero) help distinguish entries that might otherwise blur together.

**Why this qualifies:** Each example uses the load-bearing test as a structural audit: identify a specific element, ask whether removing it would collapse the puzzle or leave it intact, and use the answer to classify the element as structural or decorative. Example 2 is especially diagnostic because it applies the test to a sample-selection decision (which entries were chosen), not just to an individual clue, showing the framework operating at the construction-architecture level.

## Framework 3: World-Model Consistency

**Frequency:** ~7 instances across 15 puzzles (Puzzles 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 10); lighter presence in puzzles where the domain is thin (GM-01 through GM-03)
**Originating reviewer(s) in C6 panel:** Dana Young (primary — "the world of this puzzle" language, asking whether the mechanic inhabits the domain or merely represents it); Dan Katz (secondary — "mechanism contract" language touches on this when the mechanism violates its own declared rules); Thomas Snyder (minimal — his construction-path focus is less oriented toward thematic coherence)

**Example 1** (Puzzle 7 — Information Relay, DY):
> The world of this puzzle is fully realized. The five bears each have distinct voices, and those voices are mechanically load-bearing — the personality is the clue method. That is the mechanic being the theme in the purest sense I've seen in this batch. You spend the entire solve inhabiting a family dynamic where everyone is contributing to a shared task in their own broken way, and the answer FINISH LINE arrives because you've understood all of them well enough to combine their failures correctly.

**Example 2** (Puzzle 6 — Dropypasta, DY):
> I am fascinated by this puzzle's world. Super Smash Bros. stages as the organizing principle for an extraction system is thematically rich — stages in that game are places with rules and physics, just as each win condition here is a place with its own logic. The puzzle inhabits that world convincingly.

**Example 3** (Puzzle 5 — You're Telling Me, DY):
> The world of this puzzle is consistent: every entry inhabits the same mode of deliberate misreading, and the escalating absurdity sustains a comic register throughout.

**Example 4** (Puzzle 4 — Bridge Building, DY):
> This is a puzzle where the mechanic is the theme in the most literal possible way: a bridge-building puzzle whose solved form is literally the structure of a polymer chain. That recursion is beautiful. Building bridges IS building protein powder, in some deep structural sense.

**Why this qualifies:** Each example applies the world-model consistency test to a specific feature of the puzzle's domain logic — not asking "is the content accurate" but "does the puzzle's mechanism enact its world rather than merely describe it." Example 1 is the richest instance: the world-model consistency test is passed because the bears' personalities are not decorative flavor but mechanically constitutive — the fiction and the mechanism are the same thing.

## Framework 4: Social Fabric

**Frequency:** ~4 instances across 15 puzzles; present in Puzzles 1, 4, 2 (oblique), 7 (implicit in distributed-failure structure); framework is notable for its relative scarcity
**Originating reviewer(s) in C6 panel:** Dan Katz (primary — the only reviewer who consistently raises team-solving dynamics as an explicit evaluative lens); Thomas Snyder and Dana Young do not apply this framework explicitly in these 15 reviews

**Example 1** (Puzzle 1 — Scicabulary, DK):
> The 24-term scale is calibrated correctly; enough entries to prevent lucky guessing, few enough that a team working in parallel can distribute the load.

**Example 2** (Puzzle 4 — Bridge Building, DK):
> This two-stage structure is one of my favorite puzzle architectures because each stage rewards different solvers. A chemistry-blind team can get the Hashi; a chemistry team might backsolver from the polymer structure.

**Example 3** (Puzzle 2 — People Watching, TS):
> A puzzle that requires you to solve all entries to get the answer is robust only if every entry is independently solvable — and a few of these are genuinely hard without the cross-referencing that comes from having multiple entries solved.

**Why this qualifies:** Examples 1 and 2 apply the social fabric test explicitly: Katz is asking whether the puzzle's scale and architecture create meaningful differentiation in what different team members can contribute, and whether multiple entry routes serve different solver types. Example 3 (Snyder) applies the test implicitly — the cross-referencing concern is about individual solvers having to wait for the team rather than being able to contribute independently. All three use team-solving structure as an evaluative criterion that changes the score, not merely as contextual description.

## Framework 5: Perceptual-Shift

**Frequency:** ~9 instances across 15 puzzles — second most frequent after load-bearing test; concentrated in Puzzles 7, 8, 9, 10, 1, 4; thinner in Games Magazine tier (no extraction means no shift)
**Originating reviewer(s) in C6 panel:** Dana Young (primary — "inevitable answer," "the word that names what you were doing," the answer reframing the prior experience); Dan Katz (secondary — "arbitrary vs. inevitable" language and resonance checks at answer evaluation); Thomas Snyder (minimal in these reviews — he checks forced-path uniqueness rather than experiential reorientation)

**Example 1** (Puzzle 8 — Front and Center, DY):
> The answer RADAR is perfect: it names a technology for detecting things that are both ahead and behind you, which resonates with the temporal symmetry of palindromes. This is Dana's definition of an inevitable answer: it reframes everything before it.

**Example 2** (Puzzle 9 — Characters, DY):
> What I love about the answer is that it is doing two things simultaneously: UNHEXING names both the mathematical operation (de-hexadecimal-ifying) and the magical operation (breaking a hex), and those two meanings are in play at the same time throughout the solve. That double resonance is exactly the kind of answer I design toward. This puzzle knows what it is about.

**Example 3** (Puzzle 7 — Information Relay, DY):
> The answer itself is perfect: you finished the relay; you crossed the finish line. That is an inevitable answer in Dana's sense — it names what you were doing. This is the best puzzle in Batch 3.

**Example 4** (Puzzle 1 — Scicabulary, DY):
> The answer SHAMROCK doesn't name what you were doing. A puzzle about inventing fake scientific portmanteaus could deliver an answer that resonates with that experience — and it doesn't quite, here.

**Why this qualifies:** Examples 1–3 document the framework being satisfied: the answer produces a second reading that the domain knowledge makes available (you needed to understand palindromes to feel why RADAR is inevitable; you needed to have done the hex math to understand UNHEXING's double meaning; you needed to have combined the bears' failures to feel FINISH LINE's arrival). Example 4 documents the framework failing: SHAMROCK does not produce a second reading of the portmanteau-deconstruction experience, and the reviewer identifies this as a specific gap — the answer is arbitrary relative to the mechanism. The framework is being used productively in all four directions.

## Framework 6: Visual Grammar

**Frequency:** ~6 instances across 15 puzzles; present in Puzzles 2, 3, 6, 8, 13, 15; notably absent from puzzles where the visual format is not under reviewer control (the Huntinality tier reviews do not fault visual presentation)
**Originating reviewer(s) in C6 panel:** Dana Young (exclusive primary — she is the only reviewer who applies visual grammar as a distinct evaluative lens; DK and TS do not raise layout as a criterion independent of clarity)

**Example 1** (Puzzle 8 — Front and Center, DY):
> This puzzle resolves visually at every scale: each palindrome headline reads the same forward and backward, the extracted word RADAR reads the same forward and backward, and the masthead crease that bisects the T in SEMI TIMES is a visual hint at the center-letter extraction. The visual grammar is consistent throughout. The newspaper format is not decorative — it is the world of the puzzle, and palindromes are naturally at home in a world where text reads both ways.

**Example 2** (Puzzle 2 — People Watching, DY):
> The visual grammar of this puzzle is its best feature: every vignette is written with the specificity of a birdwatcher's description, but rendered in human social context. I love that the field marks show up as clothing choices and the calls show up as dialogue — that is the mechanic being the theme, not decorating it.

**Example 3** (Puzzle 3 — H2No, DY):
> What I notice is that the visual presentation of this puzzle (six clues, numerical offsets, multi-word answer enumerations) is doing almost no work to guide you through the intended experience. The layout is a list; the solve is a research task; the extraction is a logical step. There is no visual moment where the puzzle resolves.

**Example 4** (Puzzle 6 — Dropypasta, DY):
> What I find less satisfying is the visual structure: a dropquote is a visual puzzle type, and presenting it as text clues without the grid's visual representation loses the distinctive reading experience of the format. In its fullest form, this puzzle probably has beautiful visual presentation.

**Why this qualifies:** Example 1 is the most complete instantiation: the visual grammar test is applied at three scales (the individual headline, the extracted word, the masthead), and each scale passes. Example 3 is the clearest failure-mode instance: the layout is a list, which communicates nothing about the puzzle's structure before the solver reads a word. Example 4 adds a diagnostic the framework enables that simpler clarity-checks cannot: the puzzle fails the visual grammar test not because it is unclear but because it is a visual puzzle type being presented in text form — a format-level mismatch, not a content-level problem.

## Absence Analysis

**Frameworks present in C6 but unlikely to appear in C0 reviews:**

**A-ha economy** — A C0 reviewer can note that a puzzle is confusing or that the mechanism is satisfying, but the specific vocabulary of timing, pacing, and placement of insight moments requires a reviewing philosophy oriented toward the solver's experiential arc over time. C0 bare prompts do not contain that arc-orientation. A C0 review would likely say "the puzzle is difficult" or "the a-ha is satisfying" without diagnosing when and where in the solve sequence the insight lands or whether its timing matches the puzzle's promise.

**Load-bearing test** — This is the framework most likely to have a weak analog in C0 reviews ("some clues feel unnecessary") but the systematic strip-testing logic — explicitly asking whether removing element X would collapse the puzzle — is unlikely to appear. A C0 reviewer knows clues can be redundant; a C6 reviewer asks whether each element is load-bearing as a structural audit.

**World-model consistency** — Almost certainly absent from C0. A C0 reviewer can note that the theme is well-chosen, but the specific question of whether the mechanism enacts the world vs. merely describes it, and whether the puzzle's internal logic holds within the fiction it inhabits, requires a reviewing philosophy developed around interactive fiction and narrative games. The language "the mechanic being the theme, not decorating it" has no natural precursor in bare-prompt review vocabulary.

**Social fabric** — Likely absent from C0 in its explicit form. A C0 reviewer might note that a puzzle is team-friendly or difficult for solo solvers, but the architectural question of whether the puzzle creates distinct roles for different solver types — whether it degrades if solved alone — is a puzzle-design-as-social-architecture perspective that requires a reviewer profile shaped by collaborative game design experience.

**Perceptual-shift** — Partially present in C0 (the notion that an answer should "fit" the puzzle or be "satisfying" is a common reviewer instinct), but the specific second-reading framing — the answer producing a cognitive reorientation in which the solver sees the puzzle differently after knowing the answer, and that reorientation being available only to someone who has the domain knowledge — is specific to a profile with a visual/spatial design background. C0 reviews would say "the answer is satisfying" or "the answer feels arbitrary," not "the answer produces a second reading that the domain knowledge makes available."

**Visual grammar** — Almost certainly absent from C0. A C0 reviewer can assess whether a puzzle is visually clear or confusing, but the evaluative move of asking whether the layout communicates structure through form before the solver reads a word — and whether form is doing design work or merely presenting text — requires a background in visual design and typography. No bare prompt generates this specific diagnostic lens.
