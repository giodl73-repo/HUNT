# Review: Orientation Before Expertise: A Unified Tier Model of Creative Quality in AI Authoring and Reviewing
**Reviewer:** Pablo Gervás | **Expertise:** Computational creativity, narrative generation, cognitive models of creativity | **Round:** 1

## Scores (1-4)
Novelty: 2 | Rigor: 3 | Clarity: 3 | Significance: 3 | **Overall: 2.8**

## Overall Assessment
The empirical findings are credible and the cross-role symmetry is a meaningful observation. However, the paper's central theoretical claim — that the tier model is a generalizable architecture of creative quality rather than a description of the specific testbed in which it was measured — is not adequately supported. The paper demonstrates the pattern in puzzle-hunt design across two roles; it does not demonstrate that the pattern is generated by the tier model's proposed mechanism (orientation as the activation condition for all downstream processing) rather than by domain-specific features of puzzle-hunt evaluation. Without predictive power beyond the training data, the tier model is an empirical description, not a theoretical contribution. The OLE formula is useful but its justification is circular.

## Strengths
1. **The leave-one-out ablation design is the right methodology for this question.** Measuring the marginal contribution of each component by removal, rather than by addition, avoids the confound of component ordering in sequential construction. The Phase 2 results (ranking by drop magnitude) provide a clean tier separation.
2. **The reconstruction curve (Phase 3) provides independent confirmation of the tier structure.** The threshold crossing at exactly R3 (T1+T4+T5 = 35.7) is the paper's most persuasive evidence that the three tiers are not post-hoc — the reconstruction confirms the ablation tier assignments from the opposite direction.
3. **The designer lens comparison (§4.4) is theoretically interesting.** Young's perceptual lens producing a larger cross-role gain than Katz's operational lens, interpreted through the tier model as Tier 1 substituting for blocked Tier 3, is a non-obvious prediction that the data confirm. This is the paper's strongest evidence of predictive rather than descriptive tier behavior.
4. **The boundary condition specification (§5.3) is appropriately narrow.** Identifying formal logic and mathematical proof as domains where Tier 1 is domain-defined rather than receiver-defined is a correct and important constraint.

## P1 — Blocking Issues

1. **The tier model has no predictive power beyond the testbed in which it was derived.** This is the central theoretical problem. The paper claims the tier model is "a generalizable architecture" but the only cross-domain "predictions" in §5.2 are derived by applying the tier model to new domains analogically — by labeling the new domain's components as Tier 1, 2, or 3 according to whether they resemble the puzzle-hunt tier assignments. This is not prediction; it is relabeling. A genuine predictive test would be: given the tier model, what score would a marketing-copy prompt achieve under each tier condition? The paper explicitly identifies this as future work (§7), which means the generalization claim in the current paper is unsupported. The paper should describe the generalization as a prediction framework, not as a validated finding. Every claim in the abstract, introduction, and conclusion that asserts universality beyond puzzle-hunt design must be qualified accordingly.

2. **The mechanism by which orientation "activates" downstream processing is unexplained.** The paper's causal account (§6.2) asserts that "the orientation sentence activates the capacity that is most latent" in language models, but provides no mechanistic account of what "activation" means computationally. Is the orientation sentence functioning as a context-setting frame that shifts the model's output distribution? Is it activating a different attention pattern over the domain content? Is it functioning as a prior constraint that filters subsequent generation? The paper gestures at "theory of the other" from developmental psychology but does not connect this to any computational account of how transformer-based language models process receiver-experience framing differently from domain-identity framing. This lacuna matters because the mechanism determines whether OLE generalizes: if orientation works by activating a specific training-corpus pattern, it may not generalize beyond domains where "artistic" or "receiver-oriented" associations are strongly represented in the training data. The paper should either propose a computational mechanism or be clear that the mechanism is unknown.

## P2 — Important Issues

1. **The quality thresholds (35/45 for authoring, implicit threshold for reviewing) are critical to the tier structure but are not justified.** The tier designation of Tier 2 as "load-bearing" depends entirely on whether the threshold is at 35/45. If the threshold were 30/45, then T1 alone (T1 = 31.3 in isolation, R1 = 28.3 in reconstruction) would be close to sufficient, and the Tier 2 designation would be weakened. If the threshold were 38/45, T1+T2 alone would not cross it. The paper should justify the threshold location. One option: if "publishable puzzle quality" is defined independently of the study (e.g., by a pre-registered external standard), state that. If it was set post-hoc based on the data distribution, acknowledge that this introduces circularity into the tier assignments.

2. **The C5 (lens-only) degradation below baseline is over-interpreted as a general principle.** The paper states that "domain specificity without orientation is not merely less helpful than orientation — it actively degrades review quality." This conclusion follows if the C5 result is reliable and not a measurement artifact, but the paper reports only aggregate scores without confidence intervals, inter-rater reliability, or replication evidence. A single condition falling 2.0 points below a bare baseline (21.7 vs. 23.7 on a 30-point scale) is a small absolute difference. If the measurement noise is comparable to this difference, the "below baseline" conclusion is not statistically supported. The paper should report measurement uncertainty.

## Recommendation
Accept with major revisions. The empirical evidence is real and the methodology is sound, but the theoretical claims exceed what the data support. The generalization must be reframed as prediction rather than finding. The mechanism must be acknowledged as unknown or speculatively proposed. Measurement uncertainty must be reported.
