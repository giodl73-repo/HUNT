# Review: Orientation Before Expertise: A Unified Tier Model of Creative Quality in AI Authoring and Reviewing
**Reviewer:** Takashi Ikegami | **Expertise:** Complex systems, artificial life, emergence in creative systems | **Round:** 1

## Scores (1-4)
Novelty: 3 | Rigor: 2 | Clarity: 3 | Significance: 3 | **Overall: 2.8**

## Overall Assessment
The paper documents an interesting asymmetry in how prompt components contribute to creative quality, and the cross-role symmetry finding suggests something structurally real is being measured. However, the paper treats the tier structure as a prescriptive design framework while leaving open the more interesting question of whether the hierarchy is an emergent property of the creative system or an artifact of the evaluation rubric. The computational meaning of "orientation" is entirely unspecified, which makes it impossible to assess whether OLE is a discovery about how creative AI systems work or a prompt-engineering trick that happens to align with how the evaluation rubric was constructed.

## Strengths
1. **The non-monotonicity result (C7 < C6; Tier 3 without Tier 1 < baseline) is the paper's most interesting finding from a complex systems perspective.** Adding more structured information to a system producing worse output is exactly the kind of counterintuitive behavior that indicates a non-linear coupling between components — the system has attractors, and injecting domain-specific information without the orientation component pulls it into the wrong basin. This is not a failure of the components; it is evidence that the components interact non-linearly, which is the beginning of a systems account.
2. **The distinction between orientation as irreplaceable and expertise as substitutable captures something real about how complex creative systems self-organize.** A creative system without a receiver-orientation constraint optimizes for whatever local signal is available (domain format, genre conventions, surface grammar) — the same dynamics observed in evolutionary systems without fitness constraints that reflect environmental coupling. The catastrophic failure at L-T1 (12.3 from 41.7) is analogous to a complex system losing its environmental coupling: it continues to generate structure, but structure uncoupled from the selection environment.
3. **The Dreyfus connection in §2.2 is productive.** Expert performance as orientation-driven rather than rule-following is a well-established phenomenological account; applying it to AI persona behavior is a genuinely interesting analogical move, even if the paper does not develop it into a computational model.
4. **The reconstruction curve in Phase 3 is the cleanest evidence in the paper.** The progressive reconstruction from T1 through T1+T4+T5 provides a growth curve that resembles phase transitions in complex systems: the system is subcritical until Tier 2 is complete, then crosses into a qualitatively different regime at R3.

## P1 — Blocking Issues

1. **"Orientation" has no computational definition in this paper.** The paper uses "orientation" as if it names a well-defined computational property, but the term is never given a computational interpretation. Is orientation a prior over output distributions (the orientation sentence shifts the model toward receiver-experience-relevant outputs)? Is it a constraint on the generation process (the model must maintain a receiver-perspective representation throughout generation)? Is it a feature of the context window that activates a particular latent subspace? The paper discusses "activation" (§6.2) but only metaphorically — "activating the capacity that is most latent." This is not a computational account; it is a psychological metaphor applied to a system whose processing is quite different from human cognition. Without a computational definition, "orientation" is an observation-level label, not a theoretical construct. The paper cannot distinguish between: (a) orientation sentence shifts output distribution by providing a receiver-experience framing; (b) orientation sentence reduces domain-format attractor basin by introducing a competing constraint; (c) orientation sentence is just longer and more specific and therefore scores higher due to prompt-length effects. All three are consistent with the data; only (a) and (b) support the tier model's theoretical claims.

2. **The tier structure may be an artifact of the evaluation rubric rather than an emergent property of the creative system.** The 45-point authoring rubric was designed to capture puzzle quality. If the rubric was designed with experience-first criteria as primary dimensions (as the paper's puzzle-design testbed would naturally produce), then prompts that activate experience-first orientation would score higher because the rubric is constructed to reward them. The tier structure then follows from the rubric's internal weighting, not from the system's behavior. This is not the same as claiming the rubric is wrong — it may correctly capture puzzle quality — but it means the tier structure is a property of what the rubric measures, not an independent discovery about creative AI systems. The paper should address this circularity: how do we know the tier structure is real rather than a projection of the evaluation criteria?

## P2 — Important Issues

1. **The paper does not address whether tier structure is stable across different language models or model temperatures.** The experiments appear to use a single model configuration. If the tier structure is a genuine architectural property of creative quality in AI systems, it should be observable across different base models, different temperatures, and different context window sizes. If it is specific to the model used, it may reflect that model's training distribution rather than a general property of AI creative systems. The generalization claim requires at minimum a note about which model was used and whether replication across models was attempted.

2. **The emergence question is never asked.** The paper frames the tier structure as a prescriptive design framework (OLE tells you what order to put things in). But the more interesting question is whether a system trained with many OLE-structured prompts would develop orientation-first processing endogenously — whether the tier structure would emerge from training rather than requiring explicit prompt engineering. This question is mentioned obliquely in §6.1 (the training distribution discussion) but not developed. It is the natural direction for artificial life researchers who want to understand whether orientation-first creative behavior can be a stable evolutionary strategy rather than a pre-imposed constraint.

## Recommendation
Accept with major revisions. The empirical findings are real and the non-monotonicity result is genuinely interesting. The paper needs a computational account of "orientation" (even a speculative one) and must address whether the tier structure is a property of the creative system or the evaluation rubric. The emergence question should be acknowledged as an open direction.
