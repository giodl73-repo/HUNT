# Phase 1 Trait Isolation — C8 Panel Evaluation

**Rubric:** /45 — Clarity ×1 + Solvability ×1 + (Elegance ×2) + (Reading Reward ×2) + Fun ×1 + Confirmation ×1 + Riven Standard ×1. Each dimension scored /5 before multiplier. Pass ≥ 33/45.

**Reviewers:** Dan Katz (DK), Thomas Snyder (TS), Dana Young (DY)

**Calibration note:** All 8 puzzles read before any scoring. Mental rank (best→worst): AA, T2, T1, T3, T4, T5, T6, A0. AA is the only puzzle with a non-indexing mechanism. T2 earns structural irony. T4 has genuine stripped elegance but self-announces its extraction so completely that the puzzle offers almost no discovery. T5 and T6 are the two most procedural. A0 is the declared baseline.

## A0-bare.md — The Indexed Garrison — Trait: Control (baseline)

A0 is a transparent letter-indexing puzzle: five AoE2 clues, five explicit position markers, one straightforward acrostic reading. DK acknowledges the clues span game systems (landmark, religious, economic, tower, siege) but notes the mechanism is never disguised — indexing is named in the title and executed in a visible table, so the "blame the player" moment is absent because there was no misdirection. TS gives mediocre marks for construction: the entry point is obvious, the deduction chain is trivial, and a ten-line script could reproduce and solve this exactly (Computer Test fail). DY accepts the thematic resonance of TOWER as a final answer but finds the Design Notes section doing work the puzzle itself should have done — the flavor text "Indexed Garrison" tells the solver what they are doing rather than letting the world communicate it; the extraction adds process without adding meaning.

## AA-artist.md — Damaged Inscriptions — Trait: Control (top scorer, 40.3/45)

DAMAGED INSCRIPTIONS uses a mechanism neither shared by any other puzzle in this set nor derivable from standard puzzle hunt templates: the solver must restore a damaged inscription rather than index into a known word. The missing letters are self-evidently at the gap; no extraction step is required beyond reading them in order. DK rates this highly on elegance and fun because the mechanism is invisible until the solver recognizes it, and "blame the player" operates cleanly — a solver who fails to restore TREBUCHET from _REBUCHET has only their own domain knowledge to blame. The thematic close (Step 3: "the five missing letters name the one structure that stands over all of them") is structural narrative, not cutscene text. TS scores it well on construction: the entry point is the visual gap in the word, which was placed deliberately; the deduction path is one-directional; every element is load-bearing (remove any tablet and the answer becomes unsolvable). He docks slightly for the Riven Standard — this is not a hard puzzle, and the deduction layer is thin. DY gives it the highest Reading Reward in the set: the visual grammar (stone tablet rendering, monospace gaps) holds consistently, the answer TOWER is inevitable (the "heart of every castle" flavor is precise), and the extraction earns its step — missing letters read in sequence is the most natural action in the world.

## T1-experience-first.md — Turning the Tide — Trait: Experience-First

T1 leads with narrative vignettes — each clue is a paragraph-length battlefield moment before asking for the game term. The mechanism is a standard acrostic (first letter of each answer), but the misdirect in entry 3 (Aztec/Jaguar Warrior → Celtic/Woad Raider) is the puzzle's structural expression of its trait. DK likes the mechanism variety at the clue level, and the misdirect earns a "blame the player" moment: a solver who knows AoE2 feels the wrongness immediately and self-corrects. However, he notes the explicit correction embedded in the clue ("Read the clue again... Celtic player") hands the solver the answer rather than letting them earn it — this reduces the elegance. TS is concerned about the misdirect from a construction standpoint: entry 3 introduces and then overwrites an incorrect answer, which means there is a deliberate bad deduction path laid in the middle of an otherwise linear solve sequence; for TS this is noise masquerading as difficulty. DY appreciates the experiential framing and notes that the narrative structure makes the answers feel earned, but observes that the reading reward drops sharply after the puzzle's instruction line because the flavor has no visual dimension — it is a prose list, and the layout does not direct attention. The answer TOWER as confirmation is present but does not reframe the experience the way it does in AA.

## T2-surprise-instinct.md — Five Things to Research Before You Forget — Trait: Surprise Instinct

T2's structural irony is the sharpest in the set: the solver spends the puzzle naming monks, archers, infantry, and cavalry upgrades — none of which are towers — and extracts TOWER, the one defensive building they were (literally) not thinking about. This is a thematic and mechanical coincidence the trait made possible: the "post-match debrief" framing mirrors a real AoE2 mistake (ignoring tower upgrades while obsessing over unit upgrades). The hidden self-reference in entry 3 ("Two-Handed Swordsman" contains "Two" encoding the extraction index) is the kind of puzzle-within-puzzle discovery DK rewards as easter egg elegance. TS is more measured: the five clues are technically clean and the extraction (second letter) is consistent, but the solve path is procedural and a computer could execute it trivially (Computer Test partial fail). He gives higher marks than usual for construction because the clues are factually precise and the extraction position is verified against actual letter sequences. DY approves the post-match conceit — "you played well but something slipped your mind" — as a framing that makes the solver complicit in the puzzle's irony, but notes the visual presentation is a plain numbered list with answer blanks and no structural reinforcement of the surprise.

## T3-visual-thinking.md — Fortification Lines — Trait: Visual Thinking

T3 deploys the tower upgrade diagram (Watch Tower → Guard Tower → Keep) as a visual header, grounding the solver in a spatial image before any clue is read. The table uses circled index numbers as "crosshair reticles" — a visual metaphor for zooming into a unit card. DK gives this modest elegance marks: the visual header adds a genuine dimension not found in A0 or T4–T6, but the body of the puzzle is still a standard indexing table, and the mechanism is not novel — it is indexing rendered with visual chrome. TS is interested in the entry point: the visual structure gives a deliberate first-attention location (the diagram), which is a designed entry point in his framework. He notes the diagram is load-bearing because it thematically confirms the answer before it is extracted (the tower upgrade chain is the answer's lineage), not decorative. DY scores T3 the highest of any indexing puzzle on visual grammar: the circled numbers have consistent meaning throughout, the header diagram communicates structure before the solver reads a word, and the layout directs attention in a way no other trait puzzle achieves. The answer TOWER, confirmed by the flavor "What structure does your army's defense aspire to be?", lands as more inevitable here than in T4/T5/T6 because the diagram already showed the tower's upgrade chain. The reading reward is the best among the indexing-mechanism puzzles.

## T4-elegance-drive.md — Fortifications — Trait: Elegance Drive

T4 is the most stripped puzzle in the set: one title word, one instruction sentence, five clues in minimal form, five bracketed first letters shown directly in the clue lines. The elegance claim is real — there is no design note section doing the puzzle's work, no flavor text, no over-scaffolding. DK notes, however, that the bracketed letter [T], [O], [W], [E], [R] essentially provides the answer before the solver begins: a solver who reads down the bracket column before solving the clues sees T-O-W-E-R immediately. This is not "blame the player" — it is the designer handing the player the answer in plain sight. The elegance collapses into transparency. TS agrees: stripping to minimum was the correct instinct, but the extraction mechanism (reading first letters) was made so explicit that the puzzle cannot distinguish a solver who identified all five units from a solver who simply read the five brackets. No deduction is required to extract. DY finds the answer TOWER insufficiently inevitable in this framing: the only flavor is the title "FORTIFICATIONS," which is thematically appropriate but not precise enough to make TOWER feel like the only possible landing. The five unit clues are accurate and tightly worded, which she respects, but without a thematic wrapper the answer could have been any five-letter word.

## T5-rigor-verification.md — Field Dispatch — Trait: Rigor/Verification

T5 is an acrostic-extraction puzzle dressed as a military intelligence report. Five fragments, each clue citing verifiable costs (200W 200G for TREBUCHET, 25F 35W for ELITE SKIRMISHER, 475G for REDEMPTION), upgrade prerequisites, and counter relationships pulled directly from the world data. The rigor is genuine and distinguishes T5 from A0: every datum in the clues is checkable against a reference source. DK appreciates the precision but notes the "Field Dispatch" framing is superficial military-report prose rather than earned narrative — the flavor describes the puzzle from outside rather than making the solver inhabit it. The puzzle also contains a structural awkwardness: Fragment III's answer is WATCH TOWER (two words), meaning the first-letter extraction yields W, which works, but the presentation asks for the answer and first letter separately, making the two-word answer mildly jarring when the solver fills in "WATCH TOWER" and boxes only "W." TS rates the construction quality the highest of all acrostic puzzles in the set because the clues are verifiable and the solve path is deterministic; he docks Riven Standard for the same thinness of deduction as the others. DY finds the extraction mechanical and unrewarding — the five fragments deliver five answers, the first letters spell TOWER, and the confirmation is a stated "Why TOWER is fitting" paragraph in the solution notes (solver-visible text is ambiguous here, but the puzzle page itself provides no visual confirmation moment).

## T6-systematic-thinking.md — Chain of Upgrades — Trait: Systematic Thinking

T6 is the most procedurally explicit puzzle in the set. Five table rows, each row a complete specification: clue, identification step, position parameter, letter output. The "procedure" framing is intentional and accurate. DK rates this the lowest of all trait conditions: the mechanism (variable positional indexing via a table) is the same as A0 but in a slightly more elaborate container, the theme "chain of upgrades" is stated not structural, and the puzzle has the quality of a worked example rather than an experience. There is no narrative, no misdirect, no surprise, no visual grammar — just a procedure to execute. TS has a split reaction: the extraction indices were chosen systematically (positions 3, 2, 1, 5, 2 across five terms of different lengths), and the world data citations are precise, but the deduction layer is absent — identifying BATTERING RAM from the clue and extracting T from position 3 requires no puzzle-solving skill, only knowledge retrieval. DY finds the answer TOWER not earned by the framing: the "Chain of Upgrades" title and the five clued terms (Battering Ram, Horse Collar, War Elephant, Onager, Iron Casting) have no visual or experiential relationship to towers — the connection in the solution notes ("The Battering Ram destroys towers; the War Elephant demolishes walled positions...") is retrospective rationalization, not thematic inevitability. The answer does not reframe the experience.

## Key Findings

**1. Which traits scored closest to AA (41.0)?**

T3 (Visual Thinking, 35.7) is the closest, followed by T2 (Surprise Instinct, 34.7). The gap to AA is still substantial: T3 sits 5.3 points below, which is roughly one full dimension score. No trait condition approaches AA's Elegance or Reading Reward scores because all six trait conditions remain within the indexing/acrostic mechanism family, and AA used a genuinely different mechanism.

**2. Which conditions escaped mechanism gravity?**

None fully escaped. All eight puzzles extract TOWER via acrostic or positional indexing — the mechanism gravity toward letter-extraction is total. However:

- **T3** is the farthest from bare indexing in surface form: the visual diagram and circled indices create a different *experience* of the same mechanism. DY's scores reflect this — she scored T3's Reading Reward at 5/5 and Elegance at 4/5, the highest of any non-AA puzzle on those dimensions.
- **T2** achieved the deepest *semantic* escape: the solver spends the entire puzzle not thinking about towers, making the extraction feel like a punch line rather than a procedure. The mechanism is still indexing, but the *surprise* of the answer constitutes a partial escape from gravity.
- **T1**'s misdirect is the one moment where the solve path is not purely extractive — there is a genuine deduction correction required — but the mechanism returns to acrostic immediately after.

**3. Does T1 or T3 replicate the artist's novel mechanism?**

Neither replicates it. AA's mechanism is *restoration of a damaged inscription*, which requires identifying a word from partial information (semantic reconstruction), not extracting a letter from a known word (syntactic indexing). T1 and T3 both remain within the letter-extraction family. T3 comes closest in surface presentation (visual layout, non-standard index markers), but the underlying cognitive action is still: "identify this AoE2 term, count to letter N." AA's underlying cognitive action is: "what word fits this pattern, given the domain?" — a categorically different act.

## Predicted Answer: What Trait Drove the Artist Condition?

The artist condition was produced by a persona operating primarily from **Experience-First thinking combined with Visual Thinking** — specifically, the instinct to ask "what does the solver *do* with the puzzle?" before asking "what does the puzzle *contain*?"

The evidence: AA's mechanism (damaged stone tablets) is the only mechanism in the set where the solver's action *is* the theme. The solver is literally an archaeologist reading damaged inscriptions, not a puzzle-solver applying a letter-extraction rule. This is Dana Young's "world is the puzzle" principle — the mechanism and the theme are the same thing. T1 gets close by making the solver feel a battlefield moment before naming a mechanic, and T3 gets close by grounding the visual experience before the clue work begins, but neither collapses mechanism and world into one.

The artist likely holds **Experience-First** as a dominant trait (the solver's journey is designed before the clue content) plus **Visual Thinking** (the stone tablet rendering, the monospace gaps as primary puzzle material, the visual confirmation of the missing letter). The Elegance Drive trait (T4) actively worked against AA's quality: T4's most "elegant" version stripped all narrative scaffolding and produced the lowest-scoring non-baseline puzzle because stripping flavor also stripped the world. Rigor (T5) and Systematic Thinking (T6) drove toward verifiable procedure, which moved the puzzles further from AA's experiential mechanism, not closer.

**Predicted primary trait: Experience-First. Secondary: Visual Thinking.**

The finding is consistent with the rubric's distribution: AA's exceptional scores on Elegance (×2) and Reading Reward (×2) — the two highest-weighted dimensions — were produced by the mechanism being *experiential* rather than *procedural*. Experience-First directly addresses the Reading Reward dimension; Visual Thinking directly addresses Elegance and the "inevitable answer" criterion. Both traits work on the highest-weight rubric dimensions. None of the remaining four traits (Surprise, Elegance Drive, Rigor, Systematic) operates primarily on Reading Reward or Elegance in the way AA demonstrates.
