## L-T1-minus-experience.md — FIVE THINGS THAT TOUCH A TOWER — Missing: Experience-First

L-T1 is the most radically degraded condition. The solver-visible content is five AoE2 terms listed in a column and a single question: "What word do they spell?" There are no clues — answers are handed over — and the extraction method is completely undeclared. DK flags this as a contract violation: the puzzle gives no meaningful task to perform, offers no mechanism to discover, and provides no calibrated difficulty curve. TS immediately applies the Computer Test: a trivial script that lists first letters of five provided strings and joins them would "solve" this puzzle — nothing human-constructed is required on the solver's side. DY notes the visual grammar is undefined (is it first letters? lengths? something else?) and that the absence of a journey means there is no moment where the world resolves; the puzzle is a blank check made out to the solver's guessing.

## L-T2-minus-surprise.md — RECORD OF EXTREMES — Missing: Surprise Instinct

A competent, transparent puzzle. The mechanism is declared in the setup line before the solver reads a single clue: "Take its first letter. Read down." The clues are precise specifications (record-holding statistics, upgrade chains, cost values) rather than images or riddles, so no misdirection is available. DK approves the structural honesty — the puzzle's contract is clearly stated, and the task is well-defined — but notes that a declared acrostic with direct-specification clues is solving as confirmed task completion rather than discovery. TS finds the deduction sequence clean and the clues individually tight, but observes that the solve path is obvious from the first line, eliminating the deduction of mechanism that he considers load-bearing. DY misses the moment of visual or experiential surprise: the solver knows what they are doing before they begin, so the answer TOWER confirms rather than reframes.

## L-T3-minus-visual.md — FIVE REPORTS — Missing: Visual Thinking

The strongest of the leave-one-out conditions. Five scout dispatches written as narrative battle reports — the puzzle reads as fiction until the solver realizes it is a word puzzle, which creates genuine misdirection and a discovery moment. The extraction mechanism (second letter of each answer) is declared verbally at the bottom but is not spatially anchored — no circled positions, no column alignment. DK finds the narrative thematically integrated: the five reports describe building material, conversion, infantry upgrade, tower upgrade, and siege — a complete story about a tower's lifecycle. TS notes that the deduction is genuinely forced (each report identifies a unique entity when read carefully) and that the mechanism, though declared, is non-obvious enough that solvers must still work to confirm it. DY's core objection is that the mechanism is given rather than found: without a spatial cue, the solver cannot independently notice the structural trick and must instead follow an instruction, reducing one layer of satisfaction — but she credits the narrative misdirection as a partial substitute for the missing visual surprise.

## L-T4-minus-elegance.md — FIELD NOTES ON UNUSUAL THINGS — Missing: Elegance Drive

The mechanism works and the domain knowledge is accurate, but nothing has been cut. The framing paragraph, italic subtitle, and closing note ("names something you can build to watch the approaches to your base") are all present — the closing note is a particular problem because it telegraphs the answer before the solver has found it, converting a reframe into a confirmation of what they were already told to expect. Individual clue entries run one sentence longer than the minimum needed to force unique identification. DK notes the extraneous surface area without any structural justification — every element should earn its slot, and several here do not. TS finds the clue construction unfinished: the Woad Raider entry provides cost, civilization, and speed when any one of these would uniquely identify the answer; the excess triangulation signals a constructor who stopped before editing. DY reads the closing note as flavor text doing the job the visual design should have done — it over-scaffolds and simultaneously exposes that the puzzle did not trust its own mechanic to land.

## L-T5-minus-rigor.md — LINE OF SIGHT — Missing: Rigor/Verification

The table format and explicit letter-index extraction are clean and functional — this puzzle has the most transparent structure of the leave-one-out set. The solution key self-reports two unconfirmed claims: Watch Tower's Feudal Age advancement trigger may require a Blacksmith prerequisite in some versions, and "Stone Wall" may not be the exact in-game label. DK applies Blame-the-Player: a solver who knows AoE2 well and finds Watch Tower's prerequisite incorrect will blame the puzzle, not themselves — which is the exact inversion of the correct relationship. TS treats a factual error in any constraint as a structural failure: if the clue for entry 1 is wrong, entry 1 has no valid unique answer, and the puzzle is unsolvable for an informed solver. DY notes that the difficulty curve is undermined if a solver's domain knowledge catches the error mid-solve: an apparent ambush at the first clue breaks the experience. The extraction mechanism itself is honest and the letter indexing is verified; the risk is entirely in two specific clue facts.

## L-T6-minus-systematic.md — STANDING OVER — Missing: Systematic Thinking

The atmospheric prose clues are the strongest writing in the leave-one-out set — the Onager erasing its own forest and the Relic earning gold in silence are images that land as ideas, not just identifications. The presentation is minimal: five prose descriptions, two short instruction lines, no table, no column anchoring. Two structural problems emerge from the absent systematic thinking. First, the extraction ("Take the first letter... Read down") lacks a grid column or anchoring device, leaving the interface slightly underspecified. Second, two entries permit multiple valid resolutions: entry 4 ("lowly trash unit... defeats most expensive archer") can resolve to "Skirmisher" or "Elite Skirmisher" (only the latter gives E; the former gives S, breaking the answer), and entry 5 ("earns gold while doing nothing") can resolve to "Relic" or "Monastery" (a solver might reasonably write the building rather than the garrisoned object). DK notes the structural ambiguity is a binding-choice problem — the solver can commit to a wrong answer with equal confidence. TS finds two entries that fail to force a unique reading, which is a uniqueness failure at the construction level. DY credits the visual minimalism and the atmospheric writing as high-quality surface, but the answer cannot be confirmed unless the solver happens to pick the right resolution at both ambiguous entries.

## Key Findings

**Largest drop: Experience-First (T1), -29.4 points.** Removing the experience-first orientation does not merely degrade the puzzle — it eliminates the puzzle. Without a clue layer, the solver is handed the answers and asked to guess the mechanism from a list of terms. There is no solvable task. The floor-level scores across all three dimensions (Clarity, RR, Fun, Riven) confirm this is a categorical failure, not a quality gradient. Experience-first is not one trait among equals; it is the trait that makes a puzzle a puzzle.

**Negligible effect: Visual Thinking (T3), -4.4 points.** Removing visual thinking leaves a puzzle that remains above threshold and generates strong scores across all three reviewers. The narrative misdirection in L-T3 (scout reports that read as fiction) provides a partial substitute for the visual surprise that spatial cueing would have delivered. The mechanism declaration at the bottom costs one layer of discovery but does not break the puzzle. Visual thinking enhances the experience but is not structurally load-bearing when narrative surprise compensates.

**Mid-tier casualties: Elegance Drive (T4, -11.7) and Rigor/Verification (T5, -12.7).** These both fail the panel. Elegance failure is a death by accumulation — the puzzle is functional but unedited, and the closing note that spoils the answer before the solver finds it is a fatal self-sabotage. Rigor failure is a death by specific error: two unverified clue facts introduce solvability breaks for informed solvers, and the panel's domain-expert reviewers treat a factual clue error as a construction failure regardless of how clean the extraction mechanism is.

## Minimum Sufficient Trait Set

The leave-one-out results identify three tiers of trait load-bearing:

**Tier 1 — Irreplaceable (removal = failure at -20+ points):**
- **Experience-First (T1):** Structures the entire clue layer. Without it, the puzzle has no solver task. No other trait can compensate. This is the architectural foundation.

**Tier 2 — Load-bearing (removal = below threshold, -11 to -13 points):**
- **Elegance Drive (T4):** Controls surface economy and prevents self-sabotage (answer-telegraphing before discovery). Unedited puzzles fail because they undermine their own extraction.
- **Rigor/Verification (T5):** Ensures factual claims in clues are defensible against expert solvers. A single wrong fact breaks solvability for the population the panel represents.

**Tier 3 — Quality-amplifying but substitutable (-4 to -8 points, pass maintained):**
- **Visual Thinking (T3):** Significantly improves Reading Reward and the discovery moment, but narrative misdirection can partially compensate. Pass threshold maintained.
- **Surprise Instinct (T2):** Elevates the puzzle from task-completion to discovery. Without it the puzzle is competent but flat. Barely passes.
- **Systematic Thinking (T6):** Prevents structural ambiguity in answer identification. Without it, two entries permit multiple valid resolutions. Barely passes due to high prose quality.

**Minimum sufficient set for ≥33/45:** T1 (Experience-First) + T4 (Elegance Drive) + T5 (Rigor/Verification) form the irreplaceable core. Adding any one Tier 3 trait pushes the puzzle to a comfortable pass; adding all three Tier 3 traits reaches the artist's 41.7 ceiling. The artist's quality advantage over the minimum-sufficient baseline is attributable to all six traits operating together — each Tier 3 trait contributes approximately 4–8 points of quality that no other trait recovers.
