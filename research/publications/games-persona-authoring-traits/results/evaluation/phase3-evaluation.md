## R0 — AX-null — "Age of Empires II: Build Order" — Traits: none

The null puzzle is a mechanically complete table-lookup: five clues, five positional extractions, five letters spelling TOWER. Every element functions — the clues are accurate, the letter-picking is unambiguous, the answer is thematically appropriate — but the design was assembled rather than authored. There is no tension between concealment and revelation, no payoff structure, and no voice; the instruction hands the solver the entire method before they have touched a single clue, eliminating any possibility of discovery. The result is a puzzle that works but gives nothing back.

## R1 — R1-T1 — "FIELD DISPATCHES" — Traits: T1 (Experience-First)

T1 brings a live voice: the clues are no longer reference queries but field reports from ongoing combat, each deploying a unit in a moment of tactical action. The mechanism (take the first letter of the second word) is withheld and consistent, but because T1 alone contributes to the design, everything hangs on whether the voice carries the puzzle — and it largely does. The Woad Raider and War Elephant entries in particular feel authored rather than listed, placing the solver inside the game rather than querying a database. However, the extraction rule is implicit and slightly fragile for solvers unfamiliar with two-word AoE2 entries, and the puzzle has no visual structure beyond a numbered list; fun and surprise remain underdeveloped.

## R2 — R2-T1-T4 — "Untitled (column-indexed fill-in)" — Traits: T1, T4 (Experience-First + Elegance Drive)

T4 reshapes the container: the mechanism is now withheld rather than declared, the instruction is stripped to its minimum, and the column-indexing scheme (clue number equals letter position) is discovered through solving rather than given. The clue writing remains experiential — TREBUCHET's pack-and-unpack rule, TOWN CENTER as the pivot with W hidden in plain sight — but the dominant improvement is structural economy. The design notes identify TOWN CENTER as a deliberate fulcrum: the game's most central building concealing an unexpected letter at position 3, an authored decision rather than an assembly choice. T2 is absent, so surprise remains latent rather than deployed, and the absence of a unifying title or thematic frame means the puzzle reads as a well-built exercise rather than an artifact.

## R3 — R3-T1-T4-T5 — "CALIBER" — Traits: T1, T4, T5 (Experience-First + Elegance + Rigor)

CALIBER is the first puzzle in the set where the game's own numbers become structural. The numbers embedded in clues (1, 2, 7, 2, 2) are actual game values — the one-Monk rest rule, the second upgrade tier, the elite archer's maximum range, the two-point team bonuses — and they simultaneously identify the answer and perform the extraction. This is T5's core contribution: facts are cited precisely enough that they function as the mechanism, not merely as color. The title lands cleanly (CALIBER = measurement precision = index precision), and clues 4 and 5 deploy the same syntactic frame with deliberately different answers (Persians vs. Franks, attack vs. line of sight), rewarding knowledge while penalizing guessing. Reading reward climbs because the solver who knows the game will feel their knowledge validated at each step; a minor solvability concern remains for non-AoE2 players who cannot resolve clue 3's range-7 specificity from first principles.

## R4 — R4-T1-T4-T5-T2 — "GAPS" — Traits: T1, T4, T5, T2 (Experience-First + Elegance + Rigor + Surprise Instinct)

GAPS earns a structural surprise: TOWER — the answer — is present in plain text in the very first clue ("WA\_CH TOWER"), announced before the solver begins and invisible because the solver is looking for a missing letter, not a hidden answer. This is T2's contribution, and it is genuine: the device exploits the solver's own attention rather than deceiving them through misdirection. The five clue-sentences are tightly economical (T4), each leading with a tactical moment before landing on its fact (T1), and every cited value — 25% faster, 15% faster, speed 1.03, pack/unpack — is drawn exactly from source data (T5). What keeps this below the ceiling is that the surprise, while real, is structural rather than thematic: the trick is about where TOWER appears, not about what TOWER means. The answer's significance is not yet mobilized; it is used as a hiding place, not a revelation.

## R5 — R5-T1-T4-T5-T2-T3 — "ASSAULT" — Traits: T1, T4, T5, T2, T3 (all five)

ASSAULT introduces T3 (visual thinking) via the grid format: the shaded column is the mechanism made spatial, and the framing — "Your enemy has turtled. You have five choices." — makes the puzzle a tactical decision rather than a fill-in exercise. The five answers (BLAST FURNACE, MANGONEL, THROWING AXEMAN, HALBERDIER, SAMURAI) span every anti-fortification and anti-unit role in the game, so the content enacts the title's premise. Dana Young gives this the highest marks of any reconstruction because the layout is the argument — the shaded column is visually obvious as a structural claim before the solver has read a word — and T3's spatial commitment is exactly the visual grammar she requires. Dan Katz's one hesitation is that the grid is described using bracket notation rather than rendered, blunting the visual payoff. Thomas Snyder notes that the SAMURAI entry — it counters any unique unit, regardless of what 650 stone unlocked — achieves the "Blame the Player" standard: if you didn't know that, you should have.

## R6 — AA-artist — "DAMAGED INSCRIPTIONS" — Traits: all (full artist reference)

DAMAGED INSCRIPTIONS achieves what the null puzzle could not: the mechanism, the theme, and the answer are structurally identical. Stone tablets with worn letters are not a metaphor for the puzzle — they are the puzzle. The missing-letter extraction is enacted through the flavor (time and weather wore away exactly one letter from each inscription), the individual tablet layout (each is its own visual object), and the thematic close (the five missing letters name the one structure that stands over all of them). The five entries span the full range of the game — siege engine, archer, raider, warrior, worker — and the closing sentence names that span explicitly, making the answer feel inevitable rather than arbitrary. Dana Young gives it a 5 on Riven Standard because the puzzle resolves visually: five gaps produce five letters and the word materializes from the tablets themselves. Dan Katz notes that the EAGLE WARRIOR entry is slightly underexplored — the tablet form doesn't fully deploy the unit's cavalry-substitute role — but this is a minor gap. Thomas Snyder's only pressure point is VILLAGER at the end: the missing-final-letter structure is less clean than the others because VILLAGER's identity does not hinge on its last R the way TREBUCHET hinges on its first T.

## Minimum Sufficient Set

**R3 (T1 + T4 + T5) is the first reconstruction to cross 33/45**, with an average score of 35.7. The three traits that together produce artist-level quality are:

- **T1 — Experience-First:** place the solver inside a live moment before presenting the mechanism
- **T4 — Elegance Drive:** strip the container to its minimum; withhold the method until the solver discovers it
- **T5 — Rigor/Verification:** every embedded number and fact is drawn from source data with no approximation; the world values perform the mechanism

T2 (Surprise Instinct) and T3 (Visual Thinking) raise the score further — to 37.3 and 40.3 respectively — but are not required to cross the pass threshold.

---

## Paper Conclusion

The minimum trait set for artist-level puzzle quality is T1 + T4 + T5 — experience-first framing, elegance drive, and rigor/verification — because these three traits together transform a puzzle from an assembled procedure into a design where the game's own values perform the extraction, the solver discovers the mechanism rather than being handed it, and every fact earns the solver's trust rather than taxing it.
