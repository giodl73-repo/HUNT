# Condition T2 — Surprise Instinct

## Puzzle

### Five Things to Research Before You Forget

*A post-match debrief. You played well — aggressive, efficient, always at the Blacksmith or the Monastery. But something slipped your mind. Five upgrades in particular. Name each one below.*

---

**1.**
This Monastery technology costs only gold and unlocks a peculiar kind of arms race: the one fought not with swords but with faith. Without it, an enemy Monk can simply walk up and undo every unit you converted. Name the technology that lets your Monks fight back.

`_ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _`

---

**2.**
Three tiers define the ranged attack chain at the Blacksmith: one in Feudal Age, one in Castle Age, one in Imperial Age. This is the middle tier — 200 food and 100 gold, available the moment you complete your Castle Age advance. Every archer in your army benefits, including cavalry archers and ranged unique units. Name it.

`_ _ _ _ _ _   _ _ _ _ _`

---

**3.**
The soldier who wields this Imperial Age upgrade has graduated beyond the Long Swordsman. He carries no shield — that comes later, with Champion — but his grip frees both hands for a heavier stroke that punishes buildings and cheap trash units alike. He is fast enough to close distance. He is strong enough to matter. Name this unit.

`_ _ _  - _ _ _ _ _ _   _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _`

---

**4.**
A Monastery upgrade available from the Castle Age, costing only gold. It does not help Monks convert faster or survive longer. Instead, it moves them. Monks who have researched this cross the battlefield with an urgency their enemies rarely expect. Name this technology.

`_ _ _ _ _ _`

---

**5.**
The final upgrade in the standard archer line, available only in the Imperial Age. Skirmishers still ruin its day, and Siege Onagers are a nightmare — but against massed infantry or an undefended Town Center, nothing in the late game delivers fire quite so efficiently from a fixed position. Name this unit.

`_ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _`

---

*Now take the second letter of each answer, in order. What did you forget to build?*

## Solution Key

**Step 1 — Identify each answer:**

| # | Clue Topic | Answer | Letters |
|---|-----------|--------|---------|
| 1 | Monastery tech that lets Monks convert enemy Monks | **ATONEMENT** | A **T** O N E M E N T |
| 2 | Middle tier of the Blacksmith ranged attack chain | **BODKIN ARROW** | B **O** D K I N  A R R O W |
| 3 | Imperial Age infantry upgrade from Long Swordsman | **TWO-HANDED SWORDSMAN** | T **W** O - H A N D E D  S W O R D S M A N |
| 4 | Monastery tech that makes Monks move faster | **FERVOR** | F **E** R V O R |
| 5 | Final upgrade in the standard archer line | **ARBALESTER** | A **R** B A L E S T E R |

**Step 2 — Extract the second letter of each answer, in order:**

A **T** ONEMENT → **T**
B **O** DKIN ARROW → **O**
T **W** O-HANDED SWORDSMAN → **W**
F **E** RVOR → **E**
A **R** BALESTER → **R**

**Step 3 — Read the answer:**

T — O — W — E — R

**TOWER**

---

**Why it works:**

Each answer is a real AoE2 upgrade (verified against the data files):

- **Atonement**: Monastery, Castle Age, 325G. "Monks can convert enemy Monks." (techs.md)
- **Bodkin Arrow**: Blacksmith, Castle Age, 200F 100G, +1 attack +1 range — the second tier of Fletching → Bodkin Arrow → Bracer. (techs.md)
- **Two-Handed Swordsman**: Infantry line, Imperial Age, 300F 100G upgrade cost. Precedes Champion. Strong vs Trash units and buildings. (units.md)
- **Fervor**: Monastery, Castle Age, 140G. Monks move 15% faster. (techs.md)
- **Arbalester**: Archer line, Imperial Age. Final standard archer upgrade. Weak to Skirmishers and Siege Onager. (units.md)

The extraction is the second letter of each answer, which is a consistent rule applied uniformly.

## Trait Application

The "wait, really?" is structural, not decorative: the solver spends the entire puzzle thinking about monks, arrows, infantry, and archers — none of which are towers — and then the extracted answer is the one defensive building they weren't thinking about. The irony is sharp because in competitive AoE2, neglecting tower upgrades while obsessing over unit upgrades is a real, named mistake; the puzzle embodies that mistake in its own design.

The surprise is compounded by clue 3, where the word "Two" appears in plain sight as part of the answer's name, encoding the extraction index (position 2) without disguising it — solvers who notice feel like they found something hidden in plain view, while solvers who don't notice still solve it correctly.
