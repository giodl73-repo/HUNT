# Condition L-T4 — Artist minus Elegance Drive

## Puzzle

**FIELD NOTES ON UNUSUAL THINGS**
*Observations from someone who has spent too long watching a game*

---

There are units and technologies in Age of Empires II that have exactly one thing that makes them remarkable — one function, one fact, one behavior that no other thing in the game shares. I have collected five of them here because I find them interesting. They are not grouped by age or type or cost. They are grouped only by the fact that they surprised me when I learned what they were.

---

**I.**
This unique unit fights at range — but it is not an archer. It throws its weapon rather than firing it, which means it can send damage over walls that would stop a sword entirely. It belongs to a cavalry civilization that does not otherwise train ranged infantry. Trained at the Castle.

**II.**
This siege engine can do something that no other weapon in the game can do: it can remove part of the map. Fire it at a stand of trees and the trees come down. This opens new routes for armies that did not exist when the game began. Upgraded from the Mangonel. Costs 160 wood and 135 gold.

**III.**
This is the fastest infantry unit in the game. Speed 1.03. That number sounds small until you are watching it chase down cavalry that expected to be safe. It costs only food and gold — no wood — and it belongs to a civilization whose entire bonus structure is built around moving quickly and hitting hard. Celtic.

**IV.**
This unit is the upgraded form of one of the cheapest units in the game. It costs no gold. It is classified as a trash unit. And yet it hard-counters archers — including Arbalests, which cost 25 wood and 45 gold each. The math does not seem to work until you understand the armor class system. Available from Castle Age.

**V.**
This is a technology researched at the Monastery, and it fundamentally changes what a Monk can target. Without it, Monks can only convert living units. With it, Monks can convert buildings. They can convert siege weapons. They can, in principle, steal your Castle from you. It costs 475 gold and nothing else.

---

*The first letter of each answer, read from I to V, names something you can build to watch the approaches to your base.*

---

## Solution Key

The five entries identify specific AoE2 units and technologies, selected because each possesses exactly one property that is unique to it in the game. Taking the first letter of each answer spells TOWER.

**I.** "Fights at range — not an archer — throws its weapon — can send damage over walls — belongs to a cavalry civilization" — **Throwing Axeman** (Franks unique unit; the only ranged infantry in the 8-civ set; explicitly described in units.md as "throws axes past walls") → **T**

**II.** "Can remove part of the map — fire at trees and they come down — opens new routes — upgraded from the Mangonel — 160 wood 135 gold" — **Onager** (the Siege Onager variant can destroy trees; cost matches the Mangonel/Onager cost in units.md) → **O**

**III.** "Fastest infantry in the game — speed 1.03 — costs food and gold, no wood — Celtic" — **Woad Raider** (Celts unique unit; explicitly flagged in units.md as "fastest infantry in the game" at speed 1.03; costs 65F 25G) → **W**

**IV.** "Upgraded form of a cheap unit — no gold cost — trash unit — hard-counters archers including Arbalests — available Castle Age" — **Elite Skirmisher** (upgrade of the Skirmisher; 25F 35W, no gold; explicitly listed in units.md as hard-countering Archers/Crossbows/Arbalesters) → **E**

**V.** "Monastery technology — allows Monks to convert buildings and siege weapons — 475 gold" — **Redemption** (techs.md: "Monks can convert buildings and siege weapons"; cost 475G) → **R**

**T — O — W — E — R**

## Omitted Trait Effect

Without the elegance drive, the puzzle does not prune itself: the framing paragraph, the italic subtitle, and the closing note are all present even though a single unlabeled extraction instruction would suffice, and each clue runs one sentence longer than the minimum needed to point to a unique answer — the Woad Raider entry specifies cost, civilization, and speed in a way that provides more triangulation than the mechanism requires. The puzzle is legible and the mechanism works, but nothing has been cut, so the solver has to carry more surface area than they need to, and the surprise of landing on TOWER is slightly dulled by the closing note that tells them what the answer will name before they have found it.
