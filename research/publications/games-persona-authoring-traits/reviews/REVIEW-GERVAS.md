# Review: What Does a Persona Do? Cognitive Trait Decomposition of Authoring Identity in AI Creative Task Generation
**Reviewer:** Pablo Gervás | **Round:** 1

## Scores (1-4)
Novelty: 3 | Rigor: 3 | Clarity: 3 | Significance: 3 | **Overall: 3.0**

## Overall Assessment
The paper reports an interesting empirical study of how decomposed cognitive trait prompts affect AI creative output quality in a puzzle authoring domain. The three-phase experimental design is methodologically sound, and the core finding --- that T1+T4+T5 constitutes a minimum sufficient set --- is supported by converging evidence across phases. My primary concern is that the cognitive trait taxonomy lacks a principled basis: the six traits are derived through introspective analysis of what "artist" might activate, cross-referenced against creativity theory, but the mapping from theory to trait is selective and the derivation procedure is not replicable. The paper presents an empirically discovered taxonomy as though it were theoretically grounded, when in practice the ground is informal and retrospective.

## Strengths

1. **The cognitive model proposed is coherent at the operational level.** The three-tier structure (architecturally irreplaceable / load-bearing / quality-amplifying) maps cleanly onto the experimental data and provides a vocabulary for discussing persona component dependencies that did not previously exist in the literature.

2. **The failure mode analysis is unusually specific and instructive.** Rather than simply reporting that conditions fail, the paper explains *how* each leaves the quality space: L-T1 produces a list rather than a puzzle, T4 alone produces transparency rather than clarity, T6 alone produces a lookup table rather than a mechanism. These are specific, theoretically interesting failure modes that add value beyond the score tables.

3. **The experience-first / elegance paradox interaction is well-explained.** The argument that T4 needs T1 to know what to keep --- that elegance without an experiential target strips the solver's journey rather than the clutter --- is a genuinely useful theoretical contribution. The dependency chain model (T1 sets target, T4 enforces reduction discipline toward it, T5 verifies contents) is a compact and plausible cognitive model.

4. **Phase 3 reconstruction is the paper's strongest methodological contribution.** Testing the minimum sufficient set by building up from the null rather than only by ablation from the full persona is more rigorous than either approach alone, because it confirms that the three-tier ordering is not merely an artifact of interaction effects in the full persona.

## P1 — Blocking Issues

1. **The trait taxonomy has no principled cognitive basis.** The six traits are identified by "introspective analysis of the artist persona" cross-referenced against "creativity research and design theory." This is a post-hoc rationalization procedure, not a principled derivation. The paper does not explain why exactly six traits, why these six rather than others, or what theoretical framework determines the exhaustiveness or independence of the set. Cognitive psychology offers several principled taxonomies of creative cognition: Guilford's divergent production factors, Finke, Ward and Smith's geneplore model, Ward's structured imagination framework, and Boden's three types of conceptual space transformation (combinatorial, exploratory, transformational) are all candidates. The paper could position its six traits within one of these frameworks, or argue explicitly for a new taxonomy with criteria for what would count as a different or additional trait. Without this, the taxonomy is an empirically useful but theoretically arbitrary decomposition.

2. **The scope of the theoretical claims about "artistic orientation" is not warranted.** The claim that "artistic training cultivates a small number of domain-agnostic cognitive habits" and that these habits generalize to "any creative generation task where audience experience is the primary design constraint" requires a model of artistic cognition, not a literature review. The paper cites Norman, Hassenzahl, Rams, Berlyne, Arnheim, and Sawyer, but these citations are used to support individual traits rather than to construct a model of the cognitive architecture that "artistic orientation" instantiates. Why is it that these six traits and not others constitute the cognitive signature of artistic orientation? The paper needs either a principled answer to this question or a more modest framing that presents the taxonomy as empirically derived rather than theoretically grounded.

3. **Reproducibility is compromised by the single-run protocol.** All conditions are single runs at temperature 1.0. The paper acknowledges "no sampling variance" but treats the three-reviewer spread as "a partial proxy for evaluative uncertainty." This is not equivalent: the three reviewer spread measures evaluative disagreement given a fixed generated output, not uncertainty about whether a different run would produce a different output. For single-run conditions, particularly the borderline cases (R2 at 30.3/45, which is 2.7 points below threshold), the difference between pass and fail may be entirely within single-run generation variance. A minimum of three runs per condition with average scores would address this concern.

## P2 — Important Issues

1. **The relationship between the six traits and narrative cognition is unexplored.** For ICCC's narrative and cognitive modeling community, the most interesting question is whether these six traits constitute a domain-general creative production model that could be applied to other generative systems. The paper is entirely about prompt engineering for LLMs; it does not ask whether the three-tier trait structure corresponds to anything in computational models of creative cognition (e.g., production rules, evaluation functions, revision operators). Addressing this connection would substantially increase the paper's relevance to the CC community.

2. **The motivation for including T6 (systematic thinking) as a creativity trait is weak.** The paper identifies systematic thinking as the trait that "connects to well-documented expertise advantages in structured problem decomposition" (Chi et al.). But expertise-based structured decomposition is a problem-solving competency, not a creative cognition trait. The inclusion of T6 in a taxonomy of "what does artist activate?" seems motivated by completeness rather than theoretical necessity. If T6 is a problem-solving trait rather than a creative trait, its below-baseline isolation score is not surprising and is not a finding --- it is an artifact of including a non-creative trait in a creative trait taxonomy. The paper should address whether T6 belongs in the taxonomy or acknowledge that its role is as a control condition rather than a genuine creative trait candidate.

## Recommendation: Major Revision
