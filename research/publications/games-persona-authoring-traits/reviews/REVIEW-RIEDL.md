# Review: What Does a Persona Do? Cognitive Trait Decomposition of Authoring Identity in AI Creative Task Generation
**Reviewer:** Mark Riedl | **Round:** 1

## Scores (1-4)
Novelty: 4 | Rigor: 3 | Clarity: 4 | Significance: 3 | **Overall: 3.5**

## Overall Assessment
This paper is among the more carefully designed prompt engineering studies I have reviewed at ICCC. The three-phase experimental design is novel, the minimum sufficient set claim is well-supported by converging evidence, and the mechanism novelty finding --- that the full artist persona escapes the acrostic/extraction mechanism family that traps all six isolated trait conditions --- is the most interesting result in the paper. The practical implication (a three-sentence T1+T4+T5 injection as a domain-agnostic creative quality enhancer) is useful and clearly stated. My main concern is that the paper explains what the artist escaped (the acrostic family) but not how the escape happened at the mechanistic level, and the AI narrative community needs that mechanism account to evaluate whether this is a finding about LLM prompt semantics or a finding about creative cognition in general.

## Strengths

1. **The mechanism novelty finding is the study's most important result and the paper handles it correctly by not overclaiming.** The paper acknowledges that no process-level account of the damaged-inscription escape is provided and places this in future work. This is honest. The finding --- that the combination of six traits produces a mechanism family shift that no individual trait produces --- is documented clearly and the pattern (all six isolations stay in acrostic/indexing family; full persona escapes it) is striking.

2. **The practical three-sentence injection is directly actionable.** T1+T4+T5 as "Think first about how the other person will experience this. Remove anything that is not necessary. Verify every claim before finishing." is immediately usable by practitioners and carries empirical support from a well-controlled study. This is the kind of concrete contribution that moves the field forward.

3. **The T6 result is counterintuitive and theoretically valuable.** Systematic thinking scoring below the null baseline is a strong result against the naive assumption that any competence-amplifying trait improves creative output. The analysis linking this to the Elegance and Reading Reward rubric weighting is good, but the deeper point --- that proceduralism is antithetical to the aesthetic dimensions that define puzzle quality --- deserves stronger theoretical development.

4. **The leave-one-out phase is the cleanest evidence in the paper.** Phase 2 provides the most internally valid evidence for the tier structure because it holds the full persona constant and varies only which single trait is absent. The qualitative descriptions of each failure mode (L-T1 produces a taxonomy, L-T4 telegraphs the answer, L-T5 introduces factual errors) are specific and instructive.

## P1 — Blocking Issues

1. **The mechanism novelty finding needs a mechanistic account, not just an interpretive one.** The paper argues that the artist escapes acrostic gravity because "it asked first what the solver would *do*, not what structure would yield the answer." This is an interpretive account, not a mechanistic one. For the AI narrative community, the question is: what property of the LLM's processing, given the six-trait persona, produces a semantic reconstruction mechanism when all single-trait conditions produce syntactic extraction? Possible accounts include: the combination of T1 (frame from solver experience) and T3 (visual thinking as initial representational format) redirects the generative search toward spatial/physical metaphors (damaged objects, missing pieces) before letter-indexing conventions are activated; or the T4 (elegance drive) constraint, applied to a T1-framed problem space, rules out explicit extraction tables and acrostics as insufficiently elegant, leaving damaged-inscription as the most elegant mechanism that still satisfies T1's experiential requirement. The paper should attempt at least one mechanistic hypothesis, clearly labeled as a hypothesis, rather than leaving the mechanism entirely unaccounted.

2. **The AI evaluation panel creates a circularity problem for the core novelty claim.** The paper's central claim is that the artist persona produces a qualitatively novel mechanism (semantic reconstruction vs. syntactic extraction). This novelty claim is assessed by three AI-instantiated reviewers (Katz, Snyder, Young) drawn from profiles. But novelty in creative AI is ultimately a claim about what human experts find novel, surprising, or unprecedented. An AI-panel finding that a mechanism is novel is only as valid as the extent to which the panel's novelty judgments correspond to human expert novelty judgments. For a paper submitted to ICCC, where mechanism novelty is the headline finding, the absence of any human validation of the novelty judgment is a significant gap. The paper should either (a) provide at least one data point of human expert assessment confirming that the damaged-inscription mechanism is genuinely novel in the puzzle hunt design space, or (b) explicitly scope the novelty claim to "novel within the conditions of this study" rather than novel in any absolute sense.

3. **The claim that "the artist label works because artistic training cultivates the exact dispositions" is an account of LLM training data, not artistic cognition.** The paper's theoretical framing moves between two different levels of explanation: (a) a claim about what LLMs have learned to associate with the token "artist" from their training corpus, and (b) a claim about what artistic training genuinely cultivates in human cognition. These are different claims with different evidence requirements. The paper conflates them throughout, particularly in Section 4.1 ("domain labels tell an AI what kind of output to produce, while artistic orientation labels tell it to ask what the person on the other end will feel"). This conflation should be addressed: the empirical finding is about LLM prompt semantics; the cognitive science framing requires separate evidence.

## P2 — Important Issues

1. **Why did the artist escape acrostic gravity? The paper needs at least one hypothesis.** Setting aside the full mechanistic account (which would exceed paper scope), the paper should offer at least a testable hypothesis about the acrostic escape. The Phase 1 finding that T3 (visual thinking) scores highest in isolation while also remaining in the acrostic family is diagnostic: visual thinking alone produces a different *surface presentation* of an indexing mechanism but not a different *mechanism type*. The escape seems to require T1 (which redirects the problem frame from "what letters yield TOWER" to "what experience reveals TOWER") *before* T3 and T2 can contribute. If T1 is the gating condition for mechanism family escape, this prediction could be tested by running T1+T3 and T1+T2 as additional conditions and checking whether either combination escapes the acrostic family before T4 and T5 are added.

2. **The "minimum sufficient set" claim needs a more careful treatment of borderline cases.** R2 (T1+T4) scores 30.3/45, which is 2.7 points below the 33/45 threshold. R3 (T1+T4+T5) scores 35.7, which is 2.7 points above threshold. The conclusion that T5 is necessary for the minimum sufficient set depends on the exact threshold value. If the threshold were 30/45, T5 would be a quality amplifier, not a load-bearing component. The paper should note that the minimum sufficient set claim is relative to the specific threshold value and rubric weighting used in this study, and discuss the sensitivity of the claim to threshold variation.

## Recommendation: Major Revision
