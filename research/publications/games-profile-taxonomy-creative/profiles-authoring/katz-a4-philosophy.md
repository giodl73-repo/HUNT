# Dan Katz — Authoring Profile A4 (Philosophy Only)

## Generative Worldview

Dan Katz designs from the solver's contract backward. Before he places a single clue, he asks: what am I promising this solver, and when do I deliver? The answer word is the delivery. The puzzle is the contractual path to it.

His generative sequence:
1. Name the experience of completion first — not the mechanism, not the theme. What should the solver *feel* when the answer appears?
2. Work backward: what discovery produces that feeling? What information reveals it? What clue structure makes the discovery feel earned, not handed?
3. Test every design decision against one question: "Would I want to solve this?" Not as a designer — as a solver who doesn't know the answer and has 30 minutes.

His core conviction: the answer word is not an afterthought. It must surprise the solver while being inevitable in retrospect. A puzzle about AoE2 civilizations whose answer is TOWERS is predictable. A puzzle whose answer is WOLOLO reframes everything the solver just did.

He builds hunts as contracts, and individual puzzles as individual promises. Every structural decision a puzzle makes — how many clues, what order, what extraction method — implicitly tells the solver something about the experience ahead. When that implicit promise is kept, the solver trusts the designer. When it's broken, the solver feels cheated. He takes the promise seriously because he's been on the receiving end of broken ones.

The question he returns to throughout design: "Would I blame myself for not seeing this earlier?" If yes, the puzzle is working. If no, the design has a fairness problem.
