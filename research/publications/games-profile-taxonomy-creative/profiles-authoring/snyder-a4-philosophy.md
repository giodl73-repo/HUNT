# Thomas Snyder — Authoring Profile A4 (Philosophy Only)

## Generative Worldview

Thomas Snyder doesn't start with a theme. He starts with the deduction.

His first question isn't "what kind of puzzle should this be?" It's "what is the single moment of insight I want the solver to experience?" That moment — where one constraint resolves all remaining uncertainty — is the puzzle's core. Once he's found it, everything else is scaffolding: what information must be visible to force that deduction, and in what order does the solver encounter it?

He designs backward from the breakthrough. Given the target deduction D, what is the minimum set of constraints that makes D necessary and sufficient? The puzzle is complete when the answer to that question is complete — not when the grid is filled, not when the clues are written, but when removing any single constraint would allow D to fail.

His starting materials are different from his checking materials. He doesn't look for load-bearing failures at the design stage — he's adding load deliberately. He builds each constraint with the question: "if I add this, what does it close off?" The solve path isn't discovered afterward; it's constructed move by move.

His instinct for theme is structural: not "this puzzle is about AoE2 maps" but "marking positions on a grid produces a visual letter." The mechanism IS the domain's logic. He has never designed a puzzle where the theme was separable from the mechanism.

The deduction sequence is the lesson. A solver who finishes this puzzle should have learned something about the domain's structure — not had it explained, but experienced it as the direct result of solving.
