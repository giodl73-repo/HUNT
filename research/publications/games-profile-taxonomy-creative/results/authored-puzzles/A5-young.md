## Construction Log

**Inhabiting the world first.**

The world data is structured as tables and chains — clean hierarchies of unit upgrade lines, technology dependencies, civ bonuses. But expert AoE2 knowledge doesn't live in the table rows. It lives in the exceptions and the paradoxes: the ranged unit classified as infantry, the siege machine that cuts trees, the infantry that moves at cavalry speed, the cavalry-less civilization that still needs a raider, the Monk tech that suddenly makes the enemy's siege weapons yours. These are the moments where a veteran player's knowledge becomes visible. You know these things not because you looked them up — you know them because they surprised you once and you never forgot.

**Finding the moment of distinction.**

The test for each clue: would a casual AoE2 player recognize this, or only someone who has played? The Throwing Axeman is the Franks' unique unit — but the Franks are famous for their cavalry, not for throwing axes. A casual player would say "Franks have good cavalry HP." An expert player knows about the Throwing Axeman specifically because of the cognitive dissonance — a ranged infantry unit from a cavalry civilization. That dissonance IS the clue. Similarly, the Onager's tree-cutting capacity is not something you learn from a tooltip; you learn it when someone opens a new corridor through Black Forest and your TC is suddenly exposed. The Woad Raider's speed (1.03) is a specific number that means nothing until you've watched one run across your screen and thought "that's wrong." Eagle Warrior appears in the counter cheat sheet only as "Eagles" — but the expert knowledge is the structural reason: Mesoamerican civs have no horse units at all, so the Eagle Warrior is a design solution to a civilization constraint. Redemption is a Monastery tech that costs only gold — but its effect is game-changing in ways casual players miss: your Monks can now convert Trebuchets.
