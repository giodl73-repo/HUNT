# Condition A6-Katz — Katz Reviewer Profile as Author

## Puzzle

**WOLOLO: Five of a Kind**

Each clue below describes a specific unit, technology, or item from *Age of Empires II*. Identify all five. The first letter of each answer, read in order, spells the name of a defensive structure central to AoE2 strategy.

---

1. The only siege weapon trained directly from a Castle — not a Siege Workshop. In Imperial Age, it is your primary tool for demolishing enemy Castles and Town Centers from beyond arrow range. Its mandatory standoff distance is a known tactical weakness.

2. The Imperial upgrade to the Mangonel line. Unlike its predecessor, this siege unit can destroy trees — carving new paths through forests like Black Forest and reshaping the battlefield itself. It deals friendly fire.

3. The unique unit of the Celts, and the fastest infantry in the game. Trained at the Castle, it moves at speed 1.03 — outrunning every other foot soldier. Its civ is known for siege and lumberjacks.

4. The Castle-Age upgrade to the Skirmisher. Its primary purpose is countering archers — the unit that counters infantry. Research it or lose your ranged infantry to crossbow fire.

5. The Monastery technology that extends a Monk's conversion ability to siege weapons and buildings. Without it, Monks can only convert living units. With it, a single Monk can seize an enemy Battering Ram.

---

*Take the first letter of each answer. What defensive structure do they spell?*

---

## Solution Key

T: **Trebuchet** — trained at the Castle, attacks buildings from minimum range, Imperial Age siege
O: **Onager** — Imperial upgrade to Mangonel, cuts trees, deals splash damage including friendly fire
W: **Woad Raider** — Celtic unique unit, fastest infantry (speed 1.03), trained at Castle
E: **Elite Skirmisher** — Castle-Age upgrade to Skirmisher, primary counter to archers
R: **Redemption** — Monastery technology, allows Monks to convert siege weapons and buildings

Answer: **TOWER**

---

## Reviewer-as-Author Notes

Katz's "Blame the Player" principle drove clue construction most directly: each clue had to close tightly enough that a knowledgeable solver feels they should have gotten it, not that they guessed right. This pushed every clue toward a structural property — minimum range for the Trebuchet, tree-cutting for the Onager, the speed stat for the Woad Raider — rather than a vague category description. The "Snyder's Computer Test" shaped elimination of weaker candidates: early drafts used Theocracy (T) and Watch Tower (W), but a script scanning "Monastery tech / name starting with T" or "tower upgrade / name starting with W" would find them trivially. Trebuchet and Woad Raider resist that attack because their clues use cross-referencing properties (trained location + minimum range; civ identity + speed stat). The "Verify the Last Mile" check exposed one near-miss: clue 4 originally described the Elite Skirmisher's upgrade cost, which is true but shared with other upgrade-line items — revising to the counter relationship (beats archers, which beat infantry) made extraction unambiguous. The Katz principle that proved awkward to apply during authoring rather than evaluation was "mechanisms varied enough" — a reviewer can scan five clues and notice repetition instantly, but an author building clue 3 while already holding clues 1 and 2 must hold the full variety constraint actively in working memory, which is a different cognitive load than retrospective assessment.
