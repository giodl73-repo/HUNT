# Condition A6-Young — Young Reviewer Profile as Author

## Puzzle

**HOLDING THE LINE**

*You are playing Age of Empires II. Each situation below describes a specific moment of play — a unit, technology, or structure that defined that moment. Name it. The first letter of each answer, read top to bottom, spells the thing you were building all along.*

---

1. Your enemy is massing on the treeline. You have stone, food, and a University. You queue this Castle Age research and your walls go up faster — 20% faster — because you know the assault is coming and every second of construction is a second you cannot afford to lose.

2. Across the map, your opponent has just reached Imperial Age. You watch the projectile arc. It lands in the dense forest that was your natural wall, and where trees stood, now there is a gap. This siege unit does not merely destroy buildings — it erases terrain itself, cutting corridors through forests no other weapon can touch.

3. You built it in Feudal Age from stone and positioned it to cover your gold mine. It is not the Guard Tower yet, not the Keep — just the first stone structure that fires arrows at anything that walks too close. It is the beginning of every fortification chain in the game.

4. Your back gold is under pressure from a flood of Crossbowmen. You send these — trained at Castle Age, costing food and wood but never gold — because they were built to counter exactly this: archers, at range, for free.

5. Your archers have held the perimeter through Castle Age. Now it is Imperial Age and you are at the Blacksmith. This final tier of archer armor research — 250 food, 250 gold — gives every bowman in your army the last layer of protection they will carry into the endgame.

---

## Solution Key

T: Treadmill Crane
O: Onager
W: Watch Tower
E: Elite Skirmisher
R: Ring Archer Armor
Answer: TOWER

## Reviewer-as-Author Notes

Young's question "what does it feel like to be in this place — and what would you have to do to live there?" drove the framing before a single item was chosen: the five answers needed to cohere as a sequence of decisions inside a single game, not as a trivia list. Second-person present tense was the structural consequence of that question — each clue puts the solver inside a moment rather than describing it from outside, which is as close as a text-only acrostic can come to embodying the world rather than decorating it. The selection of items was then constrained by that narrative arc: a fortification being built and defended, item by item, from Castle Age construction (Treadmill Crane) through the tower anchor (Watch Tower), the garrison troops (Elite Skirmisher), the threat that justifies everything (Onager), and the final upgrade before the siege arrives (Ring Archer Armor). The review question that most directly shaped clue construction was "Is the extraction earning its step?" — an acrostic always risks feeling mechanical, so each clue was written so the moment of recognition (naming the item) feels like it belongs to the narrative, not like an interruption of it. The questions that felt impossible to apply during authoring were the visual grammar questions: "Does the visual grammar hold consistently?" and "Is there a moment where the puzzle resolves visually?" — a five-line text acrostic has no visual grammar to audit and no visual resolution; the answer TOWER appears as a word in the solver's head, not on the page, and Young's most distinctive design instincts (images as primary puzzle material, layout as communicative act) simply have no purchase here. The gap is structural to the format, not a failure of execution.
