# Condition L08 — Leave Out: One Aha

## Puzzle

Five records were recovered from a destroyed siege camp. Each record names a single item of war — civ, unit, technology, or map — but the name itself was burned away. What remains is only what witnesses observed.

Identify each item. Then find what you have named.

---

**Record I**

A Frankish soldier, taken prisoner at the siege, described the man who climbed the wall before the gates opened. He threw no sword. He carried no bow. He launched his weapon from his arm alone — heavy iron, spinning, passing over the parapet without a ladder. The prisoner said: *I watched him throw before the defenders could raise the drop-bar.*

---

**Record II**

A supply master testified about the machine that ended the standoff. It was not the trebuchet — the trebuchet could not fire at the inner courtyard without hitting its own operator. This machine had no minimum range. It could be aimed into the breach, into a packed column, into a woodline at the forest's edge. When the Black Forest garrison refused to yield, the engineers used it to cut new paths through the trees themselves.

---

**Record III**

A Persian cavalry commander, asked to explain why his prize unit was so difficult to stop, said: *Nothing in this army costs as much. Nothing moves as slowly. Nothing hits as hard at close range. Six hundred hit points. Its tread alone breaks formations.* The interviewer noted that the unit belongs to only one civilization and is trained exclusively at the Castle.

---

**Record IV**

A garrison sergeant described the archer-type he always placed on the walls — not for damage, but for economy. *Gold runs out,* he said. *These do not need it. They cost only food and wood. They answer archers with archers, and they do it for free.* He meant the upgraded version, available from Castle Age. The base version serves the same role but at lower efficiency.

---

**Record V**

A monastery chronicle described the turning point of a siege: the moment the enemy's own battering rams changed allegiance. A single Monk, standing inside the rubble, had performed a conversion that the enemy thought impossible. The abbot's note reads: *Without the research, this was not permitted. The technology was purchased in the Castle Age for 475 gold. It granted our Monks dominion over machines and walls alike.*

---

*Write the five names you have found. Read them in order. What do you see?*

---

## Solution Key

T: **Throwing Axeman** — the Frankish unique unit, a ranged infantryman who throws his axe; can strike over walls without a ladder; trained at the Castle
O: **Onager** — Imperial Age upgrade of the Mangonel in the Siege Workshop; no minimum range (unlike the Trebuchet); can destroy trees to cut paths through Black Forest; strong vs. grouped units
W: **War Elephant** — Persian unique unit trained at the Castle; 600 HP (elite); slowest unit in the game; trample damage; most expensive unit (200F 75G)
E: **Elite Skirmisher** — the no-gold archer-line unit; costs only food and wood; hard-counters archers; Castle Age upgrade from Skirmisher
R: **Redemption** — Monastery technology, Castle Age, costs 475 gold; allows Monks to convert siege weapons and buildings (including enemy Rams)

Answer: TOWER

## Omission Notes

With "One Aha, Not Zero, Not Three" omitted, the puzzle layers four distinct insight moments: a per-clue identification aha earned through multi-step deduction (each clue withholds the item's name and requires two or three inferential steps to narrow to one answer), a mechanism-discovery aha when the solver spots that first letters spell a word, a final extraction aha when that word resolves to TOWER, and a retroactive resonance aha when the solver recognizes that every item in the puzzle has a specific mechanical relationship to towers in AoE2 — the Throwing Axeman bypasses tower walls, the Onager demolishes them, the War Elephant tramples through them, the Elite Skirmisher garrisons them cheaply, and Redemption flips the siege engines that reduce them. Rather than "two ahas is a stretch, three is a slog," this design treats each layer as its own reward: solvers who stop at TOWER are satisfied; solvers who ask *why these five* find a deeper answer.
