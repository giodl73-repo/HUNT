# Condition P13 — Framing Is Structural

## Puzzle

# Counter-Play

*Five incidents from the battlefield. In each one, something was destroyed, scattered, converted, or routed. Identify what did the destroying.*

---

1. Your stone keep had stood for three seasons. It lasted eleven seconds once they wheeled that machine into range — the one that lofts a two-hundred-pound stone in a perfect arc, accurate from outside any defensive fire.

2. Forty Crossbowmen held the ridge in tight formation, a wall of bolts. A single volley from one siege engine ended the formation. Not one bolt was fired in return.

3. Three Knights held the gate. Nothing in their cavalry training prepared them for six hundred hit points of Persian war beast bearing down at a slow, unstoppable walk, trampling everything underfoot.

4. The archer battalion had superior range and superior numbers. The javelin soldiers closed to medium range, shrugged off the arrows, and dismantled the formation bolt by bolt. The archers had no answer.

5. The Trebuchet crew watched in disbelief as a lone robed figure walked calmly out from the enemy monastery and toward their engine of war. Under Church law, a technology had to be researched before this was even possible.

*Take the first letter of each answer, in order.*

---

## Solution Key

T: Trebuchet — the machine that lofts stones in a perfect arc from outside defensive range; hard counter to static fortifications
O: Onager — the siege engine whose single area-of-effect volley scatters grouped Crossbowmen; the definitive counter to massed archers
W: War Elephant — the Persian unique unit with 600 HP and trample damage; hard counter to Knights and cavalry
E: Elite Skirmisher — the lightly armored javelin unit that hard-counters archers; resistant to archer fire, strong vs. Crossbowmen
R: Redemption — the Monastery technology that allows Monks to convert siege weapons and buildings; without it, the Monk could not approach the Trebuchet

Answer: TOWER

## Principle Application

The title "Counter-Play" is the mechanism: without it, each clue reads as a description of the victim (the fortification, the archer formation, the Knights), and the solver looks for an item matching that description. With the title, the solver's interpretive mode flips — every clue describes what is being countered, so the question becomes "what defeats this?" not "what is this?" Removing the title would make clues 1 through 4 unsolvable as written, because the correct answer does not appear in the clue at all; only the loser does.
