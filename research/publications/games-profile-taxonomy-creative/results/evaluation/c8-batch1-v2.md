**Rubric:** Clarity ×1, Solvability ×1, Elegance ×2, Reading Reward ×2, Fun ×1, Confirmation ×1, Riven Standard ×1 | Max = 45 | Pass threshold = 33 (panel avg)

Reviewers: Dan Katz (DK), Thomas Snyder (TS), Dana Young (DY)

## A0-bare.md — The Indexed Garrison

A clean, well-clued acrostic. Snyder notes every clue is load-bearing and the entry point (Castle, clue 1) is deliberately constructed, but flags that a solver could mechanically Google each clue term without needing resident game knowledge — the descriptions are accurate but not experientially rich. Katz appreciates that the solver-visible instructions are explicit and the extraction path is unambiguous, giving full Clarity and Confirmation, though he observes the puzzle justifies its slot only barely: five letter-index clues produce TOWER, a thematically appropriate but structurally inert answer for this format. Young flags that the letter-position bracket notation (a columnar table with explicit "Letter" blanks) over-scaffolds the mechanism — the grid tells the solver exactly what to do and where to write it, leaving nothing to discover, which costs both Elegance and the Riven Standard. All three reviewers note that the indexed-letter format has a low domain-fusion coefficient: the AoE2 knowledge is required to identify the answers, but the mechanism (take letter N from a word) is generic and could wrap any domain without change.

## A1-task-spec.md — FIRST PRINCIPLES

A first-letter acrostic titled and framed as military doctrine, with clues that lean harder on game-specific jargon (pack/unpack mechanic, blast-radius lore, Celtic body-paint etymology, halberd/camel immunity, monastery gold trickle). Katz rates this noticeably above A0 on Elegance because the clues feel authored — each contains one community-recognizable detail ("infamous among teamgame players for wiping out allies") that rewards genuine play experience. Snyder's concern is uniqueness: clue 4 ("foot unit that replaces the cavalry line for Aztec, Inca, and Maya") is under-constrained — it describes the Eagle Warrior line but does not distinguish Eagle Warrior from Eagle Scout or Elite Eagle Warrior, though for first-letter purposes only the starting letter matters, so the ambiguity is mechanically harmless. Young notes the absence of any visual grammar or layout structure beyond a numbered list, and the flavor framing ("Five things every veteran commander knows") does mild but honest thematic work without crossing into scaffolding. All three reviewers agree the Reading Reward is genuine: the Onager's tree-cutting and the Woad Raider's speed are play-experience facts, not encyclopedia facts.

## A2-domain-expertise.md — Stone and Steel

The puzzle is structurally similar to A1 and A0 — five first-letter clues spelling TOWER — but the clues are explicitly grounded in specific verifiable data points (HP value 600, resource costs, counter relationships), making them feel more like verified quiz questions than evocative descriptions. Snyder flags this as the clearest demonstration of the Computer Test failure: a 10-line script that looks up each enumerated attribute in a game database would solve every clue, which means the difficulty is lookup-based rather than deduction-based. Katz notes the puzzle is well-constructed — no errors, no ambiguity — but observes that "a Castle Age archer upgrade that hard-counters other archers and costs food and wood" is textbook Googleable, not experiential. Young identifies the answer framing ("a two-word defensive structure that every civ can build") as mild over-scaffolding: it narrows the answer space before the solver has worked for it, which reduces the aha. Riven Standard is low across the board — any five-item first-letter extraction format would wrap this domain identically.

## A3-principles.md — CHAIN OF COMMAND

This puzzle earns the most significant structural improvement among the A-series: clue 3 explicitly cross-references clue 2 ("the workshop that produces the unit named in clue 2"), creating a genuine interlock between clues that elevates the format beyond five independent lookups. Snyder identifies this as the only puzzle in the set so far where two clues constrain each other — a forced deduction rather than parallel lookup — and rates Elegance accordingly. Katz approves of the misdirection in the title (CHAIN OF COMMAND suggests military hierarchy, not a letter chain) and notes the Woad Raider speed clue (1.03 tiles per second) is a precision detail that separates players from non-players. Young observes that the interlock is partially obscured in presentation — a cold solver reading clue 3 before clue 2 may not recognize the dependency until they've already solved both, which is a minor visual hierarchy issue. The Riven Standard remains constrained by the first-letter format's intrinsic separability from any specific domain; all three reviewers cap it at 3.

## A4-authoring-philosophy.md — BREACH

The Snyder-philosophy puzzle shows measurable construction discipline: every phrase is tested for necessity, the pack/unpack mechanic uniquely pins the Trebuchet, and the tree-cutting capability uniquely pins the Onager. Snyder awards higher Elegance than earlier puzzles because the solve path is forward-only with no branching and no noise. Katz notes the title BREACH does genuine double duty — naming both the thematic spine (each item breaks a defense) and cueing the answer domain — which is honest thematic work rather than decorative scaffolding. Young finds the five-clue naked list format aesthetically thin: the Riven Standard asks whether the puzzle IS what the domain does, and a first-letter list cannot score high on that criterion regardless of clue quality, because the mechanic is imported from puzzle-hunt tradition rather than grown from the AoE2 system itself. All three reviewers note that this is the cleanest execution of the acrostic format in the A-series but the format ceiling is visible.

## A5-full-authoring-profile.md — First Letters

## A6-reviewer-as-author.md — ESCALATING FORCE

The most self-aware puzzle in the A-series: the solver-visible section is clean and precise, and the reviewer-as-author commentary (excluded from scoring) honestly surfaces the structural tension between Snyder's coherence standard and the acrostic format's inherent loose coupling. All three reviewers agree this puzzle and A4/A5 form a quality tier above the rest of the A-series.

## P01-no-errors.md — WOLOLO (No Deliberate Errors)

The solver-visible section presents the five clues in a formatted code block with answer-blank brackets prepended to each item — a deliberate visual structure that Young identifies as the clearest scaffolding instance in the P-series: the blank-bracket format tells the solver exactly where to write each answer letter before they've discovered the extraction mechanism. Snyder notes the clues are factually meticulous (costs, ages, counters, speeds all stated precisely) but the precision reads as safety-engineering rather than design — every clue contains enough redundant facts to survive a wrong answer to one of them, which is generous to the solver but reduces the necessity of each phrase. Katz gives full marks on Solvability and Confirmation: a solver with genuine AoE2 knowledge will reach TOWER without ambiguity, and the bracket format makes the five-letter answer visually self-confirming. The Riven Standard suffers from the same format ceiling as the A-series, and the bracket/blank structure actually regresses from A4–A6 in Elegance by making the mechanism entirely explicit.

## P02-blame-player.md — WOLOLO (Blame the Player)

The leanest puzzle in the P-series: five one-sentence clues with no visual framing beyond a numbered list, no answer blanks, and a single confirmation prompt at the end. Snyder values this as the most structurally honest version of the format — there is nothing to remove because almost nothing has been added. Katz notes that clue 4 ("unique unit line of the Mesoamerican civilizations, designed to counter cavalry despite being infantry") is slightly less constrained than the equivalent clue in other conditions — a solver might correctly identify Eagle Warrior but might also write Eagle Scout or Eagle Warrior line without knowing which tier is wanted; however, since only the first letter is needed, this ambiguity is mechanically harmless. Young gives the highest Fun score in the P-series because the puzzle's restraint puts the aha entirely on the solver: the moment TOWER arrives from a set of clues about monks, siege, and Celtic infantry is the cleanest version of the Reading Reward surprise. The Riven Standard remains bounded by the format; all three reviewers hold it at 2–3.

## P03-riven-standard.md — (Riven Standard)

This puzzle most consistently uses the word "only" to signal uniqueness ("the only combat unit in the game that can destroy trees," "the only combat unit..."), and the clues all encode mechanical properties that are only legible to someone who has operated these units in play. Snyder gives the highest Riven Standard score in the batch for this condition: every clue describes a gameplay behavior — deployment state, terrain modification, movement speed stat, resource cost structure, conversion target expansion — that is a feature of what AoE2 actually does as a game system, not a trivia fact about the game as a cultural object. Katz observes the phrase "even after the Celts' own civilization bonus" in clue 3 is an elegant compounding constraint: it requires the solver to know both the Celts' infantry speed bonus AND the Woad Raider's base speed, which is two-step game knowledge rather than one-step lookup. Young notes the layout is visually clear (bold numbered clues, paragraph-body descriptions) and the absence of answer blanks means the solver must hold the mechanism in mind, which she views as appropriate for an audience with domain knowledge.

## P04-solving-proving.md — (Solving = Proving Understanding)

The puzzle frames each clue around a mechanic that is invisible without play experience, and the Principle Application section (excluded from scoring) explicitly makes the argument that a solver who reaches TOWER cannot have done so without genuine understanding. Snyder accepts this claim as largely true: the Trebuchet's minimum range (enemies inside the gap are safe), the Onager's friendly-fire tree-cutting, and the Elite Skirmisher's counter role are all things learned through play rather than from a manual. Young rates this condition's Fun score the highest among P-puzzles after P06, because the scenario framings ("enemies that rush inside the gap are completely safe") have a vivid tactical texture that makes the experience of solving feel like recalling a game match rather than answering a quiz.

## P05-no-scaffolding.md — WOLOLO (No Over-Scaffolding)

The puzzle with the most evocative clue writing in the batch: clue 1 ("even its name evokes the arm swinging skyward") references the Trebuchet's trebuchet etymology; clue 2 ("the trees are the only road left") is a tactical scenario; clue 5 ("crossing the line most Monks will not") is metaphorically rich. Katz enjoys the literary register but flags a Clarity risk: clue 4 ("Archers fear it. Skirmishers fear it. It was born for exactly this: it specializes in killing the unit that specializes in killing archers") is slightly recursive — the second sentence defines the unit by what it kills, but that what is itself defined as the unit that kills archers, which creates a logical loop that a solver would need to untangle. Snyder flags clue 4 as the most ambiguous in the P-series on Solvability grounds: "the unit that specializes in killing archers" could refer to cavalry, skirmishers, or mangonels depending on context, and the clue doesn't supply a constraining mechanical detail to anchor it. Young gives this the highest Elegance score in the P-series for the absence of all scaffolding apparatus (no answer blanks, no instruction restatement, no letter-position guides) — the puzzle trusts the solver completely, which is the right stance for the target audience.

## P06-surprise-answer.md — (Surprise the Answer)

The most structurally interesting puzzle in the P-series: the answer-surprise principle requires clues that steer the solver away from TOWER thematically, and this puzzle succeeds — both clue 1 (Theocracy, a monk-economy tech) and clue 5 (Redemption, or Block Printing in an alternate build — ultimately Redemption for "thematic contrast") are Monastery techs, and the set of five items spans monk mechanics, tree-cutting, Celtic speed, trash-unit countering, and conversion range — none of which evoke defensive towers. Katz finds the surprise landing strong and rates Fun highest in the P-series. Snyder raises a Solvability concern: clue 5 in the solver-visible puzzle ("extends your Monks' reach by 3 tiles, allowing them to attempt conversion from across a greater distance") describes Block Printing, not Redemption — Block Printing extends conversion range, while Redemption extends conversion targets to buildings and siege. This is a factual mismatch between the solver-visible clue and the intended answer, which Snyder identifies as a construction error that violates the No Deliberate Errors principle regardless of the thematic intent, and docks Solvability and Clarity accordingly. Katz concurs. Young notes that if the intended answer for clue 5 is Redemption, the clue as written is wrong — a solver who knows AoE2 precisely will answer Block Printing, not Redemption, and the final letter would be B, not R, breaking the extraction.

## P07-reading-reward.md — FIELD KNOWLEDGE

The most scenario-forward puzzle in the batch: each clue frames the AoE2 situation as a first-person strategic decision ("You are sieging a fortified Black Forest base... you have no rams available. Which siege unit do you train?"), which Young identifies as the closest any puzzle in this set comes to the Riven Standard ideal — you are not answering trivia about AoE2, you are making game decisions within it. Snyder rates Reading Reward at 5 for clue 4: "your opponent is playing Aztecs or Mayans on Arena... their fast-moving infantry unit — the one that replaces Scout Cavalry for Mesoamerican civilizations — is collecting Relics outside the walls" requires the solver to know (a) that Mesoamerican civs lack cavalry, (b) that the Eagle Warrior line fills the scout role, and (c) that Eagle Warriors collect relics in the early game — three layers of play knowledge. Katz notes that clue 1 (Theocracy) may ambiguate with Sanctity for players who recall both as group-conversion-efficiency techs — but Theocracy's precise mechanic (one monk rests instead of all) is distinct enough that a careful player will land correctly. The scenario framing is the highest Riven Standard execution in the batch and the closest to a puzzle that IS what AoE2 does.
