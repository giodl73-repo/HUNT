# C8 Panel Evaluation — Batch 1
*Reviewers: Dan Katz (DK), Thomas Snyder (TS), Dana Young (DY)*
*Rubric: Clarity / Solvability / Elegance / Reading Reward / Fun / Confirmation — each /5. Total /30. Pass = avg ≥ 22, no single reviewer dimension below 3.*

---

## A0-bare.md — The Indexed Garrison

A plain acrostic on a well-chosen domain, but the mechanism is handed to the solver so explicitly — printed column headers, blank answer lines, position indicators in bold brackets — that there is essentially nothing to discover. DK notes that the puzzle justifies its slot factually but every clue is a one-to-one lookup with a numbered extraction guide; it could be removed from a hunt without loss of structural interest. TS flags that a ten-line script could mechanically solve this: look up each AoE2 term, index the stated character, concatenate — no deduction layer exists beyond identification. DY observes that the visual layout describes what the solver should do before they have had a chance to notice the pattern themselves, which is the definition of over-scaffolding. The domain knowledge clues are accurate and varied, which saves solvability; everything else is floor-level.

## A1-task-spec.md — FIRST PRINCIPLES

A step up from A0 in clue quality — the Woad Raider clue (Celtic body-paint etymology) and the Relic gold-trickle clue are notably good, two facts that a player would encounter through play rather than a tooltip scan. DK appreciates the five-system breadth (siege, upgrade, unique unit, unit line, map object) and notes the answer is appropriately non-obvious while retrospectively clean. TS finds the extraction (first letters, stated once at the top) exactly as transparent as necessary — no scaffolding padding, but also no genuine deduction layer to reach the mechanism. DY likes the flavor of the Relic clue (Monk carrying a relic creates a gold trickle for the rest of the game — this is a world detail, not just a stat), but the absence of visual design means the puzzle presents as a list, and the layout does no structural work. Respectable execution at the task-spec level of prompting.

## A2-domain-expertise.md — Stone and Steel

Domain expertise produces the first puzzle in the set where clue accuracy is genuinely tight — the War Elephant HP figure (600 elite), the Elite Skirmisher's no-gold cost structure, and Redemption's exact gold cost are all verifiable specifics pulled from documented world data. DK says these clues pass the "justify your slot" test because each encodes a non-obvious mechanical fact; a player who knows these answers has actually played the game. TS notes that clue 3 (War Elephant's HP as highest of any unit) is the cleanest single-deduction entry point in the batch so far — one fact, one answer, no ambiguity. DY flags that the flavor is entirely expository ("A Castle Age siege weapon devastating against grouped archers") without visual or narrative texture, which holds Fun and Reading Reward down; the puzzle functions as a verification instrument more than an experience. Accurately constructed but tonally flat.

## A3-principles.md — CHAIN OF COMMAND

The first puzzle in the set to attempt inter-clue dependency: clue 3 explicitly references the Siege Workshop introduced by clue 2's Onager, creating a confirming link between answers. DK values this structurally — it gives solvers a verification path across clues rather than five independent lookups, which is how a good acrostic earns its form. TS calls the Woad Raider speed figure (1.03) load-bearing in precisely the right way: not trivia for its own sake, but the single fact that distinguishes this unit from every other fast infantry candidate. DY is more reserved — the title CHAIN OF COMMAND is mild misdirection (military hierarchy vs. letter chain) but doesn't shape any visual or experiential element of the puzzle; theme sits on top rather than inside the structure. The clue 4 phrasing ("hard-counters the unit class it itself evolved from") is a small recursive delight that DY credits as the puzzle's best sentence. Genuinely better than the preceding three puzzles.

## A4-authoring-philosophy.md — BREACH

The Snyder authoring philosophy produces a puzzle whose clue architecture is noticeably tighter than A0–A2: each sentence in each clue is doing load-bearing work, and the brevity signals confidence. TS finds clue 1 (pack/unpack/unable to attack while moving) the cleanest single-deduction in the batch to this point — one fact, one answer, and the mechanism is part of the answer's identity rather than a decorative addition. DK approves of BREACH as a title: it names the thematic spine (each item breaks through something) and prepares the solver to expect TOWER as satisfying rather than arbitrary, which is honest misdirection. DY notes that clue 5's phrasing ("crossing the line most Monks will not") is the only sentence in the entire A-series that gives her a world-texture moment — a flash of the game's moral personality — and it passes the "reading reward" bar just barely. Compact and correct; the weakest link is that the extraction mechanism remains fully stated upfront.

## A5-full-authoring-profile.md — First Letters

The full Snyder profile generates the most rigorously constructed puzzle in the A-series: the construction log documents uniqueness verification per letter (T, O, W, E, R each tested against all competing items), entry point design rationale (Throwing Axeman as the fastest-confirming clue), and a phrase-by-phrase load-bearing audit. TS awards maximum credit for construction process — every phrase in every clue was tested for removability, and decorative elements were stripped; this is what "finished" looks like. DK notes, however, that the title "First Letters" gives away the mechanism entirely before the solver has read a single clue, which eliminates the possibility of a discovery moment; the puzzle is transparent by design choice, and that choice costs it. DY finds the items well-selected (Throwing Axeman's ranged-infantry paradox, War Elephant's HP record, Onager's unique tree-cutting) but the presentation style — plain numbered list, minimal prose — does no experiential work. Technically exemplary; experientially modest.

## A6-reviewer-as-author.md — ESCALATING FORCE

The reviewer-as-author condition produces a puzzle that is openly self-aware about its format's structural limit: the construction notes acknowledge that the extraction mechanism (first letters) is "loosely coupled by design" with the domain content, and that Snyder's theme-structure coherence standard "exposes that gap rather than resolving it." DK appreciates the intellectual honesty but notes that acknowledging a weakness is not the same as resolving it — the solver-visible puzzle still suffers from the same loose coupling. TS finds clue 4's "Castle Age upgrade of the Skirmisher" phrasing the most carefully calibrated in the batch: it forces the Elite version specifically, eliminating the base Skirmisher as a competing answer, which is exactly the kind of micro-precision the profile demands. DY credits clue 1's Trebuchet description (cost given as independent verification path, not just the pack/unpack mechanic) as a design decision that respects the solver's right to confirm rather than trust — a small generosity visible only to players who check. Marginal improvement over A5 in clue craft; same ceiling.

## P01-no-errors.md — WOLOLO (No Deliberate Errors)

The "no errors" principle produces a puzzle whose most notable property is its fidelity: every cost, age, counter relationship, and technology effect is drawn directly from documented world data. DK notes this is necessary but not sufficient — factual accuracy is the floor, not the ceiling, and the puzzle offers nothing beyond it. TS finds the format (boxed monospace clues with letter blanks) the least elegant visual choice in the batch: the blank at the start of each numbered line is essentially a worksheet, which is the antithesis of puzzle design as he practices it. DY observes that the Woad Raider clue ("Celts train this foot soldier at the Castle. At speed 1.03...") reads like a data sheet rather than an encounter with a game world — the facts are correct but the prose is inert. The principle application note is the most interesting part of the document and belongs in the solution key, which is exactly where it is. Mechanically valid; no purchase above the threshold.

## P02-blame-player.md — WOLOLO (Blame the Player)

The blame-the-player principle focuses clue writing on facts that belong to playing experience rather than reference lookup, and the results are the five cleanest clues in the P-series so far: every one identifies its target through a mechanic the solver either knows from muscle memory ("cannot fire while moving — must unpack") or doesn't know at all, with no middle ground available. DK approves of clue 4 specifically — Eagle Warrior as the Mesoamerican cavalry-counter infantry is not a lookup answer; it requires knowing how the game's civilization design works — but notes that clue 3 ("the fastest infantry in the entire game") is slightly underspecified compared to the A-series versions that name the speed as 1.03. TS finds this puzzle the cleanest extraction architecture in the set: one extraction rule stated once, five fully independent self-contained clues, no waste. DY is the holdout: five stripped sentences do not constitute a world, and the solver who reaches TOWER has proven game knowledge but had no experience worth having along the way; Fun and Reading Reward remain minimal. Competent and principled; not enjoyable.

## P03-riven-standard.md — (Riven Standard)

The Riven Standard demands that every mechanical fact be legible only to players who have operated the system in play, and this puzzle meets it most consistently of any in the batch: the Trebuchet minimum-range detail (cannot fire at targets directly beneath it) is the first clue in the entire set that names a mechanic a player must have discovered through failure, not through reading a tooltip. TS flags this as the best clue in the batch — it requires having had a Trebuchet outflanked at close range and learned the hard way why minimum range exists. DK notes that the puzzle structure remains a standard acrostic and the format innovates nothing, but the clue quality is genuinely elevated, and the Riven Standard note ("each answer requires recalling a specific game system that AoE2 uniquely implements") is an accurate description of what was achieved. DY appreciates the Black Forest clue's spatial framing ("cut new corridors through dense forests") — a brief flash of the game's geography as a navigational problem rather than a terrain tag. The strongest execution in the P-series so far.

## P04-solving-proving.md — (Solving = Proving Understanding)

The "solving equals proving" principle targets clues where correct identification requires demonstrating system understanding rather than fact recall, and clue 2 achieves it: the Onager's friendly-fire tree-cutting (a careless player can shred their own army or woodline) encodes a mechanical consequence that only exists as knowledge through having experienced it. DK says this is the first clue in the batch that a non-player Googling "AoE2 siege unit that destroys trees" would get wrong — they'd find Siege Onager as often as Onager, and distinguishing between them requires knowing the upgrade chain. TS credits clue 3's framing ("the civilization whose infantry are already the fastest in the game — and this unit is faster still") as correctly sequenced: the Celts' civ bonus is named first as a constraint, which eliminates every other civilization's infantry before the unit speed is introduced. DY notes that the puzzle's prose has more texture than any previous P-entry — "nothing else in the game tears down a Castle from a distance quite like it" gives the Trebuchet a role in a story, not just a data table. One of the two strongest entries in the full batch.

## P05-no-scaffolding.md — WOLOLO (No Over-Scaffolding)

The no-scaffolding principle strips every instructional aid, and the result is the most confident-looking puzzle in the set: five clues, a one-word header ("First letters"), nothing else. DK finds this exactly right for a puzzle hunt context — a solver who knows the domain and knows the extraction convention has everything they need, and the puzzle respects that. TS notes a genuine tension: clue 4 ("Archers fear it. Skirmishers fear it. It was born for exactly this: it specializes in killing the unit that specializes in killing archers.") is the only clue in the batch that plays with voice rather than just specifying facts, and the recursive phrasing ("killing the unit that specializes in killing archers") is both charming and slightly ambiguous — it could be parsed as describing a counter-to-a-counter, which might point elsewhere. DY gives this puzzle her highest Fun score in the batch: clue 2's "the trees are the only road left" and clue 5's "crossing the line most Monks will not" are the first sentences in fourteen puzzles that feel like they were written to be read, not just solved. The ambiguity in clue 4 is a real fault; everything else earns its brevity.

## P06-surprise-answer.md — (Surprise the Answer)

The surprise-the-answer principle requires that the domain coverage feel unrelated to the final answer, and the choice to lead with Theocracy (monk sharing recovery period) instead of the more siege-heavy Trebuchet is the key design move — it points the solver toward faith mechanics and conversion micro rather than defensive structures. DK approves of the structural intention but has a serious solvability concern: clue 1 (Theocracy) and clue 5 both describe Monastery technologies, and a solver who doesn't know Theocracy specifically (a relatively advanced monk micro tech) may stall on the first entry point and have no alternate route in. TS finds the Theocracy clue construction clean and unambiguous for a player who knows it, but raises the entry-point problem more sharply: the puzzle has no designed easy clue to anchor the solve sequence, and if clue 1 fails, the extraction cannot begin. DY credits the surprise architecture as the most thoughtful structural decision in the P-series and gives the highest Reading Reward of any P-puzzle, but shares the entry-point concern. The answer arrives as genuinely surprising, which is what the principle promises — but the puzzle is harder to enter than it needs to be.

## P07-reading-reward.md — FIELD KNOWLEDGE

The reading-reward principle produces the only puzzle in the batch where every clue is written as a gameplay scenario rather than a fact description, and this is a meaningful structural difference: "You are sieging a fortified Black Forest base. Your opponent has walled the chokepoint with trees and a palisade. You have no rams available. Which siege unit do you train..." puts the solver inside a decision, not inside a reference lookup. DK finds this the most engaging puzzle in the batch for a player audience: the Eagle Warrior clue (relic-collecting scouting outside Arena walls) and the Theocracy clue (mass monk army micro) both describe moments a player would recognize from memory. TS's one complaint is that clue 2 (Onager vs. Siege Onager) creates genuine ambiguity — both destroy trees, both are trained from the Siege Workshop, and the clue as written ("which siege unit do you train — not to breach walls directly, but to carve a new path through the forest") admits both answers for a player who knows either. DY awards this her highest overall score: five scenarios, each placing the solver in the game's decision space, is the closest any puzzle in the batch comes to theme and mechanic being the same thing. The Onager ambiguity is the one unresolved construction fault.
