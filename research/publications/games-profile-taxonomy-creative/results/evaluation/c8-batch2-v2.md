## P08-one-aha.md — One Aha, Not Zero, Not Three

The puzzle delivers five factual clues with no instruction about what to do with the answers; the first-letter acrostic is the single aha, arriving organically when the solver writes down all five items and sees T-O-W-E-R align. DK appreciates the clean single-mechanism architecture and the structural economy, but notes the clues are somewhat clue-dense — the "aha" is entirely post-identification, which satisfies the contract without exceeding it. TS finds the entry point well-constructed and the deduction path unambiguous, though the mechanism requires zero chains of inference (each clue leads directly to its answer), which slightly undercuts the computer-test standard. DY values the invisible mechanism and the fact that the answer TOWER reframes the set thematically — you assembled siege and support items to spell a defensive structure — but notes the visual format (plain numbered list, no blank structure) leaves the discovery entirely in the solver's head, which works best for experienced puzzle-hunt solvers.

## P09-book-test.md — The Book Test

The puzzle is a fully explicit acrostic: clues are numbered, answer blanks are length-coded, first-letter boxes are labeled, and the five-letter assembly grid is provided at the bottom. The mechanism is announced upfront ("Take the first letter of each answer, reading top to bottom"), which trades discovery for accessibility. DK sees a competently constructed puzzle that fully satisfies the Book Test's solvability requirement — a solver with AoE2 knowledge can confirm every answer from the clue text alone — but the upfront mechanism announcement eliminates any aha, leaving the experience transactional rather than rewarding. TS notes the clue quality is solid and the answer-blank letter counts are a legitimate fairness feature, but the puzzle fails his computer-test standard: given the mechanism is named, a lookup script reading AoE2 item lists and matching clue facts would solve every entry without genuine deduction. DY finds the worked-format clarity appropriate for an introductory-tier puzzle but sees the explicit "First letter: ___" boxes as over-scaffolding that eliminates the visual resolution moment entirely — the puzzle tells you when you have won rather than letting you discover it.

## P11-eighty-percent.md — The 80% Rule

This puzzle is structurally identical to P08–P09 in mechanism, but its design merit is explicitly the 80% property: four resolved answers constrain the fifth to a single option (T_WER / TO_ER / TOW_R / TOWE_ all have only one plausible completion). DK is the natural champion here — this is his concept — and awards it well: the puzzle demonstrates exactly the structural virtue he advocates, giving partial-completion teams a path forward without cheapening the full-solve experience. TS finds the 80% property a legitimate design feature but notes it is a meta-structural property of the answer word TOWER, not an emergent feature of the clue construction — the designer got lucky (or chose wisely) with a distinctive five-letter word rather than engineering the interlock. DY has a mild objection: the solver who reaches the aha by pattern-completing to TOWER on four letters has bypassed the domain-knowledge requirement, which slightly undercuts the Reading Reward claim — though she acknowledges this is a feature, not a bug, for hunts targeting broad audiences.

## P13-framing-structural.md — Framing Is Structural

This is the most formally creative puzzle in the set: the title "Counter-Play" functions as a structural key that flips the solver's interpretive mode — clues describe the victims, not the perpetrators, and without the title the correct answers do not appear anywhere in the clue text. DK considers this the strongest design in the full batch: the title does genuine structural work (it is load-bearing, not decorative), and the solving experience is genuinely different from any other version — the aha is not "what do the first letters spell?" but "I've been reading these clues wrong." TS agrees the entry point is deliberately constructed (the clue frame actively misleads before reorienting) and finds this the only version that survives the computer test: no keyword in any clue directly indexes the correct answer. DY scores it highest on Riven because the domain content — counter relationships, unit matchups, conversion mechanics — is exactly what AoE2 knowledge produces, and the answer TOWER names the structure all five counter-play instruments are deployed against.

## P14-multi-deduction.md — Multiple Deduction Paths

Each clue provides three independent identifying facts (cost/resource type, build location or prerequisite, and battlefield role), so any two of three facts are sufficient to determine the answer. DK values the redundancy as a structural fairness feature — a solver who blanks on one fact can triangulate from the other two — and notes this satisfies the spirit of the 80% rule at the individual-clue level. TS finds the triple-fact structure exemplary from a construction standpoint: the puzzle has no clue that reduces to a single trivia point, and each fact is genuinely independent (you cannot derive the cost from the role or vice versa). DY's score is dragged down by Riven: the three-fact structure is impeccably fair but generic — a puzzle about Medieval II units or Civilization techs could use the exact same template. The mechanism is acrostic over a domain, not a mechanism that the domain uniquely enables.

## P17-computer-test.md — Snyder's Computer Test

This is the most demanding puzzle in the set: every clue withholds the answer's name and all direct keywords, requiring multi-hop inference chains. Clue 1 requires exhaustive elimination across all nine Monastery technologies to isolate Theocracy by its conditionally null effect. Clue 2 reaches Onager through "removes an obstacle owned by no player" without using the words trees, forest, or fell. Clue 3 requires matching a team bonus to a building to a unit class and then identifying the mismatch. Clue 4 traverses two hops of the counter triangle. Clue 5 isolates Redemption by reasoning about which conversion-target category is absent in a units-only game. DK and TS both award this the highest raw scores in the set — it definitively fails the computer test, requires genuine domain expertise, and the deductions are genuinely satisfying. DY gives it a 4 on Riven (not 5) because the clue architecture is logic-puzzle framing applied to AoE2 facts, rather than a mechanism that emerges from what AoE2 itself does.

## P20-voice-rules.md — The Voice Rules

Every sentence is in second-person present tense ("You see," "You upgrade," "You face"), short (under fifteen words), and addresses the solver as a player inside an active match. The discipline is prose-level: no question marks, no puzzle-meta commentary, no sentence exceeds fifteen words. The effect is a shift in register — the clues read like a commentator calling a live game rather than a reference list. DK finds the voice choice interesting but note that it introduces a mild solvability cost: "You see a massive siege weapon in late Imperial play. It hurls stones at castles" could describe Bombard Cannon or Trebuchet to a less experienced player. TS scores Fun and Elegance higher than average for the set because the brevity and voice create a genuine reading experience; each clue is a small scene, not a database entry. DY awards it a 4 on Riven because the "you are in the game" framing is thematically faithful to how AoE2 is actually played, making the voice choice structurally meaningful rather than decorative.

DY finds one redeeming element: the detailed construction notes in the solution section (extraction trace, interlock verification, fact verification, reading reward assessment) constitute excellent design documentation, even if they belong to the constructor's notebook rather than the solver's puzzle.
