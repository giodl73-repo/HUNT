## P08-one-aha.md — One Aha, Not Zero, Not Three

The mechanic lands cleanly: five factual AoE2 clues with no extraction instruction, and the aha arrives exactly once when the first letters resolve to TOWER. DK appreciates that the extraction is genuinely invisible until it arrives — this is a model acrostic because the mechanism isn't announced. TS notes the clues are tight and unambiguous; each answer is uniquely forced by the stated facts, and no clue doubles as the extraction hint. DY observes the answer TOWER reframes the five instruments of siege and defense that produced it, making the payoff thematically satisfying rather than arbitrary. The domain barrier (real AoE2 knowledge required) is the main difficulty source, which is appropriate for the target audience. No reviewer finds a fairness or construction flaw.

## P09-book-test.md — The Book Test

This is the over-explained control version of P08: the mechanism is stated upfront ("take the first letter of each answer"), answer blanks include letter-count guides, and a "First letter: ___" prompt follows each entry. DK's concern is direct — announcing the extraction before the solver starts converts a discovery into a procedure, trading the single aha for a checklist. TS agrees that the puzzle now has a uniqueness problem in spirit: any solver can complete the form without truly experiencing the mechanic. DY notes the visual layout (blank grids, letter boxes) is orderly but kills the surprise that makes the answer word feel earned. The clues themselves are solid and the extraction is verified, but the scaffolding overwhelms the design intent. Mechanically pass-worthy; experientially a step down.

## P10-dinner-party.md — The Dinner Party Test

A cleaner variation that makes clue 3 (Watch Tower rather than Woad Raider) to sharpen the thematic resonance — five implements of siege and defense spelling the word TOWER. DK finds this the strongest version thematically: using Watch Tower as the W answer means the answer word contains one of its own building blocks, which is an elegant self-reference. TS notes that the Watch Tower clue ("three-stage upgrade chain: this → Guard Tower → Keep") is uniquely forced and well-constructed. DY points to the Onager clue as the Dinner Party moment: the idea that a siege engine fells trees to cut corridors is surprising, game-true, and specific enough to generate genuine delight. No instruction given for extraction; discovery is intact. The best-constructed variant in this cluster.

## P11-eighty-percent.md — The 80% Rule

This version is explicitly engineered so that four of five answers lock the fifth by pattern-matching: T_WER, TO_ER, TOW_R, or TOWE_ each admit only one English word. DK validates the design claim — this is a structurally honest implementation of the 80% rule, where partial progress is meaningful rather than a dead-end. TS observes that each clue is still independently forced (three distinct facts per item), so the pattern-matching shortcut doesn't undermine clue integrity. DY finds the puzzle satisfying but notes that the 80% property is more valuable in a full hunt context than in a standalone; here it reads more as a design note than a felt experience. Solid construction, slightly less distinctive in isolation.

## P12-discovered-never.md — Discovered, Never Explained

The purest version of the acrostic form: five clues, five answer blanks, no instruction whatsoever. The mechanism is discovered by the solver when they write the answers and see the first letters align. DK calls this correct by design — explaining the extraction would eliminate the only non-trivial step the puzzle asks of the solver. TS notes the clue writing is economical and every answer blank provides the letter count, offering confirmation without spoiling discovery. DY's one reservation: without any indication that a final answer is expected, a solver unfamiliar with puzzle-hunt conventions might not know to look for the pattern at all — the puzzle assumes hunt literacy as a prerequisite. Within the intended audience, this is a clean pass.

## P13-framing-structural.md — Framing Is Structural

The title "Counter-Play" does genuine structural work: it flips the solver's interpretive frame from "what is this?" to "what defeats this?" so that the described victims (the castle, the archers, the Knights) become entry points rather than answers. DK finds this the most conceptually interesting variant — the framing change is load-bearing, not decorative, and removing the title would make the puzzle unsolvable as written. TS confirms that the solve path is direction-forcing: once you read "Counter-Play," each clue has exactly one answer; without it, the clues describe the wrong entities entirely. DY appreciates that the narrative voice (battlefield incident reports) is thematically coherent and sustains the fiction through all five clues. The War Elephant clue (clue 3) is the standout — unexpected and vivid. A strong puzzle.

## P14-multi-deduction.md — Multiple Deduction Paths

Each clue provides three independent facts so the solver can triangulate from any two if the third is forgotten. DK approves of this as structural honesty — it acknowledges that not every solver will recall every stat, and builds a second path in rather than relying on trivia recall alone. TS is the most enthusiastic: this satisfies his "can the solver confirm each step independently?" requirement without creating noise, because the three facts per clue are all load-bearing identifiers, not redundant padding. DY notes the puzzle is functional but slightly dry — the multi-fact format prioritizes robustness over narrative texture. The clues read like tech specs rather than battlefield moments. Solid construction; no elegance breakdowns, but lower fun relative to P10 or P13.

## P15-no-computation.md — No Computation Without Deduction

The leanest version: one identifying fact per clue, no numbers, no costs. Identification requires recalling a distinctive property rather than matching a stat to a table. DK finds this appropriately sized — nothing is over-specified, nothing is wasted. TS applies the Computer Test directly: a ten-line lookup script fails every clue because no clue contains a string that directly indexes the answer; each requires semantic reasoning about game mechanics. DY notes the brevity works in the puzzle's favor — it reads quickly and cleanly, and the Onager tree-felling fact and Trebuchet deploy-mechanic are both distinctive enough to carry the clue alone. The puzzle does exactly what it claims and no more. Not the most memorable variant, but flawlessly constructed.

## P16-interlock.md — Interlock, Not Independence

Clues 1 and 2 share a workshop; clue 5 explicitly points back to the answers of clues 1 and 2; clues 3 and 4 are mutually dependent (each defines the other's threat and counter). DK calls this the most architecturally sophisticated version — the clues are not five independent items but a connected system, which is closer to the best hunt puzzle construction. TS validates the interlock: identifying the Siege Workshop from clue 1 meaningfully constrains clue 2, and the clue 3/4 mutual dependency is well-constructed without being circular. DY finds the interconnection thematically satisfying — the puzzle models AoE2's rock-paper-scissors counter system structurally, not just descriptively. This is the design principle realized most fully in the P-series.

## P17-computer-test.md — Snyder's Computer Test

Every clue requires multi-step inference with key terms withheld: clue 1 isolates a Monastery technology by exhaustive elimination; clue 2 requires reasoning from "removes an obstacle owned by no player" to trees to Onager without those words appearing; clue 3 requires matching team bonus to building to unit class mismatch. DK identifies this as the hardest variant and notes that the difficulty is all technique-based — no noise, no misdirection, just deeper chains of forced reasoning. TS is the most satisfied reviewer here: this is exactly what the Computer Test was designed to identify, and every clue fails the test in the right direction (the inference cannot be scripted). DY flags that clue 3 is the weakest — the "team bonus speeds up a building whose unit class the civ's unique unit doesn't belong to" chain is cognitively dense and may not resolve cleanly for many solvers. Strong puzzle overall, with one clue at the edge of fairness.

## P20-voice-rules.md — The Voice Rules

Every sentence addresses the solver as "you" in present tense, creating a match-narration voice: "You upgrade this siege weapon from a Mangonel." DK finds the voice distinctive and notes it does real work — it creates a continuous diegetic world without flavor-text scaffolding. TS observes a construction issue in clue 3: "It costs no gold" is applied to the Woad Raider, but the Woad Raider does cost gold (65 food and 25 gold); the no-gold property belongs to the Elite Skirmisher. This is a factual error that would make the puzzle unsolvable for a knowledgeable solver. DY notes the voice is the most interesting formal property in the P-series but the error in clue 3 is disqualifying — a solver who knows AoE2 well enough to get the reading reward will also catch the mistake and lose confidence in the clues.

## L02-blame-omitted.md — Leave Out: Blame the Player

The critical fairness failure here is clue 4: to reach Elite Skirmisher, the solver must identify Woad Raider from clue 3, recall that infantry are countered by archers, recognize the Skirmisher line as the archer-counter, know the Elite Skirmisher is gold-free, and then name the upgrade rather than the base. That is four unstated reasoning steps with no confirming fact in the clue text. Additionally, clue 1 switches the T answer to Throwing Axeman (Franks unique unit) — a less iconic item than Trebuchet, with a clue so sparse ("throws its weapon," "stone walls do not stop it") that multiple answers are plausible. DK calls this the clearest failure in the L-series: a solver who gets the wrong answer on clue 1 or clue 4 cannot tell they're wrong, which breaks the solver-designer contract entirely. TS agrees — the absence of a unique forced path in clue 4 violates his core construction requirement. DY finds clue 1 evocative but ambiguous. Fails on fairness grounds.

## L03-riven-omitted.md — Leave Out: The Riven Standard

The puzzle is a well-constructed acrostic — clues are tight, the interlock via clue 3 (the civilization in clue 2 is Celts; clue 3 asks for that civilization's unique unit) is functional, and the extraction is clean. The omission notes are the most intellectually honest in the series: this puzzle demonstrates that an acrostic mechanism can be applied to any domain and does not require AoE2 understanding to be structurally valid. DK finds the puzzle passes on all mechanical grounds but misses the mark on the Riven Standard's deeper claim — solving it does not prove AoE2 knowledge in a way that only AoE2 knowledge enables. TS agrees: the domain is used as setting, not as proof. DY notes that without the Riven Standard, the puzzle is a well-made version of a generic template — solid but not distinctive. An honest study in what is lost when the mechanism is domain-agnostic.

## L04-proving-omitted.md — Leave Out: Solving = Proving Understanding

The clues are accurate and the answers are uniquely forced, but each item is identified primarily by cost, build location, or prerequisite — facts a non-player can confirm by lookup. DK observes that this is a structurally sound puzzle that fails to set a domain bar: a solver with no AoE2 knowledge who has access to a units database can complete it. TS applies the Computer Test: a lookup script that takes cost parameters and build location would solve clues 1-4 directly, which means the clues don't pass the test. DY notes the puzzle reads cleanly and the extraction is verified, but there is no moment where the solver must demonstrate they understand what Monks can or cannot do, or why Woad Raiders are competitively significant. Functionally adequate; intellectually thinner than the best P-series variants.

## L05-scaffolding-omitted.md — Leave Out: No Over-Scaffolding

This is the maximally scaffolded version: full mechanism explanation, worked example, labeled blanks with letter counts, a separate letter-assembly grid, an explicit hint, and a "Surprise the Answer" analysis embedded in the notes. DK finds this the most instructive failure in the batch — not because it doesn't work, but because every piece of scaffolding removes a problem the solver was supposed to encounter. The worked example alone tells the solver the extraction method before they read a single clue. TS notes the hint ("two of the clues share a civilization") is particularly costly — it hands the solver the most interesting interlock in the puzzle before they can discover it. DY observes that the visual design is thorough but the flavor text does the job the layout should have done. Passes mechanically; fails the "No Over-Scaffolding" criterion decisively.

## L06-surprise-omitted.md — Leave Out: Surprise the Answer

The clues are among the best-written in the entire batch — terse, vivid, mechanically accurate ("outranges a Keep," "cuts through trees to open corridors no axe can make," "arrives before the gate closes"). The problem is that all five clues describe components of a tower siege: an assault siege weapon, forest-breaching ordnance, a flanking infantry speed unit, an archer-screen unit, and a Monk siege-conversion tech. A knowledgeable solver will anticipate TOWER before completing the set. DK finds this a genuine design cost: the answer no longer surprises, which is the mechanism's primary reward. TS observes that the puzzle still requires real deduction (Onager clue is non-trivial), so it doesn't entirely degrade — but the payoff is attenuated. DY rates this the most elegantly written version in the series despite the telegraphing issue: the prose is the cleanest and most evocative in the batch. A near-miss on a principle that the writing almost compensates for.

## L07-reading-omitted.md — Leave Out: Reading Reward ≥ 4

The clues anchor to real-world name origins (Trebuchet as a medieval catapult type, Onager as a Roman war machine, War Elephant as a historical unit) rather than AoE2-specific mechanics. DK notes this produces a puzzle that is broader in appeal — a general history reader could make progress — but thinner in domain specificity. TS applies the Computer Test: clue 1 ("named after the famous counterweight catapult") is Google-searchable directly; clue 3 ("Persians' unique unit is a real animal") resolves without any AoE2 knowledge. DY observes that the answer changes (War Elephant and Eagle Warrior replace Woad Raider and Elite Skirmisher) and those items are less mechanically distinctive as clue subjects — the War Elephant HP clue is vaguer than the Woad Raider speed-record clue. The puzzle passes threshold but is the weakest acrostic in the series on reading-reward grounds.
