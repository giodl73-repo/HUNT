## L08-aha-omitted — Condition L08: Leave Out: One Aha

The puzzle wraps five AoE2 clues in a destroyed-records framing device and deliberately omits the "One Aha" design constraint, resulting in four stacked reward layers: per-clue identification, mechanism discovery (acrostic), TOWER extraction, and a retroactive resonance layer (every item has a mechanical relationship to towers specifically). The narrative framing is substantive — each record is voiced by a witness, and the prose choices (prisoner account, supply master testimony, monastery chronicle) carry authentic period texture that makes domain engagement feel earned. The five items chosen (Throwing Axeman, Onager, War Elephant, Elite Skirmisher, Redemption) are all non-obvious picks that require genuine AoE2 knowledge to identify from the oblique witness-account format, and the interlock between items and the tower theme constitutes a genuine Riven-standard achievement. The multi-layer aha structure is ambitious and the top two layers land cleanly; the bottom "retroactive resonance" layer depends on the solver spontaneously asking "why these five," which not all will do, leaving the deepest reward optional rather than structurally forced. Confirmation is solid — TOWER is a real word and a core AoE2 entity, so the answer clicks without ambiguity.

## L09-book-omitted — Condition L09: Leave Out: The Book Test

The puzzle is a clean five-item acrostic with explicit mechanism declaration ("The first letter of each item, in order, spells the answer"). The clues are competent and individually unambiguous for a player with wiki access, but the omission of the Book Test is acknowledged as a design choice — the puzzle is calibrated for solvers who will look up facts. Clue 4 relies on knowing the Skirmisher upgrade tier name and clue 5 on an exact gold cost, both wiki-lookup facts for casual players. The presentation is functional but stripped of the narrative layering and domain-texture that elevate L08; there is no framing, no voice, and no interlock — the five items are fully independent. The Woad Raider appears here (replacing the War Elephant from L08), giving a different TOWER assembly that is equally valid. The puzzle solves cleanly and confirms crisply, but the experience is closer to a vocabulary test than a puzzle — the mechanism is stated upfront, the domain knowledge required is straightforward, and the aha is muted.

## L11-eighty-omitted — Condition L11: Leave Out: The 80% Rule

This puzzle omits the 80% Rule and deliberately exploits that absence — the five items (Theocracy, Onager, War Elephant, Elite Skirmisher, Ring Archer Armor) are chosen to be individually obscure, and clues 4 and 5 are explicitly interlocked so that the two hardest identifications must be solved together. The explicit fill-in-the-blanks format ("Answer: _ _ _ _ _") invites pattern completion but the omission notes confirm the answer space is deliberately ambiguous for any four of five letters. The cross-clue interlock (clue 4 names the unit from clue 5 and vice versa) is the puzzle's structural highlight: it creates a genuine mutual-dependency deduction loop that rewards solvers who notice the circular reference. Ring Archer Armor is the most obscure item — a Blacksmith technology many players know by effect but not by name — and its clue is the weakest link in solvability. Dana Young would flag the absence of a visual resolution; Thomas Snyder would note the interlock is structurally sound; Dan Katz would worry about the 20% of solvers who cannot identify Ring Archer Armor and have no fallback.

## L13-framing-omitted — Condition L13: Leave Out: Framing Is Structural

The puzzle strips all thematic framing — no title, no scenario, no voice — and presents five numbered clues with explicit mechanism instruction. The clues themselves are notably strong: they use cross-clue references (clue 2 names clue 5's technology; clue 3 points to clue 4's counter role; clue 4 points back to clue 3's civilization) creating a dense interlock web that makes this one of the more structurally sophisticated instances in the batch. The items (Throwing Axeman, Onager, Woad Raider, Elite Skirmisher, Redemption) are identified through these mutual references, and a solver who follows the cross-links arrives at answers through genuine deduction rather than isolated lookup. The absence of framing is not a flaw here — the clue interlock is substantive enough to carry the puzzle. Dan Katz would note the structural coherence; Thomas Snyder would verify the interlock holds and find it sound; Dana Young would flag that without framing, the visual/contextual entry layer is absent. The puzzle tests its own claim: that the clues and interlock are sufficient without decoration.

## L14-deduction-omitted — Condition L14: Leave Out: Multiple Deduction Paths

The puzzle omits the Multiple Deduction Paths principle and delivers on that omission precisely — each clue contains exactly one identifying fact with no redundant constraints. Clue 1 ("This siege weapon must unpack before firing") is the starkest example: a single mechanical property, no backup. The result is a puzzle that is maximally brittle for solvers who lack that one specific fact and maximally clean for solvers who have it. The five items (Trebuchet, Onager, Woad Raider, Elite Skirmisher, Redemption) are identified via minimal single-fact clues that read almost like flashcards. This is the most pared-down puzzle in the batch — the clues are short, unambiguous in isolation, and require no cross-referencing. Thomas Snyder would identify this as the puzzle that most clearly fails his "is there a puzzle here or a procedure" test — the deductions are real but trivial for anyone with domain knowledge, and a single-fact lookup table would solve every clue mechanically. Fun is low; elegance is acceptable only because the minimalism is consistent.

## L15-computation-omitted — Condition L15: Leave Out: No Computation Without Deduction

This puzzle omits the "No Computation Without Deduction" principle and delivers arithmetic as the primary identification mechanism throughout. The design choice is explicit and structurally interesting: clues 3 and 4 are interlocked through a shared numerical value (Woad Raider's 25 gold cost equals Elite Skirmisher's 25 food cost), which means the two clues must be solved together — a genuine mutual-dependency deduction that elevates the puzzle above pure lookup. Clue 1 uses a unique cost figure (200 wood, no other siege unit), clue 2 uses cost-matching between an item and its predecessor. The computation layer requires access to the AoE2 cost data table, and the puzzle is honest about this. Thomas Snyder's concern would be that arithmetic against a reference table is computation, not deduction — the "hard work" is looking up numbers, not inferring a hidden structure. Dan Katz would note that the clues 3/4 interlocking numerical relationship is the strongest element and represents genuine puzzle craft. The puzzle sits above the floor because the interlocking numerical dependency is non-trivial, but below the ceiling because most identification is ultimately table lookup.

## L19-coordinate-omitted — Condition L19: Leave Out: Coordinate Answer Words Early

This puzzle omits the "Coordinate Answer Words Early" constraint and allows the five items to be discovered during clue-writing rather than pre-assigned. The result is a notable structural bonus: Trebuchet and Onager share a cavalry vulnerability (clues 1 and 2 both note cavalry as their natural counter), and Redemption connects back to both siege entries (clue 5 explicitly refers back to "the units in entries 1 and 2"). This organic cross-reference web — achieved without pre-coordination — produces a puzzle where the items feel thematically and mechanically related rather than arbitrarily assembled around an acrostic. The clue for Elite Skirmisher (clue 4) contains the most interesting constraint: it names the base Skirmisher as trainable from Feudal Age but names the Castle Age upgrade, and adds that "the upgrade removes its only notable weakness against the melee units it must avoid" — a secondary mechanical fact that adds texture without over-constraining. Dan Katz would find the organic interlock satisfying; Thomas Snyder would note the cavalry-vulnerability callback between clues 1 and 2 is the cleanest deduction web in the batch; Dana Young would appreciate that the answer feels inevitable.

## L20-voice-omitted — Condition L20: Leave Out: The Voice Rules
