## L08-aha-omitted.md — Condition L08: Leave Out: One Aha

The narrative frame here earns its length: five witness testimonies, each requiring two or three inferential steps rather than keyword matching, and DK notes that every item has a structural relationship to towers in AoE2 that a solver who asks *why these five* will discover after extracting TOWER — a retroactive resonance aha that genuinely rewards the curious. TS finds the entry-point construction deliberate: each clue withholds the name and forces deduction from observable behavior, passing his computer test comfortably since no script can triangulate "throws before defenders raise the drop-bar" to Throwing Axeman without significant inferential work. DY calls the framing structurally integrated rather than decorative — the witness-testimony conceit is the puzzle's world, not a label pasted on top — and approves of the layered confirmation, where TOWER lands first and the tower-mechanic theme lands second, each reward distinct. The puzzle's main risk is density: five clues of this length at this knowledge depth may exhaust solvers before they reach extraction, but the multi-path confirmation structure gives an answer before the thematic resonance is required.

## L09-book-omitted.md — Condition L09: Leave Out: The Book Test

This is the cleaner, wiki-friendly version of L08's premise: five identification clues stated plainly, mechanism declared upfront, clues short and precise. DK finds the structure honest — solver is told what to do, clues are resolvable via the Civilopedia, and the puzzle makes no false promises about depth — but notes that the puzzle has no slot-justifying distinctiveness; it functions but does not surprise. TS observes that clue 3 (Woad Raider's speed 1.03) is the sharpest factual pin, while clue 4 requires knowing the Skirmisher upgrade tier name, which a non-wiki solver cannot derive — the Book Test omission is acknowledged and the puzzle is honest about requiring reference access. DY's concern is that the omitted Book Test removes any conversation-stopping moment; this puzzle is correct and forgettable, and she questions whether it earns its slot over L08 or L11. Confirmation is clean because the mechanism is pre-stated; elegance is modest because the clues are competent rather than crafted.

## L10-dinner-omitted.md — Condition L10: Leave Out: The Dinner Party Test

Three clues here ("cannot attack a moving target," "Mesoamerican civilizations field this in place of cavalry") are distinctive enough to reward AoE2 familiarity without demanding wiki consultation, but the puzzle's own Omission Notes acknowledge no showstopper moment exists. DK sees a functional first-letter acrostic — mechanism clear, clues unambiguous, TOWER resolves cleanly — but applies his "does every puzzle justify its slot" test and finds no affirmative answer: this puzzle could be removed without affecting the experience it is embedded in. TS notices that clue 3 (Woad Raider, speed 1.03) provides a cross-reference hint to clue 2's civilization bonus, a small interlock that elevates it marginally above L17, but the deduction layer is thin throughout. DY finds the Mesoamerican cavalry-substitute clue (Eagle Warrior) the most interesting identification and wishes more clues had that lateral-knowledge character; the extraction is clean but the reading reward is low.

## L11-eighty-omitted.md — Condition L11: Leave Out: The 80% Rule

This is the hardest puzzle in the batch from an identification standpoint: Theocracy, War Elephant, Ring Archer Armor are genuinely obscure, and the cross-clue interlock between clues 4 and 5 (each naming the other's item) means the two hardest letters must be solved together. DK's concern is precisely the stated omission — TOWER is not pattern-completable from any four letters, and a team stalled on Ring Archer Armor has no graceful degradation path; the 80% Rule exists to prevent exactly this brittleness. TS is more forgiving: the interlock between clues 4 and 5 is structural design, not noise-based difficulty, and the cross-reference constraint is load-bearing in the way he respects. DY appreciates the clue for War Elephant (the irony sentence — "classified as cavalry, and Halberdiers exist precisely to destroy cavalry" — is the reading reward of the batch), but the Ring Archer Armor clue is dry and the extraction confirmation feels mechanical after that high point.

## L12-discovered-omitted.md — Condition L12: Leave Out: Discovered, Never Explained

The answer-length blanks (e.g., "_ _ _ _ _ _ _ _   _ _ _ _ _ _" for Throwing Axeman) are the puzzle's distinguishing feature: they confirm correct identifications and rule out wrong ones without stating the mechanism, allowing TOWER to emerge from writing down initials. DK considers this the correct design choice — the mechanism is discoverable rather than reported, which is structurally honest — and the blanks act as lightweight checkpoints that satisfy his confirmation-economics concern. TS notes that the blanks solve his uniqueness-path problem: the solver cannot accidentally arrive at the right initial from a wrong identification because the letter count would mismatch. DY's only complaint is that clue 5 (Ring Archer Armor, "third and final tier of Blacksmith armor for the unit class that clue 2 specializes in destroying") requires the solver to already know what the Onager specializes in destroying to identify the armor class — a dependency that the blank partially mitigates but does not eliminate.

## L13-framing-omitted.md — Condition L13: Leave Out: Framing Is Structural

Five clues, mechanism stated, no title, no narrative wrapper, no thematic context — a stress test of whether the clues carry the puzzle without framing. They do, but only barely: DK finds the cross-clue interlocks (clue 2 pointing to clue 5 via Redemption convertibility, clue 3 pointing to clue 4's Cavalry Archer counter) intelligently placed and structurally load-bearing, which partly compensates for the absence of any entry context. TS approves of the interlock structure but notes that without framing, the puzzle's visual hierarchy places five identical-weight clues on equal footing — the designed entry point (clue 1, Franks cavalry HP bonus as a confirming signal) does not read as primary. DY is the most critical: the puzzle's own Omission Notes admit framing is absent as a verification exercise, and she finds a puzzle that deliberately withholds the world it lives in harder to inhabit — the reading reward suffers most.

## L14-deduction-omitted.md — Condition L14: Leave Out: Multiple Deduction Paths

Five clues, each containing exactly one identifying fact with no fallback — the stated design intent and its honest execution. DK's concern is structural: a solver who does not know that the Trebuchet must unpack before firing has no second route to T, no compensating constraint, and no recovery path; the puzzle is a binary pass/fail on five independent knowledge checks. TS identifies this as the Computer Test failure case he would cite in a critique — every clue is a direct key-value lookup against AoE2 data, and a script with the reference tables would achieve 100% accuracy with no deduction required. DY finds the clues serviceable but bare; the one-fact-per-clue constraint strips any reading reward because there is nothing to infer, only to recall. Confirmation is mechanically clean (first letters, mechanism pre-stated) but the experience is closer to a quiz than a puzzle.

## L15-computation-omitted.md — Condition L15: Leave Out: No Computation Without Deduction

The interlocked cost clues (clues 3 and 4 share a numerical value: Woad Raider's 25G = Elite Skirmisher's 25F) are the structural highlight; DK notes this cross-referencing is exactly the kind of mutual confirmation that makes acrostics feel integrated rather than procedural. TS is split: the arithmetic is precise and the constraints are individually load-bearing (200W unique among siege for the Trebuchet, the Onager's training-cost invariance across upgrade tiers), but the puzzle's own notes admit that a script with reference tables could resolve all five clues mechanically — no deduction layer is required, only lookup and comparison. DY approves of clue 3's interlocking elegance but finds clue 1's "no other siege unit costs exactly 200W" constraint dry reading; she would want the numerical hook embedded in a world detail rather than presented as a stat comparison. Confirmation is strong due to the mutual cost anchors.

## L20-voice-omitted.md — Condition L20: Leave Out: The Voice Rules

The descriptive, essay-style register is the point of this puzzle and the notes are transparent about it: past tense narration, rhetorical framing ("watch the choke points disappear"), meta-commentary linking clue 5 back to clue 1 ("the same targets that the first item on this list was designed to destroy"). DY finds the voice warmer and more inviting than any other puzzle in the batch — clue 3's "Historians might call it the fastest foot soldier on any battlefield" is the kind of register that earns solver goodwill — and the explicit clue-5-to-clue-1 link is a structural grace note rather than over-scaffolding. DK's concern is exactly the meta-commentary: calling out the connection explicitly ("designed to destroy") is doing work the solver should discover, and it converts a confirmation moment into a given; this is a mild form of the wheel-of-fortuning failure. TS finds clue 3's speed claim ("faster even than a Scout Cavalry unit can move on foot") factually imprecise — Scouts are not infantry — but the reading reward is the highest of the batch alongside L08.
