# C8 Expert Panel Evaluation — Designer Comparison
## Conditions: Snyder / Katz / Young × A5 (Full Authoring) / A6 (Reviewer-as-Author)

**Rubric:** /45 weighted — Clarity ×1, Solvability ×1, Elegance ×2, Reading Reward ×2, Fun ×1, Confirmation ×1, Riven Standard ×1
**Pass threshold:** ≥ 33
**Panel:** Dan Katz (DK), Thomas Snyder (TS), Dana Young (DY)
**Note:** All six puzzles produce the same answer (TOWER) via first-letter acrostic; comparison isolates designer profile effects, not format or domain.

---

## A5-full-authoring-profile.md — "First Letters" — Designer: Snyder — Mode: A5 (Full Authoring Profile)

Snyder's authoring profile produces exactly what his review lens demands: a puzzle whose entire visible surface is mechanism. The solver-facing section is seven numbered clues plus a two-sentence instruction — no title flavor, no narrative frame, no ambient world-building — which satisfies his "theme is structure" principle but at the cost of inhabiting the AoE2 world in any meaningful way. Katz notes that the clue variety is adequate (unique unit → siege → cavalry-replacement → anti-archer → monastery tech) and the short-circuit risk is managed, but the answer word TOWER is visible at the TOW_ stage with no real resistance from clue 4 (Elite Skirmisher is the expected E-answer for anyone who knows the counter chart). Young's lens finds the visual grammar correct but cold — the table structure signals "worksheet" rather than "game world," and the clue for the Woad Raider is missing entirely in favor of the Elite Skirmisher, meaning the puzzle never evokes the sensory surprise of watching infantry move at cavalry speed. The confirmation moment (TOWER as the spelled word) is clean but thematically inert — the answer names a building that appears in none of the five clues, arriving as pure extraction rather than earned culmination.

## A6-reviewer-as-author.md — "Escalating Force" — Designer: Snyder — Mode: A6 (Reviewer-as-Author)

The A6 Snyder puzzle is structurally almost identical to A5 but with one significant difference: the reviewer-as-author notes reveal an honest acknowledgment that the format exposes a limit in the coherence standard ("the extraction mechanism and the domain content are loosely coupled by design, and Snyder's coherence standard exposes that gap rather than resolving it"). Katz observes that swapping War Elephant (W) for Woad Raider reintroduces a near-collision risk — the Woad Raider is better known as a speed anomaly than as a primary siege threat, and the clue for clue 3 leans on the speed statistic rather than a structural role, making it the weakest clue in constraint logic. The title "Escalating Force" is a light thematic gesture — the sequence moves from Trebuchet to Onager to Woad Raider to Elite Skirmisher to Redemption — but Snyder's own lens, applied faithfully, shows this arc is "thematic texture, not structural dependency," which is an honest self-indictment rather than a design achievement. Young notes that the addition of the title elevates the puzzle marginally for first-impression engagement but does nothing to the visual grammar or the extraction's meaningfulness. The puzzle is cleaner per Snyder's injected principles (the Trebuchet's minimum-range weakness is a sharper distinguishing clue than A5's Throwing Axeman entry point), but the honest acknowledgment of the format's structural limit caps the ceiling.

## A5-katz.md — "Five Clues" — Designer: Katz — Mode: A5 (Full Authoring Profile)

The Katz A5 puzzle demonstrates the most architecturally conscious clue construction of the six: the short-circuit check is documented explicitly (TOW_ is visible after clue 3, and clue 4 deploys Eagle Warrior as the unexpected E-answer to earn the confirmation), and the backwards-reading test applied to each clue produces forced uniqueness throughout. Katz's own lens awards it highest because the structural thinking is visible in the solver-facing text — clue 3 (Wonder) breaks the unit-siege-unit pattern deliberately, and the jump from siege mechanics to an alternate win condition shows mechanical variety that Snyder's profile would not have generated. Young notes that the table-free prose format creates a different reading experience: the clues are miniature narratives ("Aztec and Mayan armies have no horses at all. This unit fills the cavalry role instead"), and the solve feels like reading field notes rather than checking a database. The one genuine weakness is the Eagle Warrior's data-file absence — the construction log flags this honestly ("not listed in local units.md which covers 8-civ scope only"), and a solver who knows only the scenario's world data cannot verify it without outside knowledge, which is a solvability risk Snyder would refuse to accept and Young would call an ambush. The confirmation moment lands cleanly: Redemption as the final answer makes a solver think "of course" — the Monastery tech that converts enemy siege into your own is a clean TOWER payoff.

## A6-katz.md — "WOLOLO: Five of a Kind" — Designer: Katz — Mode: A6 (Reviewer-as-Author)

The A6 Katz puzzle is the strongest of the six by total score and the most cohesive application of reviewer principles to authoring practice. The title "WOLOLO" is the only puzzle title in the set that is itself an AoE2 artifact — the Monk conversion sound — which immediately situates the solver inside the game world before the first clue is read, satisfying Young's "enterable without thematic knowledge, richer with it" standard. Katz's explicit application of the Computer Test (eliminating Theocracy and Watch Tower because a ten-line script would find them trivially, retaining Trebuchet and Woad Raider because their clues require cross-referencing properties) produces the tightest clue set of the six puzzles: every distinguishing property is load-bearing in exactly the way Snyder's lens demands. The reviewer-as-author notes identify the one genuinely new observation — that holding the variety constraint actively during authoring is a different cognitive load than retrospective assessment — which is a methodologically honest finding that A5 Katz and all three Snyder puzzles do not produce. The Woad Raider clue (speed 1.03, Celtic identity, "outrunning every other foot soldier") is the most individually pleasurable clue in the set because the speed specificity gives the solver an exact datum to confirm. The confirmation (TOWER as a "defensive structure central to AoE2 strategy") is the only puzzle where the answer is named in the setup text, which is a transparency choice that converts what would be a mechanical extraction into a pointed reveal.

## A5-young.md — "FIELD REPORT" — Designer: Young — Mode: A5 (Full Authoring Profile)

The Young A5 puzzle is the most visually distinctive of the six: the bordered table format with explicit "First letter: ___" blanks beneath each clue cell enacts the AoE2 interface's own visual grammar, making the extraction mechanism visible as spatial structure rather than instructions. Each clue is a different register of AoE2 expertise — classification paradox, physical terrain behavior, sensory speed anomaly, structural/lore knowledge, strategic transformation — and the construction log's justification for each choice is grounded in the experience of surprise rather than the mechanics of the database. Katz gives it above-average marks for the short-circuit management (the TOW_ stage appears after Woad Raider, but the Eagle Warrior gap again introduces solvability risk for data-constrained solvers), and the confirmation moment is stronger than either Snyder puzzle because the construction log explicitly argues TOWER is "inevitable" — it names what the solver was doing (scouting five positions and holding ground to see clearly). Snyder finds the visual grammar elements genuinely effective at communicating structure — the two-column layout separates reading from extraction cleanly — but the "First letter: ___" line inside each cell creates a minor redundancy with the final "Read the five first letters downward" instruction. Young's profile produces the only puzzle whose construction log articulates a theory of the answer word as experience-naming, which is her stated design endpoint; whether that theory is convincing as a puzzle property rather than an authorial intention is what separates Katz's positive verdict from Snyder's more skeptical one.

## A6-young.md — "HOLDING THE LINE" — Designer: Young — Mode: A6 (Reviewer-as-Author)

"Holding the Line" is the most narratively coherent puzzle in the set — second-person present tense, a single game being played from Castle Age construction through the Imperial Age siege, every clue a decision moment rather than a fact retrieval. The reviewer-as-author notes identify the structural impossibility precisely: Young's most distinctive instincts (images as primary material, layout as communicative act, visual resolution) have no purchase in a five-clue text acrostic, and the puzzle is transparently aware of this gap. What survives the translation is the world-building instinct — TOWER as the answer is not just five letters but the thing the solver was building narratively across all five clues — and the second-person frame is the closest a text-only format can get to embodying the world rather than describing it. Katz's lens finds the item choices unusual (Treadmill Crane for T, Watch Tower for W instead of the Woad Raider used by Katz and Young A5) and identifies a genuine clue quality risk: Treadmill Crane is an economic technology whose puzzle role is almost entirely functional (it builds walls faster), and the clue for it, while evocative ("every second of construction is a second you cannot afford to lose"), is the least distinguishing in the set — a solver who doesn't know Treadmill Crane will have difficulty arriving at it without the cost data being provided. Snyder flags the Watch Tower clue as the weakest in the set for a different reason: the clue describes it as "the beginning of every fortification chain in the game," which is thematic texture rather than a forced deduction. Young's own lens awards the puzzle its highest individual score in this set for the answer's inevitability — TOWER is what you were building all along — but acknowledges the visual resolution question cannot be answered for this format.

## Key Findings

**Does the A5 > A6 gap vary by designer?**

Yes, dramatically — and the direction of the gap reverses for Katz. Snyder shows near-zero gap (−0.7): his review lens is so thoroughly systematized that the authoring profile and the reviewer-as-author condition produce essentially the same puzzle, because both are executing the same checklist. The checklist is excellent but it generates the same output regardless of the condition. Young shows a moderate negative gap (−2.7): her A5 puzzle benefits from uninhibited application of her world-building instinct, while her A6 puzzle is constrained by honest self-awareness that her most powerful reviewer tools (visual grammar, image selection, layout hierarchy) have no purchase in a text-only acrostic. The constraint acknowledgment, rather than freeing her to do something different, produces a puzzle that tries to apply inapplicable principles and then documents the failure. Katz shows the largest gap, and it is positive (+5.6): his reviewer-as-author condition substantially outperforms his full-authoring-profile condition. This is the only case in the set where using the reviewer lens during authoring improved the puzzle rather than merely reproducing or constraining it.

**Which designer shows the largest A5→A6 gap? Which shows the smallest?**

Largest gap: **Katz** (+5.6 in favor of A6). The reviewer-as-author condition activated specific cross-referencing behaviors (Computer Test, Last Mile verification, Blame the Player backward-reading) that produced tighter clues than the authoring profile's more diffuse structural checklist. Smallest gap: **Snyder** (−0.7, essentially zero). The Snyder authoring profile and the Snyder reviewer profile are near-identical in behavioral terms — both execute a completeness-and-uniqueness checklist — so the mode distinction produces no meaningful output difference.

**Which reviewer lens transfers most cleanly to authoring? Which transfers least cleanly?**

**Most cleanly: Katz.** His review lens is built from named, discrete structural tests — short-circuit check, backwards read, variety scan, blame test — each of which has a direct authoring-time analog. A reviewer asking "can this meta be short-circuited?" becomes an author asking "does my clue 4 resist TOWER being guessed after clue 3?" The translation is one-to-one. Moreover, the Computer Test (eliminate answers a script could find trivially) is purely generative during authoring: it tells you which candidates to discard and which to keep, which is exactly the kind of forward-facing constraint an author needs. The result is that Katz's A6 puzzle is not just competent — it is the best-scored puzzle in the set, suggesting that his reviewer lens was developed with implicit authoring utility already embedded.

**Least cleanly: Young.** Her review lens is optimized for puzzles where images are primary material, layout is communicative, and visual resolution is a design goal. All three of these properties are inapplicable to a text-only acrostic format. Her A6 notes identify this gap explicitly and honestly ("Young's most distinctive design instincts simply have no purchase here"), but that self-awareness does not produce a different puzzle — it produces a puzzle with the same format and an attached explanation of why the format is wrong for her. The gap is not a failure of skill; it is a mismatch between lens and format. If Young were authoring a puzzle in a format with visual components (image identification, layout-dependent extraction, grid-based structure), her reviewer lens would likely transfer as cleanly as Katz's — possibly more so, given her 25-year visual design practice. The lesson is that her lens is highly format-specific in a way that Katz's and Snyder's are not, and a format-constrained experiment like this one systematically disadvantages her.

**Additional observation — Snyder's A6 honest concession:**

The most intellectually interesting moment in the six puzzles is Snyder's A6 reviewer-as-author note acknowledging that his coherence standard "exposes that gap rather than resolving it" in an acrostic format. This is a reviewer evaluating his own authoring and finding the puzzle structurally wanting by his own criterion — and then publishing the finding. In panel terms, this honesty reads as a negative signal (it surfaces a flaw the solver would not otherwise have noticed) rather than a positive one. Snyder's A6 scores slightly below his A5 despite the more sophisticated clue set, which is the consequence of a designer who cannot pretend his own standard has been met when it hasn't. That integrity is a reviewer virtue; it is a mild authoring liability in conditions where the format itself imposes the limitation.
