## A0 — The Indexed Garrison

### Dan Katz
The structural promise here is simple and it's kept: five clues, five letter-indexed extractions, five letters spell a word. What concerns me architecturally is clue 4 — "third tier in the defensive tower upgrade line, sitting above the Guard Tower" — which describes the Keep cleanly, but the position extraction [2] gives us 'E' from K-E-E-P. The puzzle is solvable, but the clue language is slightly redundant (we already know it's above the Guard Tower if it's the third tier). More seriously: does every puzzle in this hunt justify its slot? This one does, barely — it earns its place as a clean introduction to AoE2 knowledge. The mechanism is as transparent as a mechanism can be, which is a deliberate choice for accessibility but not an excuse for weak clues. Clue 3 ("Every player starts the game with one of these") produces TOWN CENTER, and position [3] gives W — the extraction math works, but "every player starts with one" is the most publicly known AoE2 fact imaginable; it passes, but just. The structural promise is kept; the puzzle does what it says.

### Thomas Snyder
The letter-indexing mechanism is clean and uniquely solvable — I can trace each extraction step independently, and nothing branches. What I cannot overlook is clue 4's ambiguity risk: the puzzle describes the Keep as "the third tier in the defensive tower upgrade line, sitting above the Guard Tower," which is accurate, but a solver who knows AoE2 history might pause on whether Bombard Tower sits in or outside this line. The puzzle's own solution key acknowledges this parenthetically ("Bombard Tower is a separate gunpowder-era siege structure, not a continuation of this line"), which means the constructor knew the ambiguity existed but placed it in the design notes rather than resolving it in the clue itself. That is a load-bearing failure: the uncertainty should be closed by the clue text, not by the solution key. The Snyder Computer Test is survivable here — a lookup script could index into the correct answers, but identifying which AoE2 entity matches each description requires genuine domain parsing. One path, one answer per clue, extraction verified letter-by-letter: T-O-W-E-R checks out.

### Dana Young
The visual grammar is absent — this is a table with column headers and underscore blanks, which is functional but communicates nothing about AoE2 as a world. The layout doesn't speak before the content does; it just organizes the content. That said, the extraction step earns its place: letter indexing is a transformation that requires both knowing the answer and counting into it, which is a genuine two-part action rather than pure lookup. The TOWER payoff is satisfying because it names an object that actually exists in the game and resonates as an AoE2 concept, but the puzzle makes no visual case for why TOWER is the right destination — the answer feels discovered rather than inevitable. The five clues together don't build a world-image; they survey game systems in isolation. A solver who finishes this puzzle knows five AoE2 facts and a six-letter acrostic variant. The extraction mechanism (position indexing) is the most interesting element here, and it's buried in a column header.

## A1 — FIRST PRINCIPLES

### Dan Katz
This is an acrostic extraction puzzle with a title that does mild misdirection work — "First Principles" signals both the mechanism (first letters) and the thematic register (foundational AoE2 knowledge). The structural contract is clean: five clues, five first-letter extractions, no hidden steps. Every puzzle in a hunt needs to justify its slot, and this one does so by testing a genuine spread of AoE2 knowledge domains: siege mechanics (Trebuchet), siege upgrades (Onager), unique units (Woad Raider), alternative unit lines (Eagle Warrior), and map objects (Relic). That breadth is the puzzle's strength. Clue 4 — the Eagle Warrior — is interesting: it tests the knowledge that Mesoamerican civs replace cavalry with this foot unit, and that the unit is immune to anti-cavalry bonuses. That is genuinely AoE2-specific knowledge that would require an experienced player, not a casual lookup. The answer TOWER doesn't obviously follow from these five items, which means the extraction arrives as a small surprise. Structurally sound; the clues don't depend on each other, which keeps solve paths clean.

### Thomas Snyder
The clues here are tighter than A0 — each one isolates a single definitive mechanic. "Packed before it can move" is the Trebuchet and nothing else; "Relic...generates a trickle of gold" is a Relic and nothing else. What I'm testing is load-bearing: if I remove "immune to the bonus damage that halberds and camels deal to horses" from clue 4, do I still land on Eagle Warrior? Not necessarily — without the immunity qualifier, "foot unit that replaces cavalry for three civs" might admit broader answers. The phrase is load-bearing. More importantly: clue 4 is the hardest clue in the set, requiring knowledge of the cavalry-replacement mechanic AND the anti-cavalry immunity exception, which is intermediate-to-advanced AoE2 knowledge. The puzzle's difficulty curve is honest — it's one hard clue in a set of four accessible ones, which is appropriate. The Snyder Computer Test: a lookup script with the right AoE2 database could find all five, but the Eagle Warrior clue requires cross-referencing three separate facts (civilization, unit class, damage exception), making it genuinely resistant to naive text matching. Solve path is linear and forward; no branching.

### Dana Young
The title does real work here — "First Principles" is a conceptual double-bind: it names the extraction mechanism (first letters) while also suggesting that these are the foundational pieces of AoE2 strategy. That's thematic integration without being clever for its own sake. The five clues span a wider world than A0 — Relics, Eagles, Onagers, and the Trebuchet's pack-move mechanic all feel like genuine fragments of AoE2 experience rather than encyclopedia entries. The Relic clue in particular reads like something a player would actually remember from a lost game: "a Monk who carries one back to a monastery generates a trickle of gold...a high-priority contest in Castle Age." That's world-building through mechanism. The TOWER payoff is thematically appropriate but still not inevitable — none of the five items are towers, which is either a smart misdirection (DK's territory) or an unresolved visual gap depending on your expectations. For me, "inevitable" means the answer reframes everything before it. TOWER does that for an AoE2 player (tower rushes, stone defenses) but only if you're already in the game's world.

## A2 — Stone and Steel

### Dan Katz
The five clues span what the title promises — units, upgrades, and technology across AoE2's major systems — and each one contains enough discriminating detail to eliminate wrong answers. My structural concern is with the instruction line: "Take the first letter of each answer, in order, to spell a two-word defensive structure that every civ can build." This is over-scaffolded. "Two-word defensive structure that every civ can build" narrows the answer space dramatically before any clue is solved — a solver who knows AoE2 might immediately think WATCH TOWER, GUARD TOWER, or TOWN WALL, and the knowledge that it's five letters would eliminate those and point toward TOWER before any clue work. This is short-circuiting the extraction by design. Whether that's a problem depends on whether you think the clue-solving is the puzzle or the extraction is the puzzle. I think both should require effort; scaffolding the answer space this aggressively tips the balance toward the clue-solving alone. Mechanically clean, but the instruction is doing work it shouldn't.

### Thomas Snyder
Clue 1 introduces an immediate problem: "The Franks' unique unit is classified as infantry — yet it attacks from range, hurling its namesake weapon over walls and past obstacles." The Throwing Axeman is a correct answer, but the clue's most distinguishing phrase ("throws its namesake weapon") relies on a meta-reference — the unit's name contains the description of its attack. That's cute, but it's not a constraint; it's a reveal. A solver who doesn't know the unit name might not be able to work backwards from "throws its namesake weapon" to "Throwing Axeman" without already knowing the answer. This is an entry-point failure: the clue is easier if you already know the answer than if you're reasoning toward it. The other clues are well-constructed — clue 3's War Elephant (600 HP, trample, extremely slow) is a clean multi-constraint identification. Clue 5 (Redemption) is uniquely identifiable by its effect. The Verify Last Mile check is clean: T-O-W-E-R spells directly from first letters.

### Dana Young
"Stone and Steel" is a better title than "First Letters" but it's doing double work that doesn't resolve: stone references the Castle-building currency and possibly the Watch Tower line, while steel references military production. The title creates a frame that neither confirms nor complicates the answer — TOWER is consistent with "stone" (towers are stone structures) but the title doesn't point you there. The instruction line's "two-word defensive structure that every civ can build" is the visual grammar failure I look for: it tells you what the layout should have communicated. If the design could have guided you to the two-word nature of the answer through the visual structure — five clues yielding a recognizable structure name — you wouldn't need to state it. The Throwing Axeman clue has a self-referential quality that I find clever but borderline unfair to a solver who hasn't heard the unit name; it asks you to recognize the pun rather than reason to the answer. The War Elephant clue is the strongest: "600 hit points, trample, extremely slow" is exactly how AoE2 players describe the unit to each other.

## A3 — CHAIN OF COMMAND

### Dan Katz
This is the first puzzle in the set with a genuine structural ambition beyond pure extraction. The interlock between clues 2 and 3 — where clue 3 references "the workshop that produces the unit named in clue 2," creating a hard dependency — is exactly the kind of architectural detail that separates a puzzle from a collection of clues. That dependency does real work: solving clue 2 (Onager at the Siege Workshop) gives you a constraint to apply to clue 3 (the Celts' team bonus reduces Siege Workshop training time), and vice versa. This is structural elegance. The title "Chain of Command" does mild misdirection — military hierarchy rather than letter chain — without being dishonest; that's the right use of title misdirection. My one concern is the "trash unit" terminology in clue 4 — this is community jargon rather than in-game text, and a player who learned AoE2 from the manual rather than the community might not recognize the term. The puzzle assumes community-facing knowledge, which may be appropriate for its target audience but isn't noted anywhere.

### Thomas Snyder
The clue 2–3 interlock is the puzzle's most interesting construction choice, and I want to test it precisely. Clue 3 states: "Its civilization's team bonus reduces the training time at the same workshop that produces the unit named in clue 2." This means clue 3 cannot be confirmed without first identifying clue 2. A solver who works bottom-up and tries clue 3 first must hold "fastest infantry at 1.03 speed" as the primary constraint, identify the Celts, confirm their team bonus involves a workshop, and then cross-check that against clue 2. That path works but requires more working memory than the designed top-down path. The question I ask is: does this interlock create a branching solve path? I don't think it does — the cross-constraint only confirms, it doesn't block. Both clues have sufficient independent constraints to reach the answer; the interlock is a verification layer, not a dependency gate. That's the right use of interlock: it rewards the attentive solver without punishing the non-linear one. Clue 4's phrasing ("hard-counters the unit class it itself evolved from") is the recursive surprise the design notes identify, and it holds up — archers evolve into Elite Skirmishers which kill archers.

### Dana Young
"Chain of Command" is the strongest title in the set so far: it signals military hierarchy (misdirection for the solver) and names the mechanism in retrospect (a chain of first letters commanding you to read TOWER). The title resolves the moment the answer lands, which is exactly the reframing I look for. The five clues also have the most kinetic energy of any version so far — "opening new attack corridors through impassable forest," "hard-counters the unit class it evolved from" — these read like tactical dispatches, not encyclopedia entries. The thematic frame of BREACH (the next puzzle, but relevant here in contrast) is present embryonically: each clue describes something that cuts through or outflanks a defense. TOWER as the answer isn't just an AoE2 building; it's the defense being breached, which gives the answer a retroactive meaning. The extraction is still first-letter acrostic, which limits visual resolution — there's no moment where the puzzle resolves visually, only textually. For me, that's the format's ceiling, not this version's failure.

## A4 — BREACH

### Dan Katz
BREACH is tighter than CHAIN OF COMMAND on clue construction but looser on structural interest. The five clues are each single-mechanism identifications: pack/unpack (Trebuchet), tree destruction (Onager), 1.03 speed (Woad Raider), counter-archer (Elite Skirmisher), Monk buildings+siege (Redemption). These are clean, but they're also stripped of the structural interplay that made CHAIN OF COMMAND memorable. Every clue here is fully independent, which means there's no cross-constraint to reward the attentive solver. The title BREACH does more thematic work than CHAIN OF COMMAND — each item is genuinely a tool for breaching defenses — but the structural elegance is lower. My concern is clue 4: "The Archery Range unit that is specifically designed to hard-counter other archers" could admit the base Skirmisher as a valid answer, not just the Elite. A solver might correctly identify Skirmisher and extract 'S', breaking the final answer. This is an ambiguity the clue doesn't resolve — "Elite Skirmisher" requires distinguishing the two, but the clue doesn't force the Elite.

### Thomas Snyder
Clue 4 is the critical failure: "The Archery Range unit that is specifically designed to hard-counter other archers" describes both the Skirmisher and the Elite Skirmisher. The distinction matters for the extraction — 'S' versus 'E' — but the clue contains no constraint that forces the Elite version. The design notes in A3 addressed this ambiguity with "Castle Age upgrade of the Skirmisher," but BREACH drops that qualifier. This is a uniqueness problem at the structural level: the solve path branches at clue 4, producing either TOWSR or TOWER, and a solver who correctly identifies Skirmisher rather than Elite Skirmisher will get a wrong answer through no fault of their own. The Blame the Player test fails: a solver who writes 'S' for clue 4 would not blame themselves after seeing the answer key — they'd blame the clue for not specifying Elite. The rest of the puzzle is clean and fully load-bearing. Clues 1, 2, 3, and 5 each contain the minimum necessary constraints and no decorative language. But a single unclosed ambiguity is a construction failure, full stop.

### Dana Young
The title BREACH is doing the most thematic work of any puzzle in the set: it names what each of the five items does (breaches a defense), it misdirects toward the word itself as a possible answer, and it retroactively frames TOWER as the thing being breached. When TOWER lands, you read back through the five items as five breach mechanisms, and the answer feels earned. That's the visual resolution I look for — not literally visual, since this is text, but conceptual: the answer reframes everything before it. The clue writing is the most spare and forceful in the set: "incapable of attacking while moving," "unlike any other siege engine, can destroy trees," "fastest infantry in the game, with a movement speed of 1.03." These are declarative, not explanatory. The Skirmisher ambiguity (DK and TS both caught it) is real, but from a reader's perspective the damage is in the solving experience: if you write "Skirmisher" and get a wrong extraction, the TOWER aha is replaced by a correction, which kills the reward. Ambiguity at clue 4 is a structural defect, but BREACH is still the best-titled puzzle in the set.

## A5 — First Letters

### Dan Katz
The title is mechanical, not thematic — it names the mechanism explicitly and nothing else. That is the safe choice for puzzle hunt accessibility (no one is confused about what to do), but it sacrifices the misdirection dividend that CHAIN OF COMMAND and BREACH collected. Every element here is clean: five independent clues, five first-letter extractions, verified letter-by-letter. The construction log in the solution key is the most rigorous in the set — the author tested each answer against all competing items beginning with the same letter, found only one T-item satisfying "ranged infantry that throws its weapon," one O-item in the whole dataset, one W-item matching the HP record, etc. That verification discipline shows in the clue quality. My sole concern is clue 1: "The only infantry unit in the game that attacks from range — it throws its weapon" is excellent, but it presupposes the solver holds a mental model of all infantry units and can confirm that none of the others are ranged. An experienced AoE2 player does hold that model, so it's appropriate, but it makes clue 1 the hardest entry point in the set for a solver who isn't certain about edge cases (is a Hand Cannoneer infantry? the answer is no, but a solver might pause). The Throwing Axeman as entry point rewards Frankish civs knowledge specifically.

### Thomas Snyder
This is the best-constructed version in the set by the criteria I apply. The construction log documents every step of the verify-uniqueness process: for each letter, all competing items are enumerated and eliminated. The Throwing Axeman's uniqueness is verified against Trebuchet, Treadmill Crane, Two-Handed Swordsman, Theocracy, and Todai-ji. The Onager is the only O-item. The War Elephant's constraints eliminate the Cataphract as a distractor. The Elite Skirmisher ambiguity (A4's failure) is resolved here with three simultaneous constraints: no-gold, anti-archer, Castle Age researched upgrade — each constraint is individually eliminatory. Every phrase in every clue is tested for whether it can be removed without creating ambiguity, and the construction log shows which phrases survived the cut and why. The solve path is fully self-contained: no clue depends on another, all five can be solved in any order, and the extraction is a direct first-letter sequence. The Snyder Computer Test: a lookup script could find the answers, but clue 4's three-part constraint requires cross-referencing three data points simultaneously, which is not a trivial script — it needs a proper query structure. Genuinely finished puzzle.

### Dana Young
The title "First Letters" tells you everything about the mechanism and nothing about the world. Compared to BREACH or CHAIN OF COMMAND, it reads like a label rather than an invitation. The clue writing is the most precise in the set — "600 at elite tier, with trample damage, but extremely slow" is exact AoE2 player vocabulary — but precision without texture leaves the puzzle feeling like a verification test rather than an experience. The War Elephant clue is the closest thing to world-building here: you can picture the slow, enormous creature lumbering across the map, absorbing everything thrown at it. The Throwing Axeman clue is the most interesting in the set: "attacks from range — it throws its weapon" contains the Frankish cultural logic (axe-throwing warriors) inside the mechanical description. The extraction to TOWER is verified precisely and the answer is appropriate, but the title's decision to name the mechanism rather than the world means TOWER arrives as a confirmed answer rather than a revelation. The puzzle passes every quality test; it just doesn't try to be more than it is.

## A6 — ESCALATING FORCE

### Dan Katz
ESCALATING FORCE is clean and fully functional, but it represents a design ceiling rather than a design advance. The clue set (Trebuchet, Onager, Woad Raider, Elite Skirmisher, Redemption) is the same as A3/A4/A6 — the same five items, reclued. The Reviewer-as-Author section acknowledges the structural honesty: this format's extraction mechanism (acrostic) is loosely coupled to the domain content, and the reviewer-transposed-as-author found the Snyder coherence standard ("does the theme shape the structure?") exposes a structural gap rather than resolving it. For me, that acknowledgment is both impressive and concerning: it's impressive that the author named the format's limit, and concerning that the format's limit was accepted rather than challenged. The Trebuchet clue here adds cost information (200W/200G) that A3/A4 didn't include. That's a Verify Last Mile addition — the cost gives the solver an independent verification path — but it also makes the clue slightly more complex without increasing difficulty. The title does adequate double-work: "escalating force" names both a military escalation sequence (siege → upgrade → infantry → counter-unit → tech) and the act of reading the answer word letter by letter.

### Thomas Snyder
Clue 4 is the most carefully constructed Elite Skirmisher clue in the set: "This trash unit — the Castle Age upgrade of the Skirmisher — counters archers with a direct attack bonus and costs no gold to produce, unlike its target." The dash-introduced appositive "the Castle Age upgrade of the Skirmisher" does exactly the right work — it closes the Skirmisher/Elite Skirmisher ambiguity by making the Elite designation explicit and parenthetical, so a solver cannot stop at "Skirmisher" without ignoring the qualifier. This is the construction fix that BREACH needed and didn't apply. Clue 1 adds cost information (200W/200G) as an independent verification path, which the Reviewer-as-Author section justifies with "can the solver confirm each step independently?" — that's the right reason to retain additional data, and I note it would survive my strip-decorative-elements test because it eliminates the Catapult Trebuchet variant (different cost structure) as a theoretical distractor. Every clue is load-bearing. The Verify Last Mile check: T-O-W-E-R, traced character by character, holds. This is the most precisely constructed version of the Elite Skirmisher fix in the series.

### Dana Young
The Reviewer-as-Author section does something no other puzzle in the set does: it shows the work of applying a specific critical lens to authoring and names where that lens fails. The admission that "for an acrostic extraction puzzle, the answer word is structurally inert relative to the five clues" is the most honest sentence in the entire A0-A6 set. I've been waiting for one of these puzzles to acknowledge that TOWER doesn't inevitably emerge from any arrangement of AoE2 items — it emerges from a letter coincidence. That admission doesn't make the puzzle worse; it makes the author's choices legible. The title "Escalating Force" describes an arc (siege weapon → upgrade → infantry → counter-unit → tech) that the Reviewer-as-Author section calls "thematic texture, not structural dependency." That honesty is valuable to me as a reviewer, even though the puzzle itself doesn't resolve it. The clue writing is confident and clean. The TOWER confirmation still feels like discovery rather than revelation, but the meta-transparency about why that is the format's ceiling rather than this puzzle's failure earns the puzzle credit in ways I don't usually give.

## Key Findings

- **The gradient is not monotonic but has a recognizable shape.** A0 is the weakest (19.3), A4 is an anomalous dip (21.3) despite being positioned mid-sequence, A3 / A5 / A6 form the top tier (24.7 / 26.0 / 26.0). The weakest puzzles are the ones with the least explicit framework — A0 (bare) and A4 (Snyder philosophy without sufficient verification applied to clue 4).

- **The Elite Skirmisher ambiguity is the single most consequential recurring defect.** It surfaces in A0 (as a Keep/Bombard Tower variant), A2 (as a naming-pun risk with Throwing Axeman), A4 (as an unclosed Skirmisher/Elite Skirmisher gate), and is resolved in A5/A6 with multi-constraint anchoring. Thomas Snyder caught the sharpest form of this failure in A4 and applied the Blame the Player test precisely: a solver who writes "Skirmisher" for clue 4 and gets the wrong answer cannot be blamed, which means the design failed.

- **Over-scaffolding damages clarity scores more than under-specification does.** A2's instruction line ("two-word defensive structure that every civ can build") short-circuits the extraction by narrowing the answer space before any clue is solved — this is a different kind of failure from A4's ambiguity, but it depressed A2's scores to the same level. Dan Katz and Dana Young both identified it independently.

- **Structural interlock (A3) and construction-log discipline (A5) are the two paths to the highest scores.** A3's cross-constraint between clues 2 and 3 earned the highest panel average among puzzles without the construction log, while A5's exhaustive verification against competing items earned the highest individual score (Thomas Snyder: 28/30). These are distinct approaches to puzzle quality that produce comparable results.

- **Title misdirection is the clearest differentiator between good and excellent versions.** BREACH and CHAIN OF COMMAND both use their titles to do thematic double work (signal mechanism in retrospect, misdirect pre-solve), while "First Letters" (A5) names the mechanism directly at the cost of thematic reward. The title choice doesn't affect Clarity, Solvability, or Elegance scores but meaningfully affects Fun and Confirmation — the two dimensions where A5 shows its only weakness.

## Evaluative Frameworks Applied

**A-ha Economy** (DK's lens — one aha moment, well-timed)
Applied to A0, A1, A3. In A0, the letter-indexing mechanism is the aha, but it fires weakly because the mechanism is visible in the table headers before solving. In A1, the aha is the moment TOWER assembles from five unrelated AoE2 items — appropriately singular and well-positioned. In A3, the secondary aha (Woad Raider's team bonus interlocks with the Onager clue) adds a smaller second moment inside the primary extraction, which DK praised as structural elegance rather than clutter.

**Load-Bearing Test** (TS's lens — strip each element; if the puzzle still works, the element was decoration)
Applied most rigorously to A4 and A5. In A4, the absence of "Castle Age upgrade of" in clue 4 fails the load-bearing test — the Elite qualifier cannot be removed from the answer without breaking uniqueness, but it was never placed in the clue. In A5, the construction log documents strip-testing every phrase explicitly; the "extremely slow" qualifier for War Elephant survived because it eliminates the Cataphract as a high-HP cavalry distractor.

**World-Model Consistency** (TS + DY — does the puzzle inhabit a coherent game world or select trivia from a database?)
Applied to A0, A2, A3, A5. A0 feels like a database query (five facts, five positions, five letters). A3 feels most like the game world — "opening new attack corridors through impassable forest" and "hard-counters the unit class it evolved from" read as tactical knowledge, not lookup values. A5 is the most carefully verified against the world model but doesn't translate that verification into texture.

**Social Fabric** (DK's lens — does the puzzle hold at the table? Can teams collaborate on it, or does it require one specialist?)
Lightly applied to A1 (the Eagle Warrior clue is the hardest, isolating Mesoamerican civ knowledge to one possible team member) and A5 (the Throwing Axeman as entry point privileges Franks players). Neither puzzle fails this test, but both have one clue that concentrates knowledge rather than distributing it.

**Perceptual Shift** (DY's lens — does the answer reframe everything before it?)
Applied to all seven puzzles on the Confirmation dimension. BREACH (A4) achieves the strongest perceptual shift — the five items read as five breach mechanisms once TOWER is known, and TOWER becomes the defense being breached. CHAIN OF COMMAND (A3) achieves it second — the "chain" of command resolves as a literal letter chain. "First Letters" (A5) achieves the weakest shift because the title announces the mechanism, leaving no retroactive reframing to occur.

**Visual Grammar** (DY's lens — does the layout communicate structure, or does the text do work the layout should do?)
Applied primarily to A0 (table layout is functional but doesn't speak before content does), A2 (instruction line compensates for absent visual hierarchy), and A5/A6 (plain numbered list format — neutral, neither aiding nor obstructing). A0's table format was flagged by DY as doing nothing thematic. A2's instruction line was flagged as doing the layout's job in text, which is the clearest visual grammar failure in the set.
