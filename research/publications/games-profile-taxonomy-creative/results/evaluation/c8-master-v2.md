### Highest-scoring single principles

**P13 — Framing Is Structural (41.0):** The clear standout. A load-bearing title that inverts the clue-reading mode, making the mechanism non-separable from the domain content. All three reviewers scored it at 39+. The mechanism cannot be transplanted to another domain without losing its function.

**P07 — Reading Reward (40.7):** Scenario-framing that puts the solver inside an active game decision rather than asking them to recall facts. DY scored it 44/45, the highest single-puzzle score in the entire P-series. The "you are in the game" framing is the closest any P-puzzle came to a full Riven Standard score.

**P04 — Solving = Proving Understanding (39.7):** Multi-step game knowledge required for each clue — not lookup, but internalized tactical understanding. All three reviewers scored 39–40. The puzzle cannot be solved without having played (or deeply studied) the game.

**P03 — Riven Standard (39.0) and P17 — Computer Test (39.0):** Tied in score, complementary in spirit. The Riven Standard principle selects for gameplay-native clue content; the Computer Test selects for inferential depth. Together they form the core of what makes a domain-integrated puzzle feel authored rather than assembled.

**P16 — Interlock, Not Independence (38.0):** Cross-clue dependencies that make clues stronger as a unit than in isolation. Riven scored at 4 because the interlock is generated by game logic (siege workshops, counter relationships, conversion targets), not imposed arbitrarily.

### Lowest-scoring single principles

**P06 — Surprise the Answer (26.3):** The lowest score in the entire P-series and among the lowest in the full table. This is a construction failure, not a principle failure: clue 5 describes Block Printing but the solution key names Redemption — an AoE2-literate solver who knows both techs extracts B instead of R, breaking the puzzle entirely. Clarity and Confirmation each scored 1.0 from TS and ~1.7 across the panel. The principle itself is sound; the implementation is fatally flawed.

**P01 — No Deliberate Errors (31.3):** Paradoxically, the safest-sounding principle produced the weakest puzzle after P06. The bracket/blank format (prepended answer blanks per clue) tells the solver exactly where to write before they have discovered the mechanism, regressing in Elegance relative to the better A-series entries. The principle is about construction discipline, which is invisible when facts happen to be correct — what's visible to the scorer is the scaffolding apparatus.

**P09 — Book Test (30.3) and P10 — Dinner Party Test (32.7):** Both announce the mechanism upfront ("Take the first letter of each answer in order"), trading the discovery moment for accessibility. The Book Test explicitly states the mechanism; the Dinner Party version is richer in clue prose but still announces. Both fail because the Elegance dimension penalizes any puzzle that removes the mechanism discovery from the solver's experience.

**P11 — 80% Rule (32.0):** A legitimate structural virtue (four resolved answers constrain the fifth) that nonetheless depresses Reading Reward because a solver who pattern-completes TOWER on four letters has bypassed the domain knowledge requirement. The 80% property is emergent from the answer word choice, not from clue engineering.

### Principles whose removal hurt most

**L07 — Reading Reward omitted (-12.5 drop):** The largest single-principle damage in the set. Without domain-relevant clue content requiring genuine play experience, all three reviewers unanimously scored 25/45. This is the only puzzle where DY, DK, and TS gave identical scores across every dimension (5/5/2/2/2/4/1). Removing Reading Reward collapses Riven (to 1.0) simultaneously because the two dimensions are co-dependent: clues that can be looked up by anyone who can use a search engine fail both tests at once.

**L17 — Computer Test omitted (-10.5 drop):** TS scored this 23/45, the lowest individual reviewer score in the entire batch. The clues are resolvable by direct keyword-to-item lookup with no inferential gap. The joint DK/DY score of 29 held the average up; TS alone dragged it to 27.0. The Computer Test's absence makes the puzzle feel procedural rather than crafted, collapsing Elegance (TS: 1/5) and Fun (TS: 1/5).

**L14 — Multiple Deduction Paths omitted (-10.5 drop):** Single-fact clues with no redundant constraints. The puzzle is maximally brittle for a solver who lacks the one specific fact — there is no fallback. Elegance and Fun both bottomed out. The principle protects solvers; its absence is felt as unfairness, which reviewers penalize across multiple dimensions.

**L02 — Blame the Player omitted (-10.2 drop):** Clue 4 chains four implicit reasoning steps without a confirming fact at any step, creating genuine ambiguity between "Elite Skirmisher" and "Skirmisher" (base unit). Without a fairness check, an AoE2 player who reaches the wrong Skirmisher tier cannot self-correct from the clue text. Confirmation dropped to 2.0 (DK/DY) and Solvability to 3.0.

### Principles whose removal had negligible effect

**L18 — Verify the Last Mile omitted (-0.5 drop):** The smallest negative drop in the set, barely below baseline. The Last Mile verification is a construction-side discipline that is invisible when no extraction error exists — and no extraction error existed. The puzzle scored 37.0, indistinguishable from the baseline. This confirms that Last Mile verification is a risk-mitigation principle, not a quality-adding one: its value is actualized only when an error is caught.

**L19 — Coordinate Answer Words Early omitted (+0.5 above baseline):** Counter-intuitively, omitting this principle improved the score. The organic process of discovering items during clue-writing rather than pre-assigning them produced a cavalry-vulnerability callback between clues 1 and 2, and an explicit cross-reference in clue 5 — an emergent interlock richer than what a pre-coordinated word selection achieved. The principle is about reducing wasted effort; its omission here produced a better puzzle by accident.

**L12 — Discovered, Never Explained omitted (+1.5 above baseline):** Also above baseline. The puzzle withholds the mechanism instruction and uses answer-length blanks as the only scaffolding, producing a genuine discovery moment. The score improvement (39.0 vs 37.5) reflects that this puzzle correctly executed what A5/A6 were trying to do.

**L08 — One Aha omitted (+3.2 above baseline):** The highest L-series score. Multi-layer reward structure (per-clue identification, mechanism discovery, TOWER extraction, retroactive thematic resonance) did not fragment the experience — the top two layers landed cleanly and the third was optional. The "One Aha" principle may be constraining rather than enabling at the high end of authoring quality: more sophisticated solvers can handle (and prefer) layered rewards.

A0 through A6 show a clear upward trend as more authoring context is added. The expected ordering from worst to best is: A0 < A2 < A1 < A3 < A4 < A5 < A6. The actual ordering largely confirms this with one exception: A2 (28.3) scores below A0 (28.0) — essentially tied — rather than above it, and A1 (32.7) falls below A3 (34.7) as expected.

The A2 result is the only genuine inversion. A2 (domain expertise emphasis) produced clues that feel like verified quiz questions rather than evocative descriptions, triggering the Computer Test failure: a 10-line lookup script resolves every clue against a game database. Adding domain expertise to the prompt without also adding the authoring philosophy and Riven Standard constraints produces a puzzle that is objectively accurate but experientially flat. This is counter-intuitive and important: more domain knowledge in the prompt does not automatically improve puzzle quality.

The A1 result is not an inversion — A1 (task spec only, 32.7) sits between A0 and A3, which is the expected position. The task specification alone adds genuine clue quality (jargon, community knowledge) but lacks the structural interlocking and Riven Standard awareness that A3 provides.

**A4, A5, A6 form a clear top tier.** All three pass (36.7, 37.3, 37.7). The differences between them are small (within 1.1 points), attributable primarily to DY's scoring, which rewards A6's self-aware meta-commentary and A5's minimal presentation. TS scores A4, A5, and A6 identically (40/45 each), suggesting diminishing returns above the A4 threshold.

**Format ceiling:** All seven A-series puzzles are scored by Riven Standard at 2–3/5. The first-letter acrostic format cannot exceed this ceiling regardless of clue quality, because the mechanism is imported from puzzle-hunt tradition and does not emerge from the AoE2 game system itself.

## 5. Key Findings

### Which principles matter most for authoring quality

- **Reading Reward (P07: 40.7, L07 drop: -12.5) is the single most impactful principle.** It correlates with both Riven Standard and Fun because clues that encode genuine play experience rather than retrievable facts are simultaneously harder to fake, more enjoyable to solve, and more domain-native. P07 and L07 form the clearest causal pair in the dataset.

- **The Computer Test (P17: 39.0, L17 drop: -10.5) and Solving = Proving Understanding (P04: 39.7, L04 drop: -7.8) are complementary depth gauges.** P17 is the most rigorous clue-level test: a clue that passes it cannot be solved by keyword lookup. P04 is the domain-level version: solving the puzzle cannot be done without having internalized the game's mechanics. Both matter independently; removing either collapses Elegance and Reading Reward together.

- **Framing Is Structural (P13: 41.0, L13 drop: -0.8) is the highest-ceiling principle but surprisingly fragile to remove.** P13 achieves the top aggregate score in the dataset because a load-bearing title creates a mechanism inseparable from domain content. However, L13 (framing omitted) still scores 36.7 because strong clue interlock carries the puzzle even without the framing layer. The principle adds a ceiling; it does not form the floor.

- **Multiple Deduction Paths (P14: 35.0, L14 drop: -10.5) and Blame the Player (P02: 35.0, L02 drop: -10.2) are complementary fairness guarantors.** Both produce similar P-scores in the mid-35 range, but their L-drops are among the largest. This is the key pattern for structural principles: they do not produce spectacular puzzles on their own, but removing them causes catastrophic failures in Solvability and Confirmation. They are necessary but not sufficient.

- **Interlock, Not Independence (P16: 38.0, L16 drop: -2.8) is the clearest craft differentiator at the high end.** A puzzle with strong interlock is meaningfully better than one without it; but a puzzle without interlock is still functional. Interlock elevates from good to excellent rather than preventing a fall from passing to failing.

### Which principles are redundant (high P score AND negligible L drop)

- **Verify the Last Mile (P18: 33.3, L18 drop: -0.5):** The most purely redundant principle in quality terms. It is a construction-discipline backstop rather than a quality-adding design choice. When applied correctly — with no extraction error to catch — it produces a puzzle indistinguishable from one without it. Value is entirely in error prevention, not quality elevation.

- **No Deliberate Errors (P01: 31.3, L01 drop: -5.2):** Slightly less redundant than Last Mile — the L01 drop is meaningful (-5.2) — but the P01 score is surprisingly low (31.3, a failing score). The principle is necessary but its isolated application triggers scaffolding (bracket/blank format) that penalizes Elegance. As a standalone principle it hurts as much as it helps.

- **The 80% Rule (P11: 32.0, L11 drop: -2.8):** The principle is a structural property of the answer word choice more than a design act; both the P and L scores are middling. It adds resilience for teams that can't solve one clue, but its isolation does not produce noteworthy quality and its absence does not cause dramatic failure.

### Which principles hurt when added alone (low P score)

- **P01 — No Deliberate Errors (31.3):** When applied in isolation, the principle manifests as defensive scaffolding (answer blanks, redundant constraint statements) that reduces Elegance significantly. A puzzle that is trying hard not to be wrong looks, in presentation, like it doesn't trust its solver.

- **P09 — Book Test (30.3) and P10 — Dinner Party Test (32.7):** Both principles, when applied alone, require announcing the mechanism upfront to demonstrate solvability or showstopper quality. The announcement trades discovery for legibility — a poor trade for solvers who want to find the mechanism themselves.

- **P11 — 80% Rule (32.0):** When applied alone it reduces Reading Reward because pattern-completion bypasses the domain knowledge requirement for the final letter.

- **P06 — Surprise the Answer (26.3):** This fails for construction reasons rather than principle reasons. The principle as stated is sound (the answer should not be telegraphed by clue content); the implementation introduced a factual error that broke the extraction. The principle is not vindicated or condemned by this score — the execution failed, not the idea.

### The A5 vs A6 result on the new rubric

A5 scores 37.3 and A6 scores 37.7 — a gap of 0.4 points. Under the old /30 rubric (six dimensions without the Riven Standard), TS and DK awarded A4, A5, and A6 identically; DY's score was the only differentiator. On the new /45 rubric, the pattern persists: TS scores A4/A5/A6 at 40 each, DK scores them at 38/38/38. DY is the sole differentiator (32/34/35 for A4/A5/A6), and her progressive upward scoring for A6 reflects the self-aware meta-commentary that she finds editorially honest.

The practical conclusion is that A5 and A6 are statistically indistinguishable on either rubric. The reviewer-as-author mode (A6) is marginally better on the new rubric only because DY credits its editorial transparency, and because A6's construction notes surface the explicit disambiguation on clue 4 (Elite Skirmisher vs Skirmisher) that A5 leaves implicit.

### The Riven Standard dimension's effect on rankings vs the old /30 rubric

The new Riven Standard dimension (×1, max 5 points) materially reorders the rankings in two ways:

**First, it depresses the entire A-series and most of the P-series.** The first-letter acrostic format earns Riven scores of 2–3 across all 20+ puzzles that use it without structural framing. Under a /30 rubric, A4/A5/A6 were the clear winners by a comfortable margin over P01–P07. Under /45 with the Riven dimension, P07 (scenario framing, Riven 4.3) and P04 (multi-step game knowledge, Riven 3.7) now challenge the A-series top tier on equal footing. The Riven Standard reweights authenticity of domain integration relative to construction discipline.

**Second, it separates P13 and P07 from everything else in the P-series.** P13 (Framing Is Structural) achieves Riven 5.0 — the only puzzle in the set to do so. P07 (Reading Reward) achieves 4.3. These two are the only P-series puzzles whose mechanisms are domain-native rather than domain-applied. Under a /30 rubric both would have been strong performers but would not have separated from P16, P17, and P04 as clearly as they do under /45.

**Third, the L-series reveals that Riven Standard is co-dependent with Reading Reward.** L07 (Reading Reward omitted) earns Riven 1.0, the lowest in the batch. The two dimensions are not independent: you cannot have gameplay-native clue content (Riven) without also having clues that require genuine play experience to decode (Reading Reward). This co-dependence suggests the rubric could compress these two dimensions or explicitly mark their correlation.

**Caveat:** The Riven Standard dimension disadvantages structurally sound puzzles that use imported mechanisms (acrostics, anagrams, crosswords) applied to domain content. P09/P10/P11 score 2.0 on Riven not because they are bad puzzles but because the first-letter mechanism predates AoE2 by decades. Any hunt that chooses a traditional mechanism will accept a Riven Standard ceiling of 2–3 regardless of clue quality. Tournament constructors should treat Riven Standard as a ceiling dimension for mechanism choice rather than a clue-quality signal.
