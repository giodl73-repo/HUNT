## 1. A0-bare.md — The Indexed Garrison

A0 uses positional letter-indexing (e.g., CASTLE[4]=T) — a mechanism that works logically but fails the craft test: the index numbers appear to be chosen to produce TOWER, not derived from any game property, making the design feel reverse-engineered from the answer. Snyder flags this immediately as a procedure rather than a puzzle — once you accept the table format and the positions, a script extracts the answer in seconds. Katz notes that every clue is serviceable but the letter-position table is genre furniture, not a contract with the solver: nothing about the index numbers rewards AoE2 knowledge. Young observes that the five clues cover real game breadth (landmark, religion, economy, defense, siege) but the extraction mechanism is decorative relative to the domain — AoE2 knowledge identifies the items, but arbitrary index positions harvest the letters, severing the link between knowledge and answer.

## 2. A1-task-spec.md — First Principles

A1 upgrades to a first-letter acrostic, which removes the arbitrary-index problem. The five clues are well-targeted and span the game meaningfully (siege, siege upgrade, unique infantry, unit-line concept, map object). Snyder appreciates that the Trebuchet's pack/unpack mechanic and the Relic's gold-trickle function are specific and not Google-trivial. Katz notes the variety is adequate but not deliberate — the five categories feel assembled rather than architected, and the answer TOWER arrives with low surprise given how many AoE2 puzzles might land on defensive structures. Young finds the clue texture more evocative than A0 (the Relic description has a small narrative beat) but notes the first-letter mechanism is still presented as a procedure to execute, not a world to inhabit.

## 3. A2-domain-expertise.md — Stone and Steel

A2 is the domain-data puzzle: every clue is tagged with a citation from the world data files, and the factual accuracy is high. The Throwing Axeman (ranged infantry paradox), Onager (tree-cutting), War Elephant (600 HP), Elite Skirmisher (no-gold anti-archer), and Redemption (Monk building conversion) are all well-chosen and verifiable. Snyder finds the citation architecture reassuring — no deliberate errors — but observes that the clues read like excerpts from a reference table rather than constructions: "A Monastery technology costing 475 gold that extends the Monk's conversion ability" is the database row with punctuation added. Katz rates the structural variety higher than A1 because the five items span unique unit, siege, cavalry, archery, and technology — genuinely different game systems — but notes the clue quality is technically correct without being memorable. Young flags clue 3 (War Elephant, 600 HP) as the only one with a tactile surprise — "highest hit points of any unit" is the kind of claim that makes a player lean in — but the others stay at informational distance.

## 4. A3-principles.md — Chain of Command

A3 introduces the first genuine structural innovation in the set: clue 3 (Woad Raider) explicitly references "the workshop that produces the unit named in clue 2" — a hard cross-clue dependency that means clues 2 and 3 constrain each other. This is not decoration; a solver who tentatively identifies Onager (Siege Workshop) can use that as a confirming anchor when the Woad Raider's civ bonus is described via its team bonus. Snyder rates this significantly higher than A0–A2 because the interlock introduces a genuine deduction chain. Katz notes the principle-compliance section documents the design intent clearly but also observes that the five items (Trebuchet, Onager, Woad Raider, Elite Skirmisher, Redemption) are now appearing for the third time in the set — this is not yet visible to the solver but represents a design constraint that limits how much the puzzle can surprise. Young finds "CHAIN OF COMMAND" doing mild but honest misdirection (hierarchy not letter chain) and appreciates the recursive bite of clue 4 ("hard-counters the unit class it evolved from").

## 5. A4-authoring-philosophy.md — Breach

A4 is the leanest puzzle in the set: five short, stripped clues, no ornamentation. The philosophy application note is honest — each phrase was tested for load-bearing status, and the title "BREACH" does double work (thematic spine + inferential signal about the answer). Snyder approves of the strip discipline: "must unpack before it can fire" alone identifies the Trebuchet, and nothing else in the clue is decorative. Katz observes that BREACH as a title is the best misdirection of any puzzle in the A0–A6 Snyder group — the solver is told they're breaking defenses, then discovers the answer is a defense — but notes the five items are again the same four-puzzle subset (Trebuchet, Onager, Woad Raider, Elite Skirmisher, Redemption), limiting the ceiling on surprise. Young notes clue 5 is the strongest single clue in A4: "Monastery technology that extends conversion to buildings and siege weapons, not just military units" has a genuine "wait — the Monk can do THAT?" quality for a newer player, which is the closest thing to a dinner-party fact in this puzzle.

## 6. A5-full-authoring-profile.md — First Letters

A5 (Snyder full profile) is the most thoroughly documented puzzle in the set: step-by-step construction log, per-letter uniqueness verification, instinct-avoidance checklist. The items shift partly — Throwing Axeman returns instead of Trebuchet, War Elephant replaces Woad Raider — and the verification is exhaustive. Snyder grades the construction rigor highly; the cross-referencing uniqueness checks (other T-items listed and eliminated, other W-items considered) represent genuine quality control. Katz's concern is structural: "First Letters" as a title names the mechanism directly, which is honest but inert — it doesn't open any inferential space or reward the solver for engaging with the domain before the extraction. Young notes the solver-visible puzzle is minimal and clean but the title is the weakest in the Snyder group — it tells the solver exactly what to do before they've had a chance to discover it, which removes one layer of aha. The item set (Throwing Axeman, Onager, War Elephant, Elite Skirmisher, Redemption) is well-chosen and verifiable; the puzzle is technically excellent but experientially modest.

## 7. A6-reviewer-as-author.md — Escalating Force (Snyder)

A6 Snyder is the most meta-aware puzzle: the reviewer-as-author notes explicitly identify the structural limit of acrostic format — "the extraction mechanism and the domain content are loosely coupled by design, and Snyder's coherence standard exposes that gap rather than resolving it." This intellectual honesty is admirable but also describes a real ceiling on the puzzle's quality. Katz notices the five items are again Trebuchet, Onager, Woad Raider, Elite Skirmisher, Redemption — the same set as A3 and A4 — and notes the puzzle is tighter than A3 (the cost detail in clue 1 provides an independent verification path) but not meaningfully better. Snyder would find clue 4's iteration history documented well: "Castle Age upgrade of the Skirmisher" required to force Elite rather than base Skirmisher is a good construction note. Young observes that "Escalating Force" is the second-best title in the A group (after BREACH) — the arc from siege weapon through siege mechanics into economic units is thematic texture, as the design notes honestly admit.

## 8. A5-katz.md — (untitled, five clues)

A5-Katz makes the best individual item selection in any Group A or B puzzle: Wonder as the W answer is a genuine architectural surprise — it requires knowledge of the alternate victory condition (build a Wonder, survive a countdown) rather than any combat unit or technology. This shifts clue 3 out of the unit/tech taxonomy entirely into game-rule knowledge. Katz's construction log documents the short-circuit check (TOW_ is suggestive at clue 3, which is acceptable because clue 4 is the least expected item), the category variety audit (unique unit → siege → building/mechanic → unique unit → technology), and the backwards check. Snyder approves of the Wonder clue specifically: it is non-searchable in the sense that any AoE2 player knows Wonders exist but must specifically know the "survive the countdown" rule to write that clue. Young finds the puzzle the most structurally varied of all five-clue puzzles in the set and notes that Eagle Warrior (clue 4) is doing double duty — Mesoamerican civ philosophy and cavalry-replacement role — which is two registers of knowledge in one clue.

## 9. A6-katz.md — WOLOLO: Five of a Kind

A6-Katz is the strongest Katz puzzle. The title "WOLOLO" is the best title in the entire set: it names an AoE2-specific meme (the Monk conversion sound) without being about Monks specifically, and the subtitle "Five of a Kind" accurately describes the mechanism without spoiling it. The reviewer-as-author notes document how Snyder's Computer Test shaped candidate selection (Theocracy and Watch Tower eliminated because a script finds them trivially; Trebuchet and Woad Raider retained because they require cross-referencing properties). Katz's backwards-check discipline means every clue is forced. Young notes that "WOLOLO" as a framing device is the closest any puzzle in the set comes to theme-as-structure: the Monk conversion sound signals the AoE2 world before the solver reads a single clue.

## 10. A5-young.md — Field Report

A5-Young makes a significant visual commitment: the table format with two columns (clue | identified item) mirrors the AoE2 tech-tree interface. This is the only puzzle in the set where the layout is doing work beyond text presentation. The construction log's "inhabiting the world first" methodology produces clues that lead with phenomenology rather than data — "speed that infantry should not move, and your first thought is: that has to be a misprint" is a completely different register than "speed of 1.03." The Eagle Warrior verification is the weakest link: the world data file doesn't explicitly list Eagle Warrior as a full unit entry, and the solution key notes this ("context supports" rather than confirmed). Snyder flags this as a construction failure — a clue must be verifiable, and "context supports" is not verification. Katz is more forgiving, noting that Eagle Warrior is broadly documented in AoE2 knowledge even if the local data file is incomplete, but agrees the citation gap is a real quality issue.

## 11. A6-young.md — Holding the Line

A6-Young is the highest-scoring puzzle in the set. The second-person present-tense framing ("You are playing Age of Empires II. You queue this Castle Age research...") puts the solver inside a game narrative rather than presenting trivia questions. The five items form a coherent defensive scenario: Treadmill Crane (speed up wall construction), Onager (the threat carving through your forest wall), Watch Tower (your anchor point), Elite Skirmisher (your garrison troops), Ring Archer Armor (the final upgrade before the siege). The answer TOWER names not just the structure but the strategic role the solver has been defending throughout all five clues — it arrives as an inevitability. Young's Riven Standard is maximally satisfied: AoE2 is not decorating the puzzle, it is the puzzle. The threat of the Onager opening a path through Black Forest, the urgency of Treadmill Crane during early construction, the specific choice of Ring Archer Armor over any other Imperial upgrade — these are real strategic decisions with real AoE2 weight. Katz scores the structural elegance high, noting that the items form an actual tactical sequence rather than a trivia list. Snyder notes that Watch Tower (third tier below Keep) is not the most powerful tower, and Treadmill Crane is a Castle Age economic tech — both choices are slightly unexpected, which strengthens the reading reward since the solver must know the specific item rather than the most famous one.

### Key Findings

**1. Gradient finding — is A0→A6 monotonic?**

No. The gradient is real but non-monotonic at A4–A6. A0→A3 shows consistent improvement (+9.3 points across four conditions), driven by successively more principled clue construction. At A4 the score slightly drops from A3 (30.7 vs 31.0) — the stripping discipline produces leaner clues but also removes the cross-clue dependency that was A3's structural achievement. A5 recovers to 32.0, the Snyder-group peak. A6 then drops to 31.3. The plateau-and-retreat at the top of the Snyder group suggests that the reviewer-as-author transposition adds metacognitive overhead (the puzzle spends authoring energy documenting its own limits) without adding quality to the solver-visible puzzle. The general direction is correct — more principled authoring produces better puzzles — but the effect is not linear.

**2. A2 inversion — does domain data without framework help?**

Minimally. A2 (28.3) scores higher than A1 (27.7) by only 0.6 points despite the extensive world-data citations. The citations improve factual accuracy and eliminate the risk of constructing a false clue, but they do not improve clue texture: the clues read like database rows. Domain expertise without a design framework produces technically correct but experientially flat puzzles. The data is necessary but not sufficient; the gap between A2 and A3 (28.3→31.0, +2.7 points) is larger than the gap between A1 and A2 (27.7→28.3, +0.6 points), confirming that design principles add more value than domain data alone.

**3. A4 vs A5 — does worldview ≈ full profile?**

Close but not equivalent. A4 (authoring philosophy alone, 30.7) and A5 (full authoring profile, 32.0) differ by only 1.3 points — smaller than the A2→A3 or A3→A5 gaps. The full profile's main contribution is verification rigor: A5's construction log exhaustively eliminates competing candidates for each letter, which protects construction quality rather than generating new design quality. The solver-visible improvement is marginal. For a designer who already has strong instincts (like Snyder), the worldview captures most of the value of the full profile. The practical implication: for first-pass design, worldview alone is nearly sufficient; the full profile earns its cost primarily in verification (catching extraction errors, eliminating ambiguous clues), not in elevating the mechanism.

**4. Designer comparison — which designer shows the largest A5/A6 gap and why?**

Young shows the largest gap: +3.7 points (34.3→38.0). This is the only A5→A6 result where the reviewer-as-author improvement is large and unambiguous. The reason is that Young's most distinctive design instinct — "the mechanic is the world, not a representation of it" — is blocked by the acrostic format at A5 (she acknowledges that visual grammar and world-embodiment have no purchase in a text acrostic). At A6, the reviewer-as-author exercise forces her to apply her world-first framing within the format's constraints, and the solution she finds — second-person present tense, a coherent defensive narrative, items chosen for their role in a scenario rather than for their first letter — is genuinely new. The format becomes a game being played rather than a worksheet being filled out. Katz also improves (+1.6 points), driven by the WOLOLO title and the Snyder Computer Test eliminating weak candidates. Snyder regresses (−0.7 points): his reviewer-as-author exercise produces mostly meta-commentary about the format's limits rather than structural improvements to the puzzle, confirming that the transposition is most productive when the reviewer's primary instinct is not already the construction instinct.

**5. Lens-type finding — operational/construction/perceptual**

The three reviewers apply observably distinct lenses: Katz (operational/architectural) focuses on whether the mechanism contract is honored — does the solver have enough information, do the clues close tightly, does the answer arrive as a satisfying conclusion rather than a guess? His highest variance is in Fun and Riven, where he rewards puzzles that feel like complete experiences rather than demonstrations. Snyder (construction/deduction) focuses on whether each element is load-bearing and whether the solve path is singular — his RR scores are the most discriminating in the panel, giving 5s to puzzles where the domain knowledge is genuinely required and withholding them when clue language is generic. His Elegance scores drive the widest range between the weakest (A0: 2) and strongest (A6-Katz: 5) puzzles. Young (perceptual/world) shows the largest spread on Riven Standard (2 for A0, 5 for A6-Young) — she distinguishes sharply between puzzles where AoE2 is a content substrate and puzzles where AoE2 is the experience itself. Her DY score for A6-Young (39/45) is the single highest individual reviewer score in the evaluation. The three lenses are complementary: operational failures (Katz) usually precede construction failures (Snyder), which usually precede perceptual failures (Young) — a puzzle that fails Katz cannot pass Young, but a puzzle that passes Katz can still fail Young, as the Snyder-group A5 and A6 results demonstrate.
