**Reviewer:** Mark Riedl

## P1 Response Assessment

**P1.1 (Score reliability — range column, small gaps hedged):** Addressed. The Range column in Table 1 and the explicit prose hedging of the A1-vs-A2 gap and the A4-vs-A5 gap are appropriate responses. The table caption note ("differences within this spread should be interpreted with caution given the small panel size") correctly signals the reliability limitation at the point of data presentation, where readers are most likely to draw conclusions. My Round 1 concern was specifically about score differences of 0.6 and 0.7 points being used as evidence of theoretical effects; the revised text now explicitly identifies these as directional signals rather than precise measurements. This is the right fix.

**P1.2 (Rubric validity — CC-theoretic paragraph):** Addressed. The rubric validity paragraph in Section 3.5 is a substantive addition. Mapping Elegance to Boden's combinational creativity, Reading Reward to SPECS' domain-competent criterion, and Riven Standard to Boden's exploratory creativity criterion provides the construct-level validation argument the paper needed. The face-validity reference to Paper I calibration (tier separation with no overlapping distributions) grounds the rubric in empirical evidence beyond the current study. Both moves are appropriate.

**P1.3 (Lens taxonomy — reframed as proposed, classification procedure added):** Addressed. The taxonomy is now framed as "proposed" and "requiring replication" throughout Section 5.2. The classification procedure described at the end of Section 5.2 — classifying profiles from their content by counting the proportion of questions framed as named operations, uniqueness/load-bearing tests, or perceptual assessments — directly addresses my Round 1 P1.3 concern that the taxonomy could only be applied retrospectively from observed transfer outcomes. The procedure is not perfectly specified (threshold values are not given) but is a substantial improvement over no procedure at all.

**P1.4 (Companion paper dependency — presence/absence table added inline):** Addressed. Table 4 resolves the structural dependency I flagged as blocking in Round 1. The C0=0 counts for all six frameworks, combined with the caption explanation that C0 zero is "by construction," makes the "none appear in unprompted reviews" claim independently verifiable. The C6 counts (ranging from 4 for social fabric to 10 for load-bearing test) are drawn from the companion paper's frequency analysis, as the caption notes, but the essential claim — that the frameworks are emergent from enriched profiles — is now documented inline.

**P1.5 (CC positioning — new subsection connecting to Colton/Wiggins, Boden, Stokes):** Addressed. Section 5.4 is a welcome addition. Three connections are made: symmetric gradient to the CC evaluation problem (Colton/Wiggins), A4-vs-A5 to Boden's exploratory creativity, and Young A6 to Stokes on creative constraints. These are not superficial citations — each is argued in one to two substantive sentences. The section also explicitly positions "profile structure as a theoretically tractable variable in CC system design, not merely a prompt-engineering parameter," which is exactly the framing shift that the ICCC audience needed. This significantly improves the paper's credibility at the venue.

## Remaining Concerns

**P1.1 partial gap — no mechanistic account of framework vs. content.** My Round 1 P1.1 was about the absence of a mechanistic account of why framework outperforms content at the computational level. This was not addressed: the paper's explanation remains cognitive (frameworks activate generative orientation) without any computational hypothesis. I raised this as blocking in Round 1. The revised paper does not engage with it. In light of the other revisions, I am willing to treat this as a P2 item rather than P1 blocking — the CC positioning in Section 5.4 partially mitigates the concern by framing it as a research question, but this is implicit rather than explicit. The paper would benefit from one sentence in Section 5.1 or 5.4 explicitly positioning "what the model computationally does with a framework versus raw content" as an open mechanistic question.

**P1.2 partial gap — alternative-explanations analysis still absent.** My Round 1 P1.2 asked for a systematic ruling-out of alternative explanations for the A5-to-A6 gaps (e.g., verbal specificity rather than lens type for Katz; accidental misreading of evaluative questions as generative for Young). This was not addressed in the revision. The lens-type explanation remains plausible but uncontested. A brief alternative-explanations paragraph in Section 5.2 would substantially strengthen the causal claim.

**P2.1 (worldview ≈ full profile as primary design implication).** My P2.1 from Round 1 — that A4-vs-A5 is the most important authoring-system design implication and should be foregrounded rather than treated as confirmatory — was addressed only partially. The conclusion now mentions the worldview result, but it is framed as one of three results rather than as the primary design takeaway. The A4-vs-A5 finding — "when building AI authoring profiles, invest in worldview elicitation; checklist elaboration is insurance" — is the paper's most immediately actionable result and deserves the prominence it still lacks.

**P3.1 (exemplar comparison of A2 vs. A3 outputs).** No A2-vs-A3 clue comparison table was added. The prose description of the Stone and Steel puzzle is evocative ("reads like a reference table with punctuation added") but a concrete clue-level comparison would ground the "framework activates generative orientation" claim empirically. This remains the paper's most significant missed opportunity to demonstrate rather than assert its core finding.
