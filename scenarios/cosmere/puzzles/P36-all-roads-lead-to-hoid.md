# P36 — All Roads Lead to Hoid

*Module F (Hoid & Worldhoppers) | Valor | Difficulty: ★★★★★*

---

One has been everywhere. Not metaphorically. One has attended the breaking of gods, the crowning of tyrants, the death of heroes, and the birth of entire magic systems. Some events, one witnessed firsthand — stood in the room, spoke the words, set the wheel turning. Others rippled outward and reached the wanderer later, across oceans of space and time.

Twelve events from across the Cosmere are described below. Every one of them connects to the wanderer. But the connections are not equal. Some roads lead directly to the fool — he was there, he acted, he chose. Others are longer roads, where consequences traveled far before finding him.

For each event, identify what occurred and determine the nature of the connection. A **direct** connection means the wanderer was present and active — he stood in the room, he spoke, he gave, he initiated. An **indirect** connection means the event's consequences reached him from afar — he was changed by it, or he benefited from it, but he did not participate.

---

## The Twelve Events

**I.** A street informant in a city of ash and nobility tells a charismatic thief about a metal that does not appear on any chart. The thief uses this information to challenge a god-king. The informant vanishes before anyone thinks to ask his name.

**II.** Sixteen individuals conspire to destroy something that should not have been destroyable. The power divides. Everyone present takes a piece — except one. That one walks away empty-handed, for reasons that remain the Cosmere's central mystery.

**III.** On a world of twelve spore seas, a young woman's beloved is kidnapped by a sorceress. The story of how she rescues him is told in first person by someone who was there — someone who insists on editorializing, who cannot resist a tangent, and whose companion spren corrects him constantly.

**IV.** In a realm of thought and shadow, a dead thief and a living wanderer meet near a pool of liquid divinity. The dead man wants the power. The wanderer wants something else entirely. Their conversation is brief, adversarial, and — to one of them — amusing.

**V.** A woman with blue lines tattooed on her skin, who once picked pockets in a city of ash, takes up the power of a dying god to destroy another god. Both the power and the woman are consumed. The world is remade by a scholar who held neither.

**VI.** In a warcourt where lighteyes duel for prestige, a professional insulter tells a young woman the story of a girl who climbed a wall to see the light beyond it. The story is about more than a girl. It is about more than a wall. The insulter knows this. The young woman will understand later.

**VII.** A Terrisman who spent his life memorizing religions finds that none of them are entirely true — and then discovers that all of them contain a fragment of what was. He takes up two opposed powers and becomes something that has never existed. The world he remakes is gentler than the one he inherited.

**VIII.** A young bridgeman on the Shattered Plains, who should have died a dozen times over, fights off an assassination attempt against a king. The bridgeman should not have been there at that exact moment. The fact that he was is not a coincidence, though no one admits to arranging it.

**IX.** In a palace of a dying empire on a world of scripts and symbols, a forger works under sentence of death to replicate the soul of an emperor. In the background, an old man plays the role of a fool. His performance is unconvincing, but no one in the palace is paying attention to fools.

**X.** A new god — one wearing the face of an old king, a man who once drew diagrams on brilliant days and wept on compassionate ones — outmaneuvers the Cosmere's oldest trickster. Memories are altered in a conversation that should have been routine. For the first time in millennia, the wanderer has been beaten. He does not even know it yet.

**XI.** On a world of storms and stone, a former surgeon's son stands in the path of a highstorm and speaks words that have not been spoken in four thousand years. Life before death. Strength before weakness. Journey before destination. The person who gave him the courage to say those words had been telling him stories about a runner who never stopped.

**XII.** A scholar-queen discovers the fundamental nature of Investiture by combining Light and anti-Light in a tower besieged by an enemy. Her work redefines what is possible in the war against the god of hatred. The implications reach far beyond her single world — to every world, and to every wanderer walking between them.
