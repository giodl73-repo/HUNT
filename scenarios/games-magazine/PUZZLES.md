## Puzzle Briefs

### P1 — Lurking Words
**Type:** Themed crossword (15x15)
**Pages:** 2-3 (full spread)
**Difficulty:** ★★★
**Byline:** By Margaret Huang

**Mechanism:** 6 theme answers each contain a common English word hidden consecutively within them. Example: CHARTER hides HART, PINEAPPLE hides APPLE. The 15x15 grid has standard crossword fill around the theme entries. Clued normally — the aha is noticing the hidden words. The first letters of the 6 hidden words, read top-to-bottom in grid order, spell SHADOW.

**Layout:** Grid centered on the spread. Across clues on left page, Down clues on right page. Theme entries run across (rows 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13).

**Intro:** ~70 words. Sets up the idea that words conceal other words. Warm, inviting — the crossword is the magazine's anchor puzzle.

### P2 — Invisible Ink
**Type:** Cryptic clues (standalone, no grid)
**Pages:** 4 (single page)
**Difficulty:** ★★★★
**Byline:** By R.J. Calder

**Mechanism:** 10 cryptic clues. Each answer is a word meaning "hidden" or "concealed": LATENT, COVERT, ARCANE, VEILED, BURIED, MASKED, UNSEEN, OCCULT, CLOSET, CIPHER. The cryptic wordplay mechanisms themselves embody concealment: containers (word inside word), hidden words (consecutive in clue text), reversals (backwards), deletions (word minus letters). Each clue is enumerated. No grid — just a numbered answer list.

**Layout:** 2-column. Clues in left column, answer blanks and enumeration in right column.

**Intro:** ~60 words. Dry, witty. Acknowledges that cryptics are already about hiding meanings.

### P3 — The Camouflage Club
**Type:** Logic grid (5x5)
**Pages:** 5 (single page)
**Difficulty:** ★★★
**Byline:** By Nadia Koresh

**Mechanism:** 5 members of a naturalists' club (Alice, Blake, Carmen, Devi, Elias) each study a different camouflage animal (chameleon, octopus, stick insect, arctic fox, cuttlefish). Each has a different observation habitat (rainforest, coral reef, woodland, tundra, mangrove) and a different observation tool (binoculars, diving mask, magnifying glass, camera trap, thermal scope). 8 clues, one of which is deliberately false (camouflaged among the true clues). The solver must identify which clue is false and solve the grid using only the 7 true clues. The false clue is identified because it creates a contradiction when combined with the others.

**Layout:** 3-column. Intro + clues in left column, 5x5 elimination grid in center, answer table in right column.

**Intro:** ~55 words. Clean, structural. Introduces the club and the twist (one clue is camouflaged as truth).

### P4 — Plain Sight
**Type:** Visual search / hidden object
**Pages:** 6-7 (full spread)
**Difficulty:** ★★
**Byline:** By Felix Ortega

**Mechanism:** A detailed line-art illustration of a public library interior. Hidden within the illustration are 10 everyday objects camouflaged into the architecture, furniture, and decor (a key disguised as a door handle detail, a fish hidden in a bookshelf pattern, a crown merged into a chandelier, etc.). Each object is a common noun. The 10 objects are listed with their letter counts. The solver finds each object in the illustration and writes its name. Taking the first letter of each object in the order listed spells CAMOUFLAGE (but this is not revealed — the solver discovers it).

**Layout:** Full-bleed illustration across both pages. Object list and answer blanks along the bottom margin.

**Intro:** ~40 words. Minimal — let the image speak. Just enough to explain the task.

### P5 — Redacted
**Type:** Word puzzle / contextual deduction
**Pages:** 8 (single page)
**Difficulty:** ★★★★
**Byline:** By Margaret Huang

**Mechanism:** A 200-word essay about famous acts of concealment throughout history (invisible ink in the Revolutionary War, hidden compartments in furniture, steganography in images, etc.). 8 words in the essay have been "redacted" — replaced with black bars. The word lengths are given. Contextual clues in the surrounding sentences, plus general knowledge, allow the solver to reconstruct each redacted word. The first letters of the 8 redacted words, in order, spell DISGUISE.

**Layout:** 2-column. The essay in the left column (with redactions clearly marked), answer blanks in the right column.

**Intro:** ~60 words. Plays with the irony of concealment in a puzzle about concealment.

### P6 — The Hidden Sequence
**Type:** Mathematical / pattern recognition
**Pages:** 9 (single page)
**Difficulty:** ★★★★★
**Byline:** By Nadia Koresh

**Mechanism:** A 7x7 grid of two-digit numbers (49 numbers total). The grid appears random but contains exactly 6 arithmetic sequences hidden within it — some horizontal, some vertical, some diagonal. Each sequence is exactly 4 numbers long (4 cells). The solver must find all 6 sequences. Once found, 25 cells are accounted for (6 sequences x 4 = 24, but some cells belong to two sequences, leaving exactly 25 cells used). The 24 unused cells, read left-to-right top-to-bottom, contain digits that encode the answer via A1Z26 (01=A, 02=B, ... 26=Z): the answer is ENIGMA (05-14-09-07-13-01).

**Layout:** 3-column. Grid centered, instructions on left, work space on right.

**Intro:** ~50 words. Spare, mathematical. Challenges the solver to find order in chaos.

### P7 — Spot the Difference
**Type:** Comparison / observation
**Pages:** 10 (top half)
**Difficulty:** ★★
**Byline:** By Sam Whitfield

**Mechanism:** Two short paragraphs (each ~60 words) that appear identical but contain 6 single-letter differences. Paragraph A might say "The castle stood on a HILL" while Paragraph B says "The castle stood on a HULL." The 6 changed letters, in order of appearance, spell SECRET.

**Layout:** 2-column (each paragraph in one column). Answer blanks below.

**Intro:** ~40 words. Friendly, accessible. The easiest puzzle in the issue — a palate cleanser.

### P8 — The Vanishing Act
**Type:** Word search variant
**Pages:** 10 (bottom half) - 11 (full page)
**Difficulty:** ★★★★★
**Byline:** By R.J. Calder

**Mechanism:** A 12x12 letter grid and a list of 20 words to find. All words relate to concealment and illusion (HIDDEN, CLOAK, SHADOW, MIRAGE, VEIL, SLEIGHT, PHANTOM, DECOY, RUSE, FACADE, GUISE, SHROUD, ILLUSION, ALIAS, PRETENSE, OBSCURE, STEALTH, COVERT, SECRET, VANISH). 19 of the 20 words can be found in the grid using standard word search rules (horizontal, vertical, diagonal, forwards or backwards). One word CANNOT be found by standard rules. That word is VANISH — and it IS in the grid, but it reads in a spiral path from the center outward. Finding the missing word and discovering its non-standard path is the aha. The answer to the puzzle is VANISH.

**Layout:** Page 10 bottom half: intro paragraph and word list. Page 11 full: the 12x12 grid with ample margin space for the solver to work.

**Intro:** ~60 words. R.J. Calder's dry wit. Acknowledges that word searches are simple — "or so you might think."
