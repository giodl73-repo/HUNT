# THE GRAND LARCENY — Puzzle Pool

## P1 Slot: The Police Report (Logic/Grid)

### Variant A: Alibi Contradiction Grid (SELECTED)
The police report contains a summary of four suspects' alibis in a formatted grid. The solver must cross-reference alibis against witness statements and physical evidence (timestamps, phone logs, key card entries) to identify contradictions. One suspect's alibi has NO contradictions — they are the only one who COULD have committed the theft in the 9:15-9:25 window. Extraction from the suspect's alibi details yields the answer word.

**Panel notes:** Strong Riven Standard — this IS what police work does. Logic grid with real deduction, not just matching. Interlock between clues (one contradiction reveals information about another). Lucas Pope rates this highest.

### Variant B: Witness Cross-Reference
Multiple witness statements, each partially reliable. The solver triangulates truth from overlapping accounts. More literary, less structured.

**Panel notes:** Harder to extract a clean answer word. Ambiguity risk. Selinker likes the narrative feel but Huang flags deductive weakness.

### Variant C: Timeline Reconstruction
A shuffled timeline of events. The solver must order them correctly using clues embedded in the report text, then read down specific positions.

**Panel notes:** Clean extraction, but feels more like a sequencing exercise than investigation. Snyder: "A computer could sort this." Downgraded.

**Consensus: Variant A.** 10/12 panelists.

## P2 Slot: The Hotel Receipt (Arithmetic/Pattern)

### Variant A: Hidden Arithmetic Pattern (SELECTED)
Four suspects' itemized hotel receipts, all correct on the surface. The solver notices that specific charges across the four receipts, when the cents digits are extracted and converted (A1Z26), spell a word. The aha: not every charge is meaningful — only charges that occurred in specific time windows. The solver must identify which charges to use and which to ignore.

**Panel notes:** Good discipline — arithmetic confirms, doesn't compute. Deduction layer (which charges matter?) prevents it from being pure calculation. The receipt format is authentic. Blow likes the "which data matters?" insight.

### Variant B: Overcharged Item
One receipt has an intentionally wrong total. The discrepancy, converted to a letter, contributes to extraction. Repeat across receipts.

**Panel notes:** "No Deliberate Errors" principle conflict — the wrong total IS the puzzle, but the world says "No fact contradicts." Tricky. Could frame it as "the forger made errors." Risky.

### Variant C: Service Code Cipher
The two-letter service codes (CH, BD, SW, etc.) from the receipts, read in order, spell something.

**Panel notes:** Clean but thin. Not enough deduction — just reading codes. Snyder's Computer Test fails.

**Consensus: Variant A.** 9/12 panelists.

## P3 Slot: The Floor Plan (Spatial/Path)

### Variant A: Thief's Route Trace (SELECTED)
An architectural floor plan of the hotel (all 4 levels). The solver uses clues from the police report and physical evidence (the hotel key card prop, the unlogged elevator use, the fire escape) to trace the thief's route through the building. The rooms visited in sequence, when their first letters or a specific property is extracted, spell the answer word.

**Panel notes:** Spatial puzzle with a physical prop (hotel key card). The floor plan IS the puzzle — no separate worksheet needed. The aha is discovering the hidden door between gallery and linen closet. Rand Miller rates this highest: "The building is the puzzle."

### Variant B: Room Number Cipher
Room numbers along the path are converted to letters. Pure number-to-letter.

**Panel notes:** Too computational. No spatial reasoning required — just follow the path and do math. Downgraded.

### Variant C: Overlapping Floor Plans
Transparent overlays of each floor, stacked. Marked rooms on different floors, when overlaid, form letters.

**Panel notes:** Brilliant concept but requires transparency printing — the physical delivery makes this possible but fragile. Reserved for a larger hunt.

**Consensus: Variant A.** 11/12 panelists.

## P4 Slot: The Witness Statement (Text/Hidden Message)

### Variant A: Acrostic in Testimony (SELECTED)
A typed witness statement (transcript of Miss Delacroix's interview). The text reads naturally as a nervous, halting account. But the first letter of each sentence spells a hidden message. The message reveals what Delacroix actually saw — and the answer word is embedded in that message.

**Panel notes:** Classic hidden-text mechanism, elevated by the narrative quality of the witness voice. The Riven Standard is met: reading witness statements for hidden meaning IS what detectives do. The challenge is making the acrostic invisible on first read. Selinker: "The voice must be so good that you don't notice the structure."

### Variant B: Redacted Statement
Blacked-out words in the transcript. The redaction pattern, when the remaining visible letters are read at certain intervals, spells a word.

**Panel notes:** Visual puzzle, good for physical. But the mechanism is arbitrary — why would redactions encode a word? Narrative justification is weak.

### Variant C: Contradictions in Word Choice
Specific word choices in the statement are "wrong" — the witness uses oddly specific or out-of-place words. The first letters of the wrong words spell the answer.

**Panel notes:** Elegant but fragile. "Wrong" words are subjective. Would need very precise writing and could frustrate solvers who read differently. Huang flags ambiguity.

**Consensus: Variant A.** 8/12 panelists.
