**Det. Callahan:** Miss Delacroix, please describe your evening at the hotel in your own words. Start from when you arrived.

**Miss Delacroix:**

I came to the gala specifically to see the Renaud. Studying art is my life, and this was a rare chance to see the original up close. After dinner I went straight to the gallery on the second floor. Wanting to examine the brushwork, I stood very close to the painting for a long time.

The attendant — Mr. Gould — was seated by the door, reading a newspaper. He barely looked up the entire time I was there. Eventually Mrs. Ashworth walked through the gallery, glanced at the painting, and left without a word.

Coming up on nine o'clock, I began to feel unwell. A headache, perhaps from the warm room. Nobody had opened a window all evening. Very soon I decided to step out into the corridor for some air. At that point — right around nine — I left the gallery.

Standing in the second-floor hallway, I could hear the dinner crowd below. I walked toward the window at the far end of the corridor and looked out. Nothing seemed unusual at first. Several minutes passed while I stood there. I remember the city lights and the quiet of the upper floors. Darkness had settled over the alley on the north side of the building.

Eventually I heard a sound. A low mechanical hum, like a motor turning, from behind the wall near where I stood.

Where the noise came from, I could not say for certain. Obviously it was somewhere inside the walls — the elevator shaft, perhaps. Only later did I realize the service elevator runs through that same wall. Drawn by unease, I began walking back toward my room.

Eventually I passed the gallery entrance. Near the door, I noticed something odd — the attendant's chair was empty.

Obviously I should have gone in. But I did not. Just a glance through the doorway, nothing more. Even from the hall, the room appeared normal — lights on, paintings on the walls. Curtains moving slightly from a draft, though I cannot say from where. The thought of anything being wrong did not cross my mind.

[pause]

I went to my room and lay down. Shortly after — perhaps ten minutes — voices filled the corridor. All at once, people were rushing downstairs. When I reached the lobby, an officer told me the painting was gone. I could not believe it.

Detective, that is everything I saw. [pause] I wish I had been paying closer attention.

---

**Det. Callahan:** Thank you, Miss Delacroix. Did you see anyone else on the second floor between 9:00 and 9:30 PM?

**Miss Delacroix:** [pause] Only a shadow. Near the staircase at the far end of the hallway, I thought I saw a figure — tall, dark suit. Just for a moment. He moved toward the third floor stairs and was gone.

---

*End of statement. Witness signed and dated.*
