# Spectrum Analyzer — Instrument Reference

**Department:** COMMS
**Location:** Bridge OPS console [1], Comms station
**Instrument class:** Frequency-domain signal display

---

## Controls

| Control | Range | Step | Default |
|---------|-------|------|---------|
| Center Frequency | 1.000 - 5.000 GHz | 10 MHz | 2.400 GHz |
| Span | 10 / 50 / 100 / 500 MHz | selector | 100 MHz |
| Resolution Bandwidth (RBW) | 1 / 10 / 100 kHz | selector | 10 kHz |
| Peak Hold | ON / OFF | toggle | OFF |
| Sweep Mode | SINGLE / CONTINUOUS | toggle | CONTINUOUS |

## Display

Horizontal axis: frequency (center +/- span/2).
Vertical axis: power in dBm. Range: -120 dBm to +10 dBm.

---

## Physics

### Carrier vs. Noise

A carrier is a coherent signal at a single frequency. It appears as a narrow spike on the display. Noise is incoherent energy spread across the band. It appears as a flat, fluctuating floor.

**Key distinction:** When RBW narrows, carrier power stays constant (the carrier is narrower than any RBW setting). Noise power drops proportionally with RBW (less bandwidth = less noise captured).

### SNR Improvement Formula

```
SNR_gain = 10 * log10(span / RBW)
```

Narrowing RBW from 100 kHz to 1 kHz while holding span constant yields 20 dB SNR improvement.

### Peak Hold Behavior

When Peak Hold is ON, the display retains the maximum value at each frequency bin across all sweeps. A carrier that persists across sweeps will appear as a stable peak. Noise peaks are random and will fill in to a flat ceiling over time.

A carrier survives peak hold. Noise converges to a flat line.

### Harmonic Identification

Spurious signals appear at integer multiples of the fundamental frequency: 2*f0, 3*f0, 4*f0, etc. They are generated by nonlinear distortion in the transmitter or receiver chain.

If a peak appears at f0 and additional peaks appear at n*f0, the signal at f0 is the fundamental and the others are harmonics. Harmonics are always weaker than the fundamental (typically 10-30 dB below).

### Noise Floor

The analyzer's own noise floor depends on RBW:

```
Noise floor (dBm) = -174 + 10*log10(RBW in Hz) + NF
```

Where NF is the analyzer's noise figure (typically 6-10 dB). Signals below the noise floor are invisible.

---

## Operating Procedure: Carrier Acquisition

1. Set span wide (500 MHz). Sweep. Look for any peak above noise floor.
2. Center on the peak. Reduce span to 50 MHz.
3. Reduce RBW to 10 kHz. If the peak narrows with RBW, it is noise. If it stays the same width, it is a carrier.
4. Enable Peak Hold. Run 10 sweeps. Carrier persists. Noise fills in.
5. Read frequency from peak marker. This is the carrier frequency.

---

## Diagnostic Notes

- If multiple peaks appear at regular spacing, check for harmonics. The lowest-frequency peak of the series is likely the fundamental.
- Carrier bandwidth < RBW at all settings = real carrier.
- Carrier bandwidth tracks RBW = noise artifact, not a real signal.
- A carrier at exactly the center of a known comm band is likely intentional traffic. A carrier offset from band center by a non-standard amount warrants investigation.
