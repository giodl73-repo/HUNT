# Set 3 — Deep Cuts

*"You know these records. You just forgot you knew them."*

---

Vee kept a list taped to the booth wall — seven albums she called "the ones that matter." She never wrote the titles. Only descriptions. She said anyone who really listened would know which records she meant.

Identify each album. Take the first letter of each album title. Read them in order.

---

## The Seven Albums

**I.**
Released in July 1980. The second and final album by a Manchester band whose lead singer would not live to see its release. The cover is a photograph of a tomb in a cemetery in Genoa, stark and pale. The band named themselves after the entertainment division of a World War II concentration camp. Their sound defined the post-punk genre — controlled, cold, rhythmic. The producer was Martin Hannett. The singer died by suicide in May 1980, weeks before the planned American tour. The remaining members formed a different band that would go on to far greater commercial success.

**II.**
October 1992. A four-piece from Athens, Georgia. The album takes its title from the slogan of Weaver D's, a local soul food restaurant: "Automatic for the People." The mandolin on track 4 became one of the most recognizable riffs in 1990s rock. Michael Stipe's vocals are more subdued than on earlier records. String arrangements on several tracks were composed by John Paul Jones of Led Zeppelin. Track 10 is about the comedian Andy Kaufman. Track 11 features solo piano.

**III.**
May 1989. A five-piece from Crawley, England, though by this point the band was largely the vision of one man — the vocalist, guitarist, and songwriter with a distinctive shock of black hair and smeared lipstick. The album is the band's eighth studio release and widely considered their masterpiece. The opening track is an instrumental wash nearly five minutes long. Track 4 became their highest-charting US single, reaching number 2 on the Hot 100. The album's themes are loss, faith, and disillusion, and its sound is massive: layered guitars, heavy reverb, orchestral arrangements. The title is a single word suggesting dissolution.

**IV.**
October 1968. A double album and the third release by a Seattle-born guitarist who revolutionized the electric guitar. Named after an undersea realm from mythology. Side one includes a fifteen-minute psychedelic blues epic. The cover art was painted by a commercial artist hired by the record label. The guitarist died in London in September 1970 at age 27. The album reached number 1 on the Billboard 200 — his only album to do so.

**V.**
September 1991. Produced by Butch Vig. A trio from Aberdeen, Washington. The cover photograph shows a baby swimming toward a dollar bill on a fishhook. The lead single became the anthem of a generation — four chords, a quiet verse, a loud chorus, a pattern that defined an entire genre. The album displaced Michael Jackson's "Dangerous" at number 1 on the Billboard 200 in January 1992. The lead singer and guitarist was born in 1967 and died in 1994.

**VI.**
September 1974. A British prog-rock band's third album, and the one that made them famous. The title suggests a felony of historic proportions. The opening track features a long electric piano intro over a propulsive bass line. "Dreamer" and "Bloody Well Right" were both released as singles. The album is a concept record about the disillusionment of youth. The band was formed in London in 1969, taking their name from a character in a William Henry Davies novel.

**VII.**
March 1983. A power trio from Houston, Texas. Three members, all with beards (two of them notably long). The cover features a red hot rod — a 1933 Ford coupe. The album spawned three top-40 hits, including a song about a woman and a song about television. The band's guitar tone shifted sharply from blues to synth-heavy rock. Their name consists of two letters and a surname meaning "top" in a German-influenced pronunciation.

Fact verification:
- Closer: Released July 18, 1980. Ian Curtis died May 18, 1980. Cover is a photograph by Bernard Pierre Wolff of the Staglieno monumental cemetery in Genoa. Remaining members formed New Order.
- Automatic for the People: October 5, 1992. "Weaver D's Delicious Fine Foods" slogan confirmed. John Paul Jones arranged strings. Track 10 is "Man on the Moon" (about Kaufman). Track 11 is "Nightswimming" (piano by Mike Mills).
- Disintegration: May 2, 1989. "Plainsong" opens the album (4:47). "Lovesong" peaked at #2. Robert Smith is the frontman.
- Electric Ladyland: October 16, 1968. Third Jimi Hendrix Experience album. Reached #1 on Billboard 200. "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)" is the epic. Cover art controversy — US vs. UK versions. Hendrix died September 18, 1970, age 27.
- Nevermind: September 24, 1991. Baby on cover (Spencer Elden). "Smells Like Teen Spirit" is track 2 (technically the first track on international releases may vary, but it's track 1 on the US release). [VERIFY: Track 1 is "Smells Like Teen Spirit" on the standard release, not track 2. The description says "the second track" — this needs correction.]
- Crime of the Century: September 1974. Supertramp's third album. "Dreamer" and "Bloody Well Right" were singles. Band name from W.H. Davies' "The Autobiography of a Super-Tramp."
- Eliminator: March 23, 1983. ZZ Top. 1933 Ford coupe on cover. Hits: "Sharp Dressed Man," "Legs," "Got Me Under Pressure."
