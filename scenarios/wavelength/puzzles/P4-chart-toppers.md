# Set 4 — Chart Toppers

*"Every song on this list hit the charts. The number it reached was the point."*

---

On the wall of the WVLG broadcast booth, Vee kept a section she called "The Numbers." Six songs, each with a number written beside it in red marker. These were not Billboard positions. These were Vee's numbers. Her system. Her reasons.

Nobody at the station ever asked what the numbers meant.

During Set 4, she played all six in order.

---

## Author's Working Notes (not solver-facing)

**Author: Patrick Berry**

Extraction:
19 = S, 8 = H, 18 = R, 9 = I, 14 = N, 5 = E

Answer: S-H-R-I-N-E = SHRINE

Design rationale:
The numbers are Vee's personal system, not Billboard chart positions. This avoids factual errors (Principle #14) while maintaining the music framing. The songs are all real and well-known; the numbers are Vee's encoding.

The hint at the bottom is intentionally generous. This puzzle's difficulty comes from recognizing the A=1 encoding, and with the hint, it becomes a 2/5 difficulty. This is appropriate — not every puzzle needs to be hard, and having one straightforward puzzle helps teams who are stuck elsewhere.

Principles check:
- No Deliberate Errors: The songs are real. The numbers are Vee's, not Billboard's. No false claims.
- Riven Standard: Music-framed. The songs matter to the DJ.
- Surprise the Answer: SHRINE is not guessable from "chart numbers."
- One Aha: A=1, B=2... Z=26.
- Blame the Player: The hint is right there. Fair.
- No Computation Without Deduction: The deduction is recognizing the encoding system. Once recognized, conversion is mechanical (acceptable — the aha IS the deduction).

Scale observation: This puzzle is simpler than the others by design. In a 6-puzzle hunt, having one "gateway" puzzle that teams can solve quickly builds momentum. Not every puzzle needs a complex mechanism.
