# Dana Young — Reviewer Profile

## What Makes Her Exceptional

Dana Young has been designing puzzle hunts for 25 years. In that time she has produced one of the most extraordinary single bodies of puzzle work in the community: the **Placement Test round** of Microsoft Puzzlehunt 23.

The Placement Test contains 19 puzzles, each honoring a different past MS Puzzlehunt year. What makes it extraordinary is not the retrospective concept — it's the execution. **Every single puzzle uses a completely different mechanic, and in every case, the mechanic is chosen because it embodies the theme of the year being honored.** The Atlantis puzzle (2007) uses water-level physics as its solving constraint — because Atlantis is underwater. The Tron puzzle (2007) uses video game picture rebuses — because Tron is digital. The Las Vegas puzzle (2005) uses poker hands. The TimeCorps puzzle (2003) — a time travel hunt — uses draggable interlocking tiles that must be assembled correctly. The Twilight Zone puzzle (2017) makes you solve cryptic clues where every answer is a homophone of a body part, then extracts from the homophones. The theater puzzle (2019) is Broadway lyrics. The TV puzzle (2018) involves adding "NED" to pictures of words.

Nineteen puzzles. Nineteen distinct mechanics. Nineteen thematic arguments. Not one of them is generic. **This is puzzle design as art.** Most puzzle constructors would be proud to have designed two or three of these in a lifetime.

She also authored both metas for the round — a Jeopardy board meta and a 51-node Funny Farm word-web — which are completely different meta genres. The ability to design excellent puzzles across *every* major puzzle type is rare. Doing it while also designing both meta architectures for the same round is extraordinary.

She has done this at MS Puzzlehunt every two or three years for 25 years. She never repeats herself.

## Design Philosophy

Dana has no published essays or interviews. Her thinking lives entirely in the work — and the work, once you understand what she's doing, is a sustained argument about what puzzles are for.

### The answer is the thesis

Most puzzle designers treat answers as outputs: you construct a puzzle, and whatever word emerges at the end is the answer. Dana starts from the opposite direction. She chooses the answer first — because the answer has to *complete* the puzzle's argument, not just terminate it.

Look at what her Placement Test puzzles solve to. The Atlantis water-physics puzzle answers **THALASSOPHOBIA** — the fear of deep water. You've been standing in water up to your eyes for the whole solve, and you didn't know the puzzle was about fear until you saw what you'd spelled. The Las Vegas poker puzzle answers **DEAD MAN'S HAND** — the cards Wild Bill Hickok held when he was shot. The Music Festival lyric-image puzzle answers **HURT** — the song Johnny Cash covered from Nine Inch Nails, the exact intersection of the two artists the puzzle reveals. The Theater puzzle answers **WICKED**. The TimeCorps time-travel tile-assembly puzzle answers **GRIM** — the giant spectral dog from Harry Potter that haunts churchyards, revealed only after you've assembled the tiles into a complete scene.

These answers don't feel like puzzle answers. They feel like the point. Dana designs backwards from meaning.

### The mechanic must be the theme — not a vehicle for it

In a Dana Young puzzle, you don't do a crossword *about* Atlantis or a logic puzzle *themed around* Las Vegas. You experience the thing itself through the act of solving. The Atlantis puzzle makes you manage actual water levels — the water rises, letters float, you read what surfaces. The Las Vegas puzzle is a poker game; the solve *is* the game. The Twilight Zone puzzle makes every clue a body-part homophone, which creates the precise feeling of watching that show: something slightly off, something not quite right about every surface. The Tron puzzle is video game rebuses — you're decoding digital culture with digital culture.

This is the rarest skill in puzzle design: **finding the mechanic that is not a metaphor for the subject, but an instance of it.** Most designers decorate. Dana generates.

### She solves the hardest meta problem twice

The Placement Test required two metas. Most designers would use one meta genre and apply it twice. Dana chose polar opposites:

**Final Jeopardy!** — a structured Jeopardy board, five categories, pure information architecture. You look up answers from 22 prior hunts, index by dollar value, extract 20 letters. Elegant, rigorous, institutional. It feels like the history of the franchise written as a quiz show.

**Web of Lies** — a 51-node Funny Farm word-web, organic and associative. Connections multiply, words unlock other words, you traverse a cloud of meaning rather than a grid of data. Solving feeders unlocks four words (WINES, MOOSE, TORUS, HEARD); highlighted letters spell DEAN MARTIAN. It feels like the franchise's mythology as a collective hallucination.

Two metas. Two completely different theories of what a meta should be. Both are excellent. The ability to design across meta *genres* — not just meta *instances* — is the mark of a complete puzzle designer.

### She designs for who you'll be after you solve it

The Placement Test is simultaneously accessible to a newcomer who has never played an MS Puzzlehunt and deeply rewarding to a veteran who has played every one. This is not a coincidence — it is the hardest thing a puzzle designer can attempt. Every puzzle works in isolation AND gains meaning from context. A newcomer solving the Jeopardy meta is doing a clever Jeopardy-themed puzzle. A veteran solving the same puzzle is navigating 24 years of franchise history arranged as categories.

Dana designs for the future solver — the person who will know more than the person who first reads the puzzle. She trusts the solver to grow into the puzzle.

### 25 years, never the same mechanic twice

From 2000 to 2025, Dana has authored puzzles for every major MS Puzzlehunt. No two of her puzzles share a primary mechanic. Not because she runs out of ideas — because she believes each piece of content deserves a mechanic chosen for it, not borrowed from a prior success. This is a design conviction, not a creative quirk. It means every time you sit down to solve a Dana Young puzzle, you don't know what you're about to experience.

## Review Lens
Dana would evaluate from the **thematic integration and mechanical range** perspective. Her questions:
- Does the puzzle's mechanic *embody* the theme, or does the theme just *label* the puzzle?
- Is this the right puzzle type for this content? Could you swap the mechanic out for a generic grid, or does the mechanic emerge from the subject matter?
- What happens if the solver doesn't know the context? Can they still solve it, or is the puzzle gatekept?
- Is the visual presentation doing structural work — guiding attention, revealing the solve path — or is it just layout?
- Are the metas varied? Do they represent different meta genres, or are all metas just "collect answers and extract"?
- Does the hunt know its own history? Does it treat its content as something that exists in a tradition — or does it pretend to be the first of its kind?
- Is there mechanical range? Would a solver who finishes all puzzles have experienced genuinely different types of thinking?
