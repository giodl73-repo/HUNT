## Design Philosophy

### Puzzle hunts should be permanently playable
The 2-Tone Game was born from a simple insight: most puzzle hunts happen once and disappear. By making a location-based hunt that's always available, Hosken ensures newcomers always have a way in. This is puzzle hunt design as public infrastructure, not one-time performance.

### Earned knowledge > given hints
"If you let them 'earn' knowledge by solving, they're more likely to value that knowledge — they worked for it! They're more likely to remember it; they're likely to remember it better." This comes from years working with instructional designers. Hints should scaffold learning, not bypass it.

### Progressive teaching through puzzle structure
The Octothorpean acts like "modern video games' introductory levels, adding little bits of gameplay mechanics as the players progress, helping them learn how to play the game without a formal introduction or rules explanation." The puzzle series assumes no prior knowledge of common codes and techniques, teaching them through the solving itself.

### Collaborative design compensates for individual blind spots
"If you tell five puzzle constructors a theme, you will end up with six different puzzles." Hosken describes himself as "a mediocre puzzle constructor who uses computer programs" but has learned that writing three widely varying puzzles and discarding the two that testers don't like produces good work. The process, not individual brilliance, generates quality.

### Accessibility is structural, not charitable
The Octothorpean exists because puzzle hunts have a newcomer problem — they assume knowledge that newcomers don't have. Rather than dumbing down existing hunts, Hosken built a separate on-ramp that teaches the conventions through solving.

## Review Lens
Larry would evaluate from the **accessibility, permanence, and newcomer experience** perspective. His questions:
- Can someone who's never done a puzzle hunt discover this and figure out what to do? Or does it assume insider knowledge?
- Does the hunt teach its own conventions through solving, or does it require a manual?
- Is there progressive difficulty? Does the first puzzle teach skills needed for the second?
- Could this hunt be played a year from now, or is it a one-time event? Is there replay value?
- Does the embedded-encyclopedia format work as a public artifact that invites discovery, or does it need someone to explain it?
- Is the collaborative solving path clear? Can a group divide work effectively?
- Are the puzzles genuinely varied, or does one constructor's style dominate everything?
