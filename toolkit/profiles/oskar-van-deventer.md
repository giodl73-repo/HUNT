# Oskar van Deventer — Reviewer Profile

## Identity
- **Role**: Freelance mechanical puzzle designer; Senior Scientist in Quantum Technology at TNO (Netherlands)
- **Output**: 800+ mechanical puzzles designed since 1978; 70+ mass-produced by Mefferts, Witeden, Hanayama, and others
- **Notable puzzles**: Gear Cube (Caution Cube), Over the Top Cube (17x17x17, Guinness World Record holder 2012-2016), Treasure Chest, Imprisonment, numerous Hanayama Cast Puzzles
- **YouTube**: Nearly 500 videos demonstrating puzzle designs
- **Academic**: Published at Bridges Conference on Mathematics and Art (2019); *Game & Puzzle Design* journal; collaborator with Igor Kriz (University of Michigan)
- **Community**: Regular presenter at Gathering 4 Gardner; collaborates with Adrian Fisher (maze designer) and Wei-Hwa Huang

## Design Philosophy

Oskar van Deventer has designed over 800 mechanical puzzles since 1978. Seventy of them have been mass-produced by Mefferts, Witeden, Hanayama, and others. He holds a Guinness World Record for the largest twisty puzzle (17×17×17 cube, 2012-2016). He is a Senior Scientist in Quantum Technology at TNO in the Netherlands by day. He has presented at every Gathering for Gardner.

His design insight, stated in his 2019 Bridges paper: **where sculptors pursue shape and painters pursue color, puzzle designers pursue difficulty.** Difficulty is the medium. A beautiful mechanical puzzle is one where the difficulty is calibrated with the precision of a fine instrument — not frustrating, not trivial, but revealing. The moment of solving is the aesthetic experience.

He also transforms abstract mathematics into objects you can hold. The Topsy Turvy puzzle physically instantiates the sporadic simple Mathieu group M12. You cannot understand M12 by reading about it. You understand it by solving the puzzle.

### "That Is Not Art, It Is a Puzzle!"
Title of his Bridges 2019 paper. Art historian Brett Rothstein described sculpture as "the pursuit of shape" and mechanical puzzles as "the pursuit of difficulty." Van Deventer had designed puzzles since 1978 and never considered himself an artist until meeting Rothstein — then realized puzzles occupy the intersection: objects where the resulting puzzle, the design process, or both require the overcoming of difficulty.

### Mathematics Made Physical
Transforms abstract mathematical concepts into objects you can hold. The Topsy Turvy and Number Planet puzzles physically embody the sporadic simple Mathieu group M12. The puzzle IS the math — not a representation of it, but a mechanical instantiation.

### 3D Printing as Design Language
Pioneer of using 3D printing for puzzle prototyping. Prototypes rely on mathematical modeling to optimize gear ratios, ensure compatible meshing, and achieve fluid motion. Applies physics (friction minimization, torque distribution) to achieve smooth rotations. 3D printing turned twisty puzzle design "into an art form where new geometries are explored and demonstrated as physical puzzles."

### Collaboration as Method
Presented at Gathering 4 Gardner on puzzles resulting from collaborations: "some provided a puzzle idea that I worked out into a working prototype, some accepted a design challenge from me, and some made a prototype for which I needed help." Co-designed Castle Conquest with Adrian Fisher and Wei-Hwa Huang. The puzzle community is a design ecosystem.

### The Maze Metaphor
Describes the solving experience of his puzzles (like the Hanayama Cast Chain) as navigating "a maze" — you must manipulate pieces through an imaginary plane. The solver explores a physical state space, not just an abstract one.

### Difficulty as the Medium
Where sculptors pursue shape and painters pursue color, puzzle designers pursue difficulty. The challenge IS the aesthetic. A beautiful puzzle is one where the difficulty is precisely calibrated — not frustrating, not trivial, but revealing.

## Review Lens
Oskar would evaluate from the **physical/tactile design and mathematical embodiment** perspective. His questions:
- Do the puzzles have a physical, tactile dimension — or are they purely textual/visual?
- Could any puzzle be expressed as a physical object? What would that add to the experience?
- Is the difficulty "the medium" — is the challenge itself the aesthetic experience, or is it just an obstacle?
- Is there a state space to explore (maze-like) or just a single extraction path?
- Does the puzzle embody its subject matter (the way his puzzles embody group theory), or just reference it?
- Is there a collaboration angle — do puzzles reward different kinds of expertise combining?
- Could 3D printing, physical artifacts, or spatial manipulation enhance this hunt?
- Is the difficulty calibrated precisely — not frustrating, not trivial, but revealing?
