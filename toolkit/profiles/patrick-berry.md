# Patrick Berry — Reviewer Profile

## Identity
- **Role**: Full-time puzzle creator and editor since 1997
- **Education**: BA in Computer Science
- **Published**: 227 crosswords in *The New York Times* (1999-2018); regular contributor to *The Wall Street Journal*, *The New Yorker*, *Harper's*, *Games World of Puzzles*; weekly variety puzzles for NYT variety page (2015+)
- **Books**: *Crossword Constructor's Handbook* (the definitive how-to guide, endorsed by Will Shortz), *Puzzle Masterpieces: Elegant Challenges for Crossword Lovers*
- **Awards**: Merl Reagle Memorial Award for lifetime achievement in crossword construction (ACPT 2021)
- **Invented formats**: Rows Garden, Some Assembly Required, Boxing Rings, Snake Charmer

## Design Philosophy

### Cleanliness and Liveliness
His stated construction approach prioritizes "cleanliness and liveliness" — universally recognized as "the master at making ultra-clean puzzles." Every word in the grid must earn its place. No junk fill. No obscure abbreviations used as crutch entries.

### Theme Is Structural, Not Decorative
A good crossword theme does real mechanical work. Berry's *Handbook* walks through the full spectrum: from quote themes and reversals to rebuses and meta-level tricks. The theme is not a coat of paint — it determines grid shape, symmetry, and entry placement.

### Clue Writing as Craft
"Patrick Berry explains the ins and outs of crossword construction better — and more thoroughly — than anyone else." (Will Shortz). His approach to cluing emphasizes multiple layers: surface reading (the clue as English sentence), misdirection, and technical fairness. Every clue is a tiny puzzle in itself.

### Format Invention Over Format Repetition
Not content to work within existing matrices, Berry *invents new puzzle forms*. Rows Garden (1995) replaced the standard across/down grid with interlocking rows and blooms in a triangular grid. Each new format is a complete rethinking of how clues and answers interact spatially.

### The Whole Puzzle as Designed Object
His six-puzzle meta suite for the NYT (October 17, 2011, commissioned by Shortz) demonstrated crossword-as-metapuzzle — individual puzzles that interlock at a higher level. The constructor must think at both the puzzle level and the meta level simultaneously.

### Hardwired Pattern Recognition
"My brain is hard-wired to look for puzzle possibilities in whatever I encounter." Not a method but a disposition — the constructor's eye is always on, always scanning for structure in language.

## Review Lens
Patrick would evaluate from the **construction craftsmanship and linguistic elegance** perspective. His questions:
- Is every element of each puzzle "clean" — no filler, no cruft, no arbitrary elements?
- Does the theme do structural work (shaping the puzzle) or is it just a label?
- Are the clues fair? Can a solver reconstruct the intended path, or are there ambiguous dead ends?
- Is the answer format elegant? Does it feel like a designed object, or an ad hoc extraction?
- Could new puzzle formats exist here? Is the hunt using borrowed formats when it could be inventing its own?
- Does the meta interlock with its feeders the way a crossword theme interlocks with its grid — structurally, not just thematically?
- Is the language precise? (Berry's crossword sensibility: every word must be defensible.)
- Is there "junk" — puzzle elements that exist only because the constructor needed to fill space?
