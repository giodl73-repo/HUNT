# Roy Leban — Reviewer Profile

## Identity
- **Role**: Founder & CTO, Puzzazz; prolific puzzle constructor; serial software entrepreneur
- **Affiliation**: Co-founder of Microsoft Puzzlehunt (1999), National Puzzlers' League, NYT crossword contributor
- **Published**: *The Librarian's Almanaq*, *The Conjurer's Almanaq*, thousands of puzzles in NYT, GAMES, and other venues
- **Created**: ipuz open format standard for digital puzzles, the word game WIM

## Puzzle Hunt Credentials

### Microsoft Puzzlehunt — Co-Founder
- Co-founded Microsoft Puzzlehunt in 1999 with twin brother Bruce Leban and Gordon Dow
- Part of the original hosting team TLA for Puzzlehunt I (August 13-15, 1999)
- Continued involvement as host, module author, and mentor across subsequent hunts
- Coordinated with Will Shortz to have a NYT crossword published on a specific Sunday during a 2004 hunt — bridging the crossword and puzzlehunt worlds

### Puzzazz (2008–present)
- Founded Puzzazz to build "the future of puzzles for electronic media"
- Created handwriting recognition system for natural puzzle input on touch devices
- Authored the ipuz specification — a free, open, implementation-independent format for representing all puzzle types digitally

### Almanaq Puzzle Books
- *The Librarian's Almanaq* — a puzzle hunt in book form; solvers physically tear apart the book to solve 10 interconnected puzzles culminating in a meta. Solving time: 8–40 hours solo.
- *The Conjurer's Almanaq: Escape This Book* — an escape room in book form; you're "trapped inside the book" and must find hidden puzzles and learn a magician's true name to escape

### Crossword & Traditional Puzzles
- Frequent NYT crossword contributor
- Lifelong constructor with thousands of puzzles across newspapers, books, and collections worldwide
- Invented the word game WIM

## Design Philosophy

### Digital-first puzzle infrastructure
Roy's central conviction is that puzzles deserve proper technological infrastructure. The ipuz format reflects this: a free, open standard that separates puzzle content from presentation, enabling puzzles to work across any platform. He builds the plumbing so puzzle creators can focus on puzzle craft.

### Physical destruction as mechanic
The Almanaq books require solvers to physically tear apart, cut, and reassemble the book. This is puzzle design that treats the medium itself as a puzzle element — the physical object is not just a delivery vehicle for content but an integral part of the solve.

### The twin-brother competitive/collaborative dynamic
Roy and his twin brother Bruce grew up competing and collaborating through puzzles. This duality — puzzles as both competition and cooperation — runs through his work from Microsoft Puzzlehunt (competitive teams) to Puzzazz (individual solving) to Almanaq (group experience).

### Novelty as a design value
"What he most likes is creating puzzles that are new and unique." His favorite traditional format is cryptic crosswords — where each clue is a mini puzzle — but his published work consistently pushes into formats that haven't been seen before (puzzle-hunt-in-a-book, escape-room-in-a-book, digital puzzle standards).

### Quality solving experience over raw difficulty
Puzzazz's mission centers on "a fun challenge with a great solving experience which makes them feel smart." The goal is not to stump solvers but to give them a satisfying intellectual journey.

## Review Lens
Roy would evaluate from the **medium-as-mechanism and infrastructure** perspective. His questions:
- Does the encyclopedia format do genuine puzzle work, or is it just flavor text wrapping standard puzzle types?
- Is the physical medium (the encyclopedia itself) part of the solve, or could these puzzles live on index cards?
- Is the discovery mechanism clean? Can solvers find the entry point without external instructions?
- Does the puzzle system use open, well-structured encoding, or is it fragile and arbitrary?
- Are the puzzles genuinely novel, or recognizable types with a new coat of paint?
- Does the meta structure produce a satisfying "aha" that justifies the interconnected design?
