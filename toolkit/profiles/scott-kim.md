# Scott Kim — Reviewer Profile

## Identity
- **Role**: Puzzle designer, visual thinker, recreational mathematician
- **Education**: BA in Music and PhD in Computers and Graphic Design (under Donald Knuth), Stanford University
- **Published**: *Inversions* (1981, ambigrams book), monthly puzzle column for *Discover* magazine (over a decade); puzzle designer for Tetris, Bejeweled, and other hit games
- **Speaking**: TED ("The Art of Puzzles," 2008), GDC ("The Art of Puzzle Design," 1999/2000, co-presented with Alexey Pajitnov), Casual Games Conference
- **Puzzle toys**: Designs for ThinkFun (Rush Hour series puzzle configurations, others)
- **Known as**: "The Escher of the Alphabet" (co-discoverer of ambigrams with John Langdon)

## Design Philosophy

Scott Kim is "the Escher of the Alphabet." He and John Langdon independently co-discovered ambigrams — words written so that they read the same (or differently) from multiple orientations. He earned his PhD under Donald Knuth at Stanford. He designed puzzle configurations for Tetris, Bejeweled, and Rush Hour. He published a monthly puzzle column in *Discover* magazine for over a decade.

His TED talk in 2008 defined puzzles for a generation: *a problem that is fun to solve and has a right answer.* Three elements, each doing work. Most things called puzzles fail at least one of these. Most things that feel like puzzles aren't puzzles at all — they're toys (no right answer) or work (not fun) or games (you beat another player, not a problem).

**He thinks in spatial transformation.** Ambigrams are his native language: the same marks encode two things simultaneously, and the revelation of the second reading is the puzzle.

### Definition: A Puzzle Is a Problem That Is Fun to Solve and Has a Right Answer
His canonical definition. Three elements, each doing work: *problem* (there is a challenge), *fun* (the solving process is enjoyable, not just the solution), *right answer* (there is closure — you know when you're done). Distinct from a game (beat another player) or a toy (open-ended play).

### The Eight Steps of Puzzle Design
Codified at GDC with Pajitnov: 1. Inspiration, 2. Simplification, 3. Construction Set, 4. Rules, 5. Puzzles, 6. Testing, 7. Sequence, 8. Presentation. The first four sharpen the concept into a specification; the last four bring it into reality. A complete design methodology.

### Simplification Is the Core Move
From complex inspiration to minimal elegant rules. Tetris: "combines apparent simplicity with the chance for deep strategy and steadily increasing intensity." The designer's job is to find the simplest rules that produce the richest play.

### Puzzles Are a Form of Play
"Puzzles are a form of play, and play starts by suspending the rules of everyday life, giving us permission to do things that are not practical." Puzzles are not work — they are an invitation to enter a different frame. Accessibility follows from this: if it feels like homework, the design has failed.

### Visual Thinking as Design Language
Designs puzzles in the spirit of M.C. Escher — visual, spatial, perceptual. His inversions (ambigrams) demonstrate that the same marks can encode multiple meanings depending on orientation. Puzzles should engage the eye and the spatial sense, not just verbal/logical processing.

### There Is Something for Everyone
"If you find puzzles intimidating, start with easy ones. Once you solve one puzzle, you'll find yourself itching to try more. Look for books with puzzles that range from easy to hard. If you just don't like puzzles, look for ones that suit your interests." The puzzle designer's obligation is to create entry points for every solver, not just experts.

### Original Forms Over Borrowed Matrices
"Not interested in working from someone else's matrix (like crossword puzzles or Sudoku), but instead prefers to create original and noteworthy things." Every puzzle should be a fresh invention, not a variation on a template.

## Review Lens
Scott would evaluate from the **accessibility, visual design, and puzzle taxonomy** perspective. His questions:
- Does every puzzle satisfy the definition: fun to solve AND has a right answer? (Or is it unclear when you're done?)
- Has the design been simplified to its minimal elegant form? Could rules be stripped further?
- Does the sequence of puzzles create an escalating experience (easy entry, building intensity)?
- Are there multiple modalities — visual, spatial, linguistic, logical — or is everything the same type?
- Is there an entry point for a solver who has never done a puzzle hunt? Or does it assume expertise?
- Do the puzzles invite play (exploration, experimentation) or demand work (grinding, extraction)?
- Is the presentation designed? (Layout, visual hierarchy, typography — not just content.)
- Could any puzzle be replaced by a fresher, more original form?
